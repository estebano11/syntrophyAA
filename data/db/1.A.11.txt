<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><strong>1.A.11 The Ammonium Channel  Transporter (Amt) Family</strong></p>
<p>The proteins of the Amt family vary in size from 391 to 622 amino acyl residues and possess 11 (N-terminus out; most members) or 12 (N-terminus in) transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs).  The <em>E. coli</em> AmtB is a trimer (<span id='ref45.12023896' class='cite'>Blakey <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  It appears to have dual functions, transporting NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+ </sup>and regulating nitrogen metabolism by directly interacting with regulatory proteins such as the PII protein and its homologue, GlnK (<span id='ref2775.12753193' class='cite'>Blauwkamp and Ninfa, 2003</span>).  <em>amtB</em> and <em>glnK</em> form an operon, and GlnK regulates the activity of AmtB.  Homologues are probably ubiquitous.  In <em>Azospirillum brasilense</em>, AmtB forms a ternary complex between AmtB, GlnZ and the nitrogenase regulatory enzyme DraG (<span id='ref4479.18028310' class='cite'>Huergo <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Eukaryotic AmtB homologues, in general, are larger than the prokaryotic proteins.  Most functionally characterized members of the family are ammonium  uptake transporters (<span id='ref3937.12032358' class='cite'>Soupene et al., 2002d</span>).  Some, but not other Amt proteins also transport methylammonium (<span id='ref5510.17710640' class='cite'>Andrade and Einsle, 2007</span>; <span id='ref6512.19273840' class='cite'>Musa-Aziz <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).&nbsp; Detailed phylogenetic analyses of plant homologues have been published (<span id='ref11148.24438197' class='cite'>von Wittgenstein <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>). Rhesus (Rh) proteins allow the rapid transport of CO<sub>2</sub> across membranes (<span id='ref34552886.33633837' class='cite'>Michenkova <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>The structures of the <em>E. coli</em> AmtB (1.35 &Aring;)  (<span id='ref43.15361618' class='cite'>Khademi <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>) and the <em>Archaeoglobus fulgidus</em> Amt-1 (<span id='ref2776.16214888' class='cite'>Andrade <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>) have been determined.  They were considered to be  gas channels with two structurally similar halves that span the membrane with opposite polarity.  There is a vestibule that recruits NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, a binding site for NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> or CH<sub>3</sub>-NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> and a 20 &Aring; long hydrophobic channel that lowers the NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> pKa to below 6 using weak interactions with C-H hydrogen bond donors such as those provided by conserved histidines.  Reconstitution of AmtB into vesicles led to the conclusion that it conducts uncharged NH<sub>3</sub>, releasing H<sup>+</sup> on the outside (<span id='ref4604.' class='cite'>Soupene <em>et al</em>., 2002a</span>,b,<span id='ref4606.' class='cite'>c</span>).  However, a more recent study (<span id='ref4603.17998534' class='cite'>Fong <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>) concludes that NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> in the transported species. Hall and Kustu (<span id='ref7856.21775672' class='cite'>2011</span>) showed that NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> transport does not require the two histidyl residues, H<sub>168 </sub>and H<sub>318</sub>,<sub>&nbsp;</sub>which can be replaced by acidic residues with retention of activity. Ortiz-Ramirez et al. (<span id='ref7857.21757699' class='cite'>2011</span>) have concluded that the bean Amt1 protein is an H<sup>+</sup>/NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> symporter.&nbsp;  Analysis of the AMT gene family in chili 
pepper and the effects of arbuscular mycorrhizal colonization on the 
expression patterns of CaAMT2 geneshave been reported (<span id='ref34556196.36991328' class='cite'>Fang <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>The <em>E. coli</em> ammonium channel, AmtB, and the P<sub>II</sub> signal transduction protein, GlnK, constitute an ammonium sensory system that effectively couples the intracellular nitrogen regulation system to external changes in ammonium availability. Direct binding of GlnK to AmtB inactivates the channel, thereby controlling ammonium influx in response to the intracellular nitrogen status. The stoichiometry of the complex is 1:1 for AmtB:GlnK (<span id='ref2349.16864585' class='cite'>Durand and Merrick, 2006</span>). Only the fully deuridylylated form of GlnK co-purifies with AmtB. Interaction of GlnK with AmtB is dependent on ATP and is sensitive to 2-oxoglutarate. Thus <em>in vivo</em> association and dissociation of the complex might not only be dependent on the uridylylation status of GlnK but also on the intracellular pools of ATP and 2-oxoglutarate (<span id='ref2349.16864585' class='cite'>Durand and Merrick, 2006</span>).</p>
<p>The 11 TMSs (M1-M11) of AmtB form a right handed helical bundle around each channel.  Residues from helices M1, M6, M7, M8 and M9 of one monomer interact with residues from helices M1, M2 and M3 of the neighboring subunit to form an interacting surface area of 2716 &Aring;<sup>2</sup>.  Polar aromatic residues (Y and W) comprise part of the membrane-aqueous phase interface. AMTs may use a conserved allosteric control mechanism to regulate ammonium flux, potentially using a gating mechanism that limits flux to protect against ammonium toxicity (<span id='ref6709.19581303' class='cite'>Loque <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The effects of ammonium nitrogen concentration on <em>Pseudomonas stutzeri</em> F2 nitorgen assimilation and the analysis of AmtB function have been reported (<span id='ref34556458.37033292' class='cite'>Fu <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>Amt proteins are homotrimers, in which each subunit contains a narrow pore through which substrate transport occurs.  Two conserved histidyl residues in the pore of the<em> E. coli</em> AmtB are absolutely necessary for ammonia conductance.  Crystal structures of variants confirmed that substitution of the histidine residues does not affect AmtB structure. In a subgroup of Amt proteins found only in fungi, one of the histidines is replaced by glutamate.  The equivalent substitution in <em>E. coli</em> AmtB is partially active, and the structure of this variant suggests that the glutamate side chain can make similar interactions to those made by histidine (<span id='ref5631.17040913' class='cite'>Javelle <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).&nbsp; As expected for a channel, NH<sub>3</sub> uniport appears to occur by energy-independent, non-concentrative, bidirectional diffusion (<span id='ref4604.' class='cite'>Soupene <em>et al</em>., 2002a</span>; <span id='ref47.17293878' class='cite'>Loque <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>), but NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> may be the true substrate (<span id='ref4603.17998534' class='cite'>Fong <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).</p>
<p>Amt proteins facilitate ammonium ion transport across the 
membranes of plants, fungi, and bacteria.&nbsp; On the basis of the structural data for <em>E. coli </em> AmtB, Wang et al.<span id='ref9229.22631217' class='cite'> (2012</span>) deduced the mechanism by which electrogenic transport occurs. Free 
energy calculations show that NH<sub>4<sup>+</sup></sub> is stable in the AmtB pore, 
reaching a binding site from which it can spontaneously transfer a 
proton to a pore-lining histidine residue (His168). The substrate 
may diffuse down the pore in the form of NH<sub>3</sub>, while the proton is 
cotransported through a highly conserved hydrogen-bonded His168-His318 
pair. This constitutes a novel permeation mechanism that confers to the 
histidine dyad an essential mechanistic role that is equivalent to symport (<span id='ref9229.22631217' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; Thus these systems blur the boundary between channels and secondary carriers.</p>
<p>Plant AMTs have been reported to mediate electrogenic transport as noted above.
Functional analysis of AMT2 from <em>Arabidopsis</em> (TC #1.A.11.2.2) expressed in yeast and oocytes
suggests that NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> is the recruited substrate, but the uncharged
form (NH<sub>3</sub>) is conducted (<span id='ref6708.19635480' class='cite'>Neuh&auml;user <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). AMT2 partially co-localizes with
electrogenic AMTs and conducts methylamine with low affinity. This
transport mechanism may apply to other plant ammonium transporters and
explains the different capacities of AMTs to accumulate ammonium in the
plant cell.</p>
<p>A P(II) signal transduction protein, GlnK, is a regulator of transmembrane ammonia conductance by&nbsp; AmtB in <em>Escherichia coli</em>. The complex formed between AmtB and inhibitory GlnK at 1.96-A resolution shows that the trimeric channel is blocked directly by GlnK. In response to intracellular nitrogen status, the ability of GlnK to block the channel is regulated by uridylylation/deuridylylation at Y51. ATP and Mg<sup>2+</sup> augment the interaction of GlnK. The hydrolyzed product, adenosine 5'-diphosphate, orients the surface of GlnK for AmtB blockade. 2-Oxoglutarate diminishes AmtB/GlnK association (<span id='ref4469.17190799' class='cite'>Gruswitz <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). MepB, in contrast to MepA and MepC, similarly appears to be the primary NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> transporter and serves as a regulator for nitrogen sensing in <em>Fusarium fujikuroi</em> (<span id='ref4601.18083831' class='cite'>Teichert et al., 2007</span>).</p>
<p>Many organisms from all major kingdoms of living organisms possess multiple homologues.  <em>Rhodobacter capsulatus</em> has two Amt family homologues, AmtB and AmtY.  The former, but not the latter, has been reported to be an NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> sensor as well as a NH<sub>3</sub> transporter (<span id='ref3942.12107124' class='cite'>Yakunin and Hallenbeck, 2002</span>).  Mep2 of <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em> has been shown to function both as a transporter and as a sensor, generating a signal that regulates filamentous growth (pseudohyphal differentiation) in response to ammonium starvation (<span id='ref48.9482721' class='cite'>Lorenz and Heitman, 1998</span>).  This protein has an N-terminal, asparaginyl-linked glycosylated domain where only Asn-4 is glycosylated.  Mep2, but not Mep1 or Mep3, has an extracytoplasmic N-terminus (<span id='ref1876.11069679' class='cite'>Marini and Andr&eacute;, 2000</span>).  This N-terminal domain is not required for either transport or sensing.  Of the three <em>S. cerevisiae</em> Amt family paralogues, Mep2 exhibits higher affinity for NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> (1 &mu;M) than Mep1 (10 &mu;M), and Mep1 exhibits higher affinity than Mep3 (1 mM).</p>
<p>The Amt family includes the Rhesus (Rh) family of   proteins, both erythroid (RhAG, RhD and RhCE) and non-erythroid (RhCG, RhBG and RhGK).  In the mammalian kidney collecting duct, RhBG is in the basolateral membrane while RhCG is in the apical membrane.  Basolateral anchoring of RhBG requires ankyrin-G (<span id='ref39.15611082' class='cite'>Lopez <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>). It has been proposed that some of these proteins are CO<sub>2</sub> channels, but this suggestion has not been substantiated (<span id='ref3937.12032358' class='cite'>Soupene et al., 2002d</span>).</p>
<p>Loque et al. (<span id='ref47.17293878' class='cite'>2007</span>) have shown that the soluble, cytosolic C-terminus of Amt1.1 of <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> is an allosteric regulator.  This domain is conserved between bacteria, fungi and plants.  Mutations in this domain lead to loss of transport activity.  The crystal structure of an Amt family member from <em>Archaeoglobus fulgidus</em> suggests that the C-terminal domain interacts physically with the cytosolic loops of the neighboring subunit.  Phosphorylation of conserved sites in the C-terminal domain with conformational coupling between monomers may allow tight regulation of transport and sensing (<span id='ref47.17293878' class='cite'>Loque <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).</p>
<p>In <em>Cryptococcus neoformans</em>, Amt1 and Amt2 are low and high affinity ammonium permeases, respectively.  AMT2 is transcriptionally induced in response to nitrogen limitation whereas AMT1 is constitutively expressed.  Amt2 is required for the initiation of invasive growth of haploid cells under low nitrogen conditions and for the mating of wild type cells under the same conditions.  It was proposed that Amt2 may be a new fungal ammonium sensor and an element of the signaling cascades that govern the mating of <em>C. neoformans</em> in response to environmental nutritional cues (<span id='ref4602.18055915' class='cite'>Rutherford et al., 2007</span>).</p>
<p>The C-terminal cytoplasmic domains allosterically activate adjacent channels in the trimeric structures of Amt channels. Mutations in helix 1 yield up to 100-fold lower affinity with 10-fold increased flux (<span id='ref47.17293878' class='cite'>Loqu&eacute; <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>; <span id='ref6709.19581303' class='cite'>Loqu&eacute; <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>) in <em>A. fulgidus</em> (TC# 1.A.11.2.7). The <em>A. thaliana</em> protein, Amt1;1 (TC# 1.A.11.2.1), is phosphorylated on Thr460 giving rise to inhibition in response to high [NH<sub>3</sub>] (<span id='ref7017.18269572' class='cite'>Lalonde <em>et al.</em>, 2008</span>; <span id='ref7018.19948793' class='cite'>Lanquar <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>; <span id='ref7019.17172286' class='cite'>Yuan <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>).<br /><br />There is presently no direct evidence to support the idea that Rh proteins transport CO<sub>2</sub>/H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>. However, physiological studies in the green alga <em>Chlamydomonas reinhardtii</em> do suggest its Rh1 protein is involved in CO<sub>2</sub> metabolism (<span id='ref7162.15096599' class='cite'>Soupene <em>et al.</em>, 2004</span>). In addition, structural work on the Rh protein of <em>Nitrosomonas europaea</em> identified a CO<sub>2</sub> binding site on the substrate conduction pathway (<span id='ref2776.16214888' class='cite'>Andrade <em>et al.</em>, 2005a</span>). By analogy to the Amt proteins (transport of hydrated ammonia), some Rh proteins may transport H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, but this is hypothetical.<br /><br />Amt proteins appear to function as channel/carrier hybrids. Consistent with these proteins functioning as channels, structural studies indicate that there are no large overall conformational differences between substrate-free and substrate-complexed proteins. <span id='ref2776.16214888' class='cite'>Andrade <em>et al.</em>, 2005b</span> did find evidence that TMS5 of <em>Archaeoglobus fulgidus</em> Amt-1 could move in a way that could be functionally significant. Genetic work (<span id='ref7163.19884311' class='cite'>Inwood <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>) suggests that such movement - an oscillation of TMS5 - may control the opening of both the entrance and exit to the AmtB conduction pore.&nbsp; Furthermore, work conducted by (<span id='ref4603.17998534' class='cite'>Fong <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>) indicated that AmtB concentrates methylammonium. The movement of TMS5 during substrate transport and the ability to concentrate substrate are characteristics of a carrier-type transporter. Whether NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> dissociates as it goes through the channel with NH<sub>3</sub> going through the pore and H<sup>+</sup> taking another route is a separate question. Because the conduction pore is hydrophobic, a mechanism based on NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> dissociation to NH<sub>3</sub> and H<sup>+</sup> appears possible.<br /><br /> The transport of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> is an active process as has been suggested experimentally by several groups. By working with an AmtB mutant in a <em>&Delta;glnA</em> background, methylammonium was concentrated roughly 100-fold (<span id='ref4603.17998534' class='cite'>Fong <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>). Accumulation was prevented by CCCP and, thus, was dependent upon the proton motive force. This experiment suggested that Amts are carriers rather than channels as suggested by several other studies.&nbsp; There are 16 soybean Amts that fall into two groups, 1 (10 genes) and 2 (6 genes).&nbsp; GmAMTs may differentially and/or redundantly regulate ammonium transport during plant development and in response to environmental factors, including nodulation genes involved in Rhzobial symbiotic N<sub>2</sub> fixation (<span id='ref34556003.36835403' class='cite'>Yang <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>The X-ray structures have revealed that the pore of the Amt and Rh 
proteins is characterized by a hydrophobic portion about 12A long in 
which electronic density was observed in the crystallographic study of AmtB 
from <em>Escherichia coli</em>. This electronic density was initially only 
observed when crystals were grown in the presence of ammonium and was 
thus attributed to ammonia molecules. The Amt/Rh protein mechanism might involve 
the single-file diffusion of NH<sub>3</sub> molecules. However,
 the pore could also be filled with water molecules. The 
possible presence of water molecules in the pore lumen calls for a 
reassessment of the notion that Amt/Rh proteins work as plain
 NH<sub>3</sub> channels. Indeed, functional experiments on plant ammonium 
transporters and rhesus proteins suggest a variety of permeation 
mechanisms including the passive diffusion of NH<sub>3</sub>, the antiport of 
NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup>, the transport of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, or the cotransport of 
NH<sub>3</sub>/H<sup>+</sup>.&nbsp;<span id='ref7353.20674437' class='cite'>Lamoureux <em>et al.</em> (2010)</span> discuss these mechanisms in light of 
functional and simulation studies on the AmtB transporter.</p>
<p><strong>Note: The AMT family was previously given the TC# 2.A.49.</strong></p>
