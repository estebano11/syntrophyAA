<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong>1.A.112. The Cyclin M Mg<sup>2+</sup> Exporter (CNNM) Family</strong></p>
<p>The hemolysin C of <em>Brachyspira hyodysenteriae</em> (268 aas; ter <a class='reflink' href='#ref4251'>Huurne <em>et al</em>., 1994</a>) and the Co<sup>2+</sup>-resistance protein, CorC of <em>Salmonella typhimurium</em> (273 aas with one putative TMS (residues 163-181)) are homologous 
throughout most of their lengths to each other. They are also similar to the C-terminal 
hydrophilic portions of 5 close paralogues in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>, all of which are about 440 aas long, have an N-terminal 4 TMS domain, and may be Mg<sup>2+</sup> exporters (<span id='ref34549272.30593530' class='cite'>Ulens 2018</span>).  One representative <em>B. subtilis</em> paralogue is YrkA (434 aas; spP54428).  The CorC protein was believed to function as an auxiliary protein to the CorA Co<sup>2+</sup>/Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;channel (TC #1.A.35) of <em>S. typhimurium</em> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4249'>Gibson <em>et al</em>., 1991</a>). The bacterial CorB/CorC/CNNM sub-family of proteins is involved in resistance to antibiotic exposure and the survival of pathogenic microorganisms in their host environment. CorC proteins possess a cytoplasmic region containing a (regulatory?) ATP-binding site (<span id='ref34553131.33912817' class='cite'>Huang <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>Mouse ACDP2 (CNNM2) was cloned from mouse distal convoluted tubule cells of the kidney, expressed in <em>Xenopus laevis</em> oocytes, and studied with two-electrode voltage-clamp techniques. When expressed in oocytes, ACDP2 was reported to mediate saturable Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;uptake with a Michaelis constant of 0.56 mM. Transport of Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;by ACDP2 was rheogenic and voltage-dependent, but was not coupled to Na<sup>+ </sup> or Cl<sup>-</sup> ions. ACDP2 transports a range of divalent cations: Mg<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Sr<sup>2+</sup>, Ba<sup>2+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, and Fe<sup>2+</sup>, thus exhibiting wide substrate selectivity. The cations Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Cd<sup>2+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup>, and Ni<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;did not induce currents, and only Zn<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;effectively
 inhibited transport. The ACDP2 transcript is abundantly present in 
kidney, brain, and heart with lower amounts in liver, small intestine, 
and colon. Moreover, ACDP2 mRNA is upregulated with magnesium 
deficiency, particularly in the distal convoluted tubule cells, kidney, 
heart, and brain. Thus, ACDP2 may be a regulated transporter for Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;and other divalent cations in epithelial cells (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4250'>Goytain <em>et al</em>., 2005</a>).</p>
<p>Mutations in the cyclin M2 (CNNM2) gene were 
identified to be causative for severe hypomagnesemia. In kidney, CNNM2 
is a basolaterally expressed protein with predominant expression in the 
distal convoluted tubule. Transcellular magnesium (Mg<sup>2+</sup>) reabsorption in the distal convoluted tubule represents the final step before Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;is
 excreted into the urine, thus fine-tuning its final excretion via a 
tightly regulated mechanism. Membrane topology studies showed that CNNM2
 has an extracellular N terminus and an intracellular C terminus (<a class='reflink' href='#ref8873'>de Baaij <em>et al.</em>, 2012</a>).
 The loss-of-function mutation found in patients disturbs ATP binding by
 the intracellular cystathionine &beta;-synthase domains. In the endoplasmic 
reticulum, the signal peptidase complex cleaves off a large N-terminal 
signal peptide of about 64 amino acids. Mutagenesis screening showed 
that CNNM2 is glycosylated at residue Asn-112, stabilizing CNNM2 on the 
plasma membrane. CNNM2a forms heterodimers with the 
smaller isoform CNNM2b.</p>
<p>The bacterial proteins, YrkA and YhdP, have 
three recognized domains: the 4-TMS DUF21 domain (residues 1-170), a 
nucleotide binding CBS domain (residues 225-335) and a CorC/HlyC hydrophilic domain 
(residues 360-430).&nbsp; The mammalian homologues have at least the first 
two of these domains which are preceded by an N-terminal TMS and an 
unidentified hydrophilic domain. The bacterial HlyC and CorC proteins 
(1.C.126) lack the 4 TMS DUF21 domain, but have the 
CBS and CorC/HlyC domains. Only the proteins with the DUF21 domain 
are likely to be transporters.&nbsp; All of&nbsp; the evidence is consistent with 
the conclusion that these homologues form divalent-cation-specific 
porters or channels.</p>
<p>There is controversy as to (1) whether the CNNM (cyclin) proteins are
 transporters or regulators, and (2) if they are transporters, whether 
they are channels or carriers.&nbsp; The CBS domains bind ATP (<span id='ref34548902.24706765' class='cite'>Hirata <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>). In humans, the <span class='highlight'>CNNM</span> family is encoded by four genes: CNNM1-4. CNNM1 is thought to act as a cytosolic 
copper chaperone, whereas CNNM2 and CNNM4 have been associated with 
magnesium handling. Mutations in the CNNM2 gene cause 
familial dominant hypomagnesaemia (MIM:607803), a rare human disorder 
characterized by renal and intestinal magnesium (Mg<sup>2+</sup>) wasting, which 
may lead to symptoms of Mg<sup>2+</sup> depletion such as tetany, seizures and 
cardiac arrhythmias (<span id='ref34548903.23027747' class='cite'>G&oacute;mez-Garc&iacute;a <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). In marine fish, there are 6 CNNM paralogues, each with a different location and function, probably catayzing Mg<sup>2+</sup> efflux (<span id='ref34548904.24965791' class='cite'>Islam <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).
 Three conserved dileucine motifs in CNNM4 are necessary for both 
basolateral sorting and interaction with the &mu;1As (AP1A) and &mu;1B (AP1B) 
proteins (<span id='ref34548905.25449265' class='cite'>Hirata <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).&nbsp;<span id='ref34548906.27564576' class='cite'>Ishii <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> also concluded that CNNM proteins are efflux permeases.&nbsp;</p>
<p>PRL phosphatases and <span class='highlight'>CNNM</span> proteins form complexes, regulated by the formation of phosphocysteine.&nbsp;<span id='ref34548907.27856537' class='cite'>Gulerez <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> showed that cysteine in the PRL phosphatase catalytic site is endogenously 
phosphorylated as part of the catalytic cycle and that phosphocysteine 
levels change in response to Mg<sup>2+</sup> levels. Phosphorylation blocks PRL binding to the <span class='highlight'>CNNM</span> Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters, and mutations that block the PRL-<span class='highlight'>CNNM</span> interaction prevent regulation of Mg<sup>2+</sup> efflux in cultured cells. The crystal structure of the complex of PRL2 and the CBS-pair domain of the Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporter CNNM3 revealed the molecular basis for the interaction (<span id='ref34548907.27856537' class='cite'>Gulerez <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>). The structural basis underlying the interaction between PRL phosphatases and <span class='highlight'>CNNM</span> transporters has been further studied (<span id='ref34548908.27899452' class='cite'>Gim&eacute;nez-Mascarell <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). Renal function of CNNM2 is necessary for maintenance of blood pressure (<span id='ref34548909.28033128' class='cite'>Funato <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>), and <span id='ref34548910.29383719' class='cite'>Funato <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>. have concluded that CNNM porters are Na<sup>+</sup>:Mg<sup>2+ </sup>antiporters. However&nbsp;<span id='ref34548911.29383729' class='cite'>Arjona and de Baaij 2018</span> suggested that they function as regulators of Mg<sup>2+</sup> transport, although <span id='ref34548912.29383723' class='cite'>Funato <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> rebutted this suggestion. <span id='ref34548901.30341174' class='cite'>Chen <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> concluded that the CBS domains in CNNM proteins mediate dimerization and are important for Mg<sup>2+</sup> transport activity. Most of the evnidence therefore suggests that CNNM proteins are Mg<sup>2+</sup> exporters (<span id='ref34549272.30593530' class='cite'>Ulens 2018</span>).</p>
<p>The cyclin M family (CNNMs; also called ancient conserved domain proteins, 
or ACDPs) may be Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters (<span id='ref34549198.30341174' class='cite'>Chen <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). CNNMs are 
associated with a number of genetic diseases affecting ion movement and 
cancer via their association with highly oncogenic phosphatases of 
regenerating liver (PRLs). Structurally, CNNMs contain an N-terminal 
extracellular domain, a transmembrane domain (DUF21), and a large 
cytosolic region containing a cystathionine-&beta;-synthase (CBS) domain and a
 putative cyclic nucleotide-binding homology (CNBH) domain. <span id='ref34549198.30341174' class='cite'>Chen <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> determined the crystal structures of the
 CNBH domains of CNNM2 and CNNM3 at 2.6 and 1.9 &Aring; resolutions. These domains did not bind cyclic nucleotides, but 
mediated dimerization both in crystals and in solution. An inverse correlation between 
the propensity of the CNBH domains to dimerize and the ability of CNNMs 
to mediate Mg<sup>2+</sup> efflux was noted. CNBH domains from active family 
members were observed as both dimers and monomers, whereas the inactive 
member, CNNM3, was observed only as a dimer. Mutational analysis 
revealed that the CNBH domain was required for Mg<sup>2+</sup> efflux activity of CNNM4.</p>
<p>CNNM/CorB proteins are a broadly conserved and are associated with Mg<sup>2+</sup> transport but it is not known if they mediate transport themselves or regulate other transporters.&nbsp;<span id='ref34553723.34188059' class='cite'>Chen <em>et al.</em> 2021</span> determined the crystal structure of an archaeal CorB protein in two conformations (apo and Mg<sup>2+</sup>-ATP bound). The transmembrane DUF21 domain exists in an inward-facing conformation with a Mg<sup>2+</sup> ion coordinated by a conserved pi-helix. In the absence of Mg<sup>2+</sup>-ATP, the CBS-pair domain adopts an elongated dimeric configuration with previously unobserved domain-domain contacts. A role of the structural rearrangements in mediating Mg<sup>2+</sup>-ATP sensing was suggested. An in vitro, liposome-based assay was used to demonstrate direct Mg<sup>2+</sup> transport by CorB proteins (<span id='ref34553723.34188059' class='cite'>Chen <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>In prokaryotes, cellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> homeostasis is orchestrated via the CorA, MgtA/B, MgtE, and CorB/C Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters (<span id='ref34555038.35819535' class='cite'>Franken <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). For CorA, MgtE, and CorB/C, the motifs that form the 
selectivity pore, are conserved during evolution. Thus, CNNM proteins, the vertebrate orthologues of CorB/C, also have Mg<sup>2+</sup> transport capacity. Whereas CorA and CorB/C proteins share the gross 
quaternary structure and functional properties with their respective 
orthologues, the MgtE channel only shares the selectivity pore with 
SLC41 Na<sup>+</sup>/Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters (<span id='ref34555038.35819535' class='cite'>Franken <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p>CNNMs (CBS-pair domain divalent metal cation transport mediators) are a ubituitous family of Mg transporters.&nbsp; There are four CNNM proteins in humans, which are involved in divalent cation transport, genetic diseases, and cancer (<span id='ref34556498.37222397' class='cite'>Chen and Gehring 2023</span>).&nbsp; Eukaryotic CNNMs are composed of four domains: an extracellular domain, a transmembrane domain, a CBS (cystathionine-beta-synthase)-pair domain, and a cyclic nucleotide-binding homology domain. The transmembrane and CBS-pair core are the defining features of CNNM proteins with over 20,000 protein sequences known from over 8,000 species. <span id='ref34556498.37222397' class='cite'>Chen and Gehring 2023</span> reviewed the structural and functional studies of eukaryotic and prokaryotic CNNMs that underlie our understanding of the regulation and mechanism of ion transport. Prokaryotic CNNMs indicates that the transmembrane domain mediates ion transport with the CBS-pair domain playing a regulatory role by binding divalent cations (<span id='ref34556498.37222397' class='cite'>Chen and Gehring 2023</span>).</p>
