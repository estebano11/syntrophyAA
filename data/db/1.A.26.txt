<TABLE>
<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center; '><strong>1.A.26 The Mg<sup>2+</sup> Transporter-E (MgtE) Family</strong></p>
<p>Several functionally characterized members of the MgtE/SLC41 family have been described. These include MgtE from the Gram-positive bacterium, <em>Bacillus firmu</em>s OF4, and MgtE from the Gram-negative bacterium, <em>Providencia stuartii</em>. These proteins are capable of transporting Mg<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> but not Ni<sup>2+</sup>. The K<sub>M</sub> values for Mg<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> uptake are in the 50-100 &mu;M range. An MgtE homologue from <em>Aeromonas hydrophila</em> expressed in a <em>Salmonella typhimurium </em>strain that is deleted for all Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters complements the defect, both phenotypically and by allowing <sup>57</sup>Co<sup>2+</sup> uptake (<span id='ref8503.11430413' class='cite'>Merino <em>et al.</em>, 2001</span>). MstE (1.A.26.1.2), CLC (2.A.49.6.1) and HlyC/CorC (HCC; 9.A.40.1.2) may all share a hydrophilic domain.&nbsp; The metal-binding site of the MgtE TM domain binds to Mg<sup>2+ </sup>approximately 500-fold more strongly than to Ca<sup>2+</sup>.&nbsp;<span id='ref34557410.36465111' class='cite'>Teng <em>et al.</em> 2022</span> determined the crystal structure of the MgtE TM domain in complex with Ca<sup>2+</sup> at 2.5 &Aring; resolution, revealing hexahydrated Ca<sup>2+</sup>. These results provide mechanistic insights into the ion selectivity of MgtE for Mg<sup>2+</sup> over Ca<sup>2+</sup>.</p>
<p>MgtE is a homodimeric Mg<sup>2+</sup>-selective channel and is negatively regulated by high intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentrations where the cytoplasmic domain of MgtE acts as a Mg<sup>2+</sup> sensor, and Mg<sup>2+</sup>-dependent gating is dependent on lipid-protein interactions  (<span id='ref34552257.33203509' class='cite'>Chatterjee <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). MgtE structures in the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-bound, closed-state, and structure-based functional analyses of MgtE revealed that the binding of Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions to the MgtE cytoplasmic domain induces channel inactivation to maintain Mg<sup>2+</sup> homeostasis.&nbsp;<span id='ref34553508.33905418' class='cite'>Jin <em>et al.</em> 2021</span> determined the cryo-EM structure of a MgtE-Fab complex in the absence of Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions. The Mg<sup>2+</sup>-free MgtE TM domain structure and its comparison with the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-bound, closed-state structure, together with functional analyses, showed the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-dependent pore opening of MgtE on the cytoplasmic side and revealed the kink motions of the TMS2 and TMS5 helices at the glycine residues, which are important for channel activity (<span id='ref34553508.33905418' class='cite'>Jin <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>Members of the family have been sequenced from a wide variety of bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes. Their phylogeny roughly follows that of the organismal taxonomies. They have sizes that vary considerably from 311 residues for the <em>Methanococcus thermoautotrophicum</em> protein, 463 residues for the <em>Synechocystis</em> homologue, and 513 residues for the human homologue, SLC41A1. The <em>B. firmus</em> transporter and several homologues examined have strongly charged, hydrophilic N-terminal domains (cytoplasmic) followed by a hydrophobic C-terminal domain with 5 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners. A central 100 residues resembles archaeal inositol monophosphate dehydrogenases (<span id='ref8504.12206392' class='cite'>Kehres and Maguire, 2002</span>). Kehres and Maguire (<span id='ref8504.12206392' class='cite'>2002</span>) suggested that the MgtE proteins are secondary carriers (uniporters?) with inwardly directed polarity. Hattori et al. (<span id='ref8505.19798051' class='cite'>2009</span>) have considered MgtE to function by a channel mechanism. They provided evidence that the MgtE cytosolic domain acts as a Mg<sup>2+</sup> sensor to regulate gating of the pore in response to the intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration. This produces a mechanism for the maintenance of homeostasis conditions (<span id='ref8505.19798051' class='cite'>Hattori <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).&nbsp; Structure, mechanism and regulation have been reviewed (<span id='ref10566.23954807' class='cite'>Payandeh <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>; <span id='ref10567.23506895' class='cite'>Sahni and Scharenberg 2013</span>).</p>
<p>Mg<sup>2+</sup> has the largest hydrated radius among all cations although its ionic radius is the smallest. How do Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters selectively recognize and dehydrate the large, fully hydrated Mg<sup>2+</sup> cation for transport? Human homologues have been functionally characterized and suggested to be involved in magnesium homeostasis.  Hattori et al. (<span id='ref8506.17700703' class='cite'>2007</span>) determined the crystal structures of the full-length <em>Thermus thermophi</em><em>lus</em> MgtE at 3.5 &Aring; resolution, and of the cytosolic domain in the presence and absence of Mg<sup>2+</sup> at 2.3 &Aring; and 3.9 &Aring; resolutions, respectively. The transporter adopts a homodimeric architecture. The carboxy-terminal domain has five TMSs. The amino-terminal cytosolic part consists of the superhelical N domain and tandemly repeated cystathionine-&beta;-synthase domains. A solvent-accessible pore nearly traverses the transmembrane domains, with one potential Mg<sup>2+</sup> bound to the conserved Asp 432 within the pore. The transmembrane helices #5 from both subunits close the pore through interactions with the 'connecting helices', which connect the cystathionine-&beta;-synthase and transmembrane domains. Four putative Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions are bound at the interface between the connecting helices and the other domains, and this may lock the closed conformation of the pore. A structural comparison of the two states of the cytosolic domains showed the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-dependent movement of the connecting helices, which might reorganize the transmembrane helices to open the pore. These findings suggest a homeostatic mechanism, in which Mg<sup>2+</sup>, bound between cytosolic domains, regulates Mg<sup>2+</sup> flux by sensing the intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration. Whether this presumed regulation controls gating of an ion channel or opening of a secondary active transporter remains to be determined.</p>
<p>The cytosolic domain of MgtE undergoes a Mg<sup>2+</sup>-dependent structural change, which may gate the ion-conducting pore passing through the transmembrane domain. Ishitani et al. (<span id='ref8507.18832160' class='cite'>2008</span>) performed molecular dynamics simulations of the MgtE cytosolic domain which reproduced the structural changes of the cytosolic domain upon binding or releasing Mg<sup>2+</sup>. The roles of the N and CBS domains in the cytosolic domain and their respective Mg<sup>2+</sup> binding sites were proposed.</p>
<p>Maruyama et al. (<span id='ref8508.22477092' class='cite'>2012</span>) showed that MgtE exhibits the channel-like electrophysiological property, i.e., Mg<sup>2+ </sup>transport occurs in&nbsp;accordance to the electrochemical potential of Mg<sup>2+</sup>. The Mg<sup>2+</sup>-permeation pathway opens in response to a decrease of the intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration, while it is completely closed at the intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration of 10 mM. The crystal structures of the MgtE dimer reveals that the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-sensing cytoplasmic region consists of the N and CBS domains. The Mg<sup>2+</sup>-bound state adopts a compact, globular conformation, which is stabilized by the coordination of a number of Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions between these domains. On the other hand, in the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-unbound state, these domains are far apart, and fixed by the crystal packing. Maruyama et al. (<span id='ref8508.22477092' class='cite'>2012</span>) reported the backbone resonance assignments of the dimer of the cytoplasmic region of the MgtE from <em>Thermus thermophilus </em>with a molecular weight of 60 KDa, in the Mg<sup>2+</sup>-unbound state.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The crystal structures of bacterial <span class='highlight'>CorA</span> and <span class='highlight'>MgtE</span> Mg<sup>2+</sup> channels revealed that both 
structures are unique, unlike that of any other channel or transporter (<span id='ref10131.18927203' class='cite'>Moomaw and Maguire 2008</span>). 
Although structurally quite different, both <span class='highlight'>CorA</span> and <span class='highlight'>MgtE</span> appear to be gated in a similar manner through multiple Mg<sup>2+</sup> binding 
sites in the cytosolic domain of the channels. These sites essentially 
serve as Mg<sup>2+</sup> 'sensors' of cytosolic Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration. MgtE tightly regulates the intracellular Mg<sup>2+</sup> concentration. The dimeric structure is composed of a total of ten transmembrane &alpha;-helices, forming a central pore. Intracellular soluble domains constitute the Mg<sup>2+</sup> sensor. The ion selectivity for Mg<sup>2+ </sup>over Ca<sup>2+</sup> resides in a central cavity in the transmembrane pore involving a conserved aspartate residue (Asp432) from each monomer. Two different classes of binding sites are present in MgtE: a high affinity site for Mg<sup>2+</sup> (Kd 0.3 mM) with low Ca<sup>2+</sup> affinity (Kd 80 mM), and a medium affinity site for Mg<sup>2+</sup> (Kd 2 mM) and Ca<sup>2+ </sup>(Kd 6 mM), tentatively assigned to the central cavity and the sensor domain, respectively (<span id='ref34548796.30252477' class='cite'>Kimura <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).</p>
