<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong>1.A.29 The Urea/Amide Channel (UAC) Family</strong></p>
<p>Proteins of the UAC family are encoded within operons that also encode ureases or amidases. The putative permeases (the AmiS proteins) may therefore catalyze transport of urea and short chain aliphatic amides such as acetamide. They are of 171-206 amino acyl residues in length and possess 6 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs). AmiS homologues are found in both Gram-positive bacteria and Gram-negative bacteria. ATP-binding proteins (AmiB) homologous to Clp proteases are found within some but not all of these operons. AmiB has been proposed to function to energize transport, but no direct evidence supports this proposal.</p>
<p>UreI homologues of <em>Helicobacter pylori</em> and <em>H. hepaticus</em> have been functionally characterized (<a class='reflink' href='#ref1832'>Weeks and Sachs, 2001</a>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref2805'>Weeks <em>et al</em>., 2004</a>). These 6 TMS proteins of 195 and 170 amino acyl residues form H<sup> </sup>-gated urea channels (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7748'>Sachs <em>et al.</em>, 2006</a>). A periplasmic histidine, his 123, is essential for H<sup> </sup> stimulation of channel activity of the <em>H. pylori</em> protein while several residues including His-50 (boundary of TMS2), Glu-56 (1st periplasmic loop), Asp-59 (boundary of TMS3) and His-70 (boundary of TMS6) affect proton gating in the <em>H. hepaticus</em> protein.  Thus, gating is due to periplasmically localized residues that control the channel conformation.</p>
<p>UreI-mediated urea transport is urea specific, passive, nonsaturable, relatively temperature-independent and nonelectrogenic. It is the H<sup> </sup>-gated urea channel that regulates cytoplasmic urease, the enzyme that allows survival and colonization of the stomach by <em>H. pylori.</em></p>
<p><em>H.
pylori</em> causes gastritis, gastric ulcers and 
gastric adenocarcinoma. Its proton-gated inner-membrane urea channel, 
HpUreI, is essential for survival in the acidic environment of the 
stomach. The channel is closed at neutral pH and opens at acidic pH to 
allow the rapid access of urea to cytoplasmic urease. Urease produces 
NH<sub>3</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub>, neutralizing entering protons and thus buffering the 
periplasm to a pH of roughly 6.1, even in gastric juice at a pH below 
2.0. Strugatsky et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9479'>2012</a>) reported the structure of HpUreI, revealing six protomers 
assembled in a hexameric ring surrounding a central bilayer plug of 
ordered lipids. Each protomer encloses a channel formed by a twisted 
bundle of six transmembrane helices. The bundle defines a previously 
unobserved fold comprising a two-helix hairpin motif repeated three 
times around the central axis of the channel, without the inverted 
repeat of mammalian-type urea transporters. Both the channel and the 
protomer interface contain residues conserved in the AmiS/UreI 
superfamily, suggesting the preservation of channel architecture and 
oligomeric state in this superfamily. Predominantly aromatic or 
aliphatic side chains line the entire channel and define two consecutive
 constriction sites in the middle of the channel. Mutation of Trp%u2009153 in
 the cytoplasmic constriction site to Ala or Phe decreased the 
selectivity for urea in comparison with thiourea, suggesting that solute
 interaction with Trp%u2009153 contributes specificity. This hexameric channel structure provides a 
model for the permeation of urea and small amides in 
prokaryotes (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9479'>Strugatsky <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>).</p>
<p>The transport reaction catalyzed by homologues of the UAC family is:</p>
