<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>1.A.43 The Camphor Resistance or Fluoride Channel (Fluc) Family</strong></p>
<p>CrcB of <em>E. coli</em> appears to confer camphor resistance when overexpressed and induces<em> resA </em>expression (<span id='ref10593.12904550' class='cite'>Sand <em>et al.</em> 2003</span>). Deletion is not lethal but increases sensitivity to camphor and fluoride. CrcB has 127 aas and 4 TMSs. Its many close homologues are small proteins, usually with 4 TMSs.&nbsp; These proteins mediate fluoride efflux and resistance (<span id='ref11330.25156118' class='cite'>Ji <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).&nbsp; The active transporter is a dimer of 4 TMS subunits arranged antiparallel (<span id='ref11379.25290819' class='cite'>Stockbridge <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).</p>
<p>Fluc family fluoride channels are assembled as primitive antiparallel homodimers (<span id='ref34550830.31945374' class='cite'>McIlwain <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). Crystallographic studies revealed a cation bound at the center of the protein, where it is coordinated at the dimer interface by mainchain carbonyl oxygens from the mid-membrane breaks in two corresponding TMSs.&nbsp; This cation is a stably bound sodium ion, and, although it is not a transported substrate, its presence is required for the channel to adopt an open, fluoride conducting conformation. The interfacial site is selective for sodium over other cations, except for Li<sup>+</sup>, which competes with Na<sup>+</sup> for binding, but does not support channel activity. Fluoride, F<sup>-</sup>, ubiquitous in soil, water and marine environments, is a chronic threat to microorganisms. Many bacteria, archaea, unicellular eukaryotes and plants use F<sup>-</sup> exporters to lower cytoplasmic F<sup>-</sup> levels to counteract the anion's toxicity (<span id='ref34550830.31945374' class='cite'>McIlwain <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p><span id='ref11259.23991286' class='cite'>Stockbridge <em>et al.</em> 2013</span> and&nbsp;<span id='ref10667.24173035' class='cite'>Li <em>et al.</em> 2013</span> showed that these ''Fluc'' proteins, purified and reconstituted in liposomes and planar phospholipid bilayers, form constitutively open anion channels with extreme selectivity for F<sup>-</sup> over Cl<sup>-</sup>. The active channel is a dimer of identical or homologous subunits arranged in antiparallel transmembrane orientation, or a single protein with two internal 4TMS repeats (as for the yeast proteins (TC# 1.A.43.2.4 and 5). This dual-topological assembly had not previously been seen in ion channels but is known in multidrug transporters of the SMR sub-family of the DMT superfamily (TC# 2.A.7.1) (<span id='ref11257.16373573' class='cite'>Pornillos <em>et al.</em> 2005; </span><span id='ref11258.16406365' class='cite'>Pornillos and Chang 2006</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Crystal structures showed
that Fluc channels contain two separate ion-conduction pathways, each with two F<sup>-</sup> binding sites (<span id='ref34545683.26344196' class='cite'>Stockbridge <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp;<span id='ref34545682.27449280' class='cite'>Last <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> examined the consequences of mutating two conserved F<sup>-</sup>-coordinating phenylalanine residues.
Substitution of each phenylalanine specifically extinguished its associated F<sup>-</sup> binding site in
crystal structures and concomitantly inhibited F<sup>-</sup> permeation. Functional analysis of concatemeric
channels, which permit mutagenic manipulation of individual pores, showed that each pore can be
separately inactivated without blocking F<sup>-</sup> conduction through its symmetry-related twin. The results
strongly supported a dual-pathway architecture of these channels (<span id='ref34545682.27449280' class='cite'>Last <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Baker, J.L., N.
