<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>1.A.77 The Mg2+/Ca2+ Uniporter (MCU) Family</strong></p>
<p>Mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> homeostasis plays a key role in the regulation of aerobic metabolism and cell survival.  Mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake occurs via the ruthenium red sensitive Ca<sup>2+</sup> uniporter (MCU), and efflux occurs via a Na<sup>+</sup>/Ca<sup>2+</sup> exchanger (mNCX). De Stefani et al. (<span id='ref7932.21685888' class='cite'>2011</span>) and Baughman et al. (<span id='ref8475.21685886' class='cite'>2011</span>) simultaneously reported the identity of a protein (named MCU) that shares tissue distribution with MICU1 (also known as CBARA1), a&nbsp;&nbsp; uniporter regulator that is present in organisms in which mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake was demonstrated and whose sequence includes two transmembrane domains.&nbsp; Short interfering RNA (siRNA) silencing of MCU reduced mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake, and MCU overproduction increased the matrix Ca<sup>2+</sup> concentration evoked by inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate-generating agonists. The purified MCU protein showed channel activity in planar lipid bilayers, with electrophysiological properties and inhibitor sensitivities of the uniporter 90. A mutant MCU, in which two negatively charged residues of the putative pore-forming region were replaced, had no channel activity and reduced agonist-dependent matrix Ca<sup>2+</sup> concentration transients when overexpressed. Thus, MCU is the channel responsible for ruthenium-red-sensitive mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake. Distant prokaryotic homologues have been identified (A. Lee and MH Saier, unpublished results).&nbsp;  A small (10 KDa) protein with 1 TMS, EMRE, is required for interaction of MCU with MICU1 and 2 (see figure4F in <span id='ref10445.24231807' class='cite'>Sancak <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).&nbsp; The entire complex includes MICU1, MICU2, EMRE and MCU, and has a molecular size of 480,000 Daltons.&nbsp; The MICU family has TC# 8.A.44 and functions in regulation.&nbsp; The EMRE family has TC# 8.A.45 and funtions to interconnect MUC and MICU in the complex (<span id='ref10445.24231807' class='cite'>Sancak <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).</p>
<p>Mitochondrial calcium uniporter, MCU, forms oligomers in the mitochondrial inner membrane, physically interacts with MICU1 (1.A.76.1.1), and resides within a large molecular weight complex. Silencing MCU in cultured cells or in vivo in mouse liver severely abrogates mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake, whereas mitochondrial respiration and membrane potential remain fully intact. MICU1 (TC# 8.A.44.1.1) is an essential component of the MCU system, and serves as the gatekeeper of MCU-mediated Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake that is essnetial  to prevent Ca<sup>2+</sup> overload and associated stress (<span id='ref9427.23178883' class='cite'>Mallilankaraman <em>et al.</em>, 2012a</span>; <span id='ref9426.23101630' class='cite'>Mallilankaraman <em>et al.</em>, 2012b</span>). The oligomeric channel can incorporate an inhibitory subunit, MCUb, that exerts a dominant-negative effect on channel formation (<span id='ref10231.23900286' class='cite'>Raffaello <em>et al.</em>, 2013</span>).<span style='font-size: 10px;'> Also essential is the EMRE subunit (TC# 8.A.45.1.1) which binds MICU1 via transmembrane helices to control Ca<sup>2+</sup> transport activity (<span id='ref34545365.27099988' class='cite'>Tsai <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>). MCUs interact with Miro1 (Q8IXI2), a mitochondrial Rho GTPase that seems to regulate MCU activities (<span id='ref34548236.29686046' class='cite'>Niescier <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).</span> Carboxylate-Capped Analogues of<span style='font-size: 10px;'> Ru265 are prodrugs that</span> inhibit mitochondrial calcium uptake in intact, nonpermeabilized cells, revealing their value as tools and potential therapeutic agents for mitochondrial calcium-related disorders (<span id='ref34555612.36260092' class='cite'>Bigham <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>)</p>
<p>MCU has two predicted transmembrane helices, which are separated by a highly conserved linker facing the intermembrane space. Acidic residues in this linker are required for its full activity. However, an S259A point mutation retained function but confered resistance to Ru360, the most potent inhibitor of the uniporter (<span id='ref8475.21685886' class='cite'>Baughman <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). MCU homologues and their cytoplasmic regulatory protein partners with two EF-hand motifs, MICU, are both present in many but not all eukaryotes having MCU (<span id='ref8720.22605770' class='cite'>Bick <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>). The phylogenies and domain orders of MCU Ca<sup>2+</sup> channel homologues have been reported (<span id='ref8720.22605770' class='cite'>Bick <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>). MCU is involved in the apoptosis in PC12 cells induced by 1-methyl-4-phenylpyridinium ions (MPP) (<span id='ref34549679.29538099' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).&nbsp; Diplacone, isolated from <em>Paulownia tomentosa</em> mature fruit, induces ferroptosis-mediated cell death through mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx and the mitochondrial permeability transition (see TC# 1.A.129) (<span id='ref34556378.37108220' class='cite'>Oh <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>Ca<sup>2+</sup> flux across the inner mitochondrial membrane (IMM) regulates cellular bioenergetics, intra-cellular cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> signals, and various cell death pathways. Ca<sup>2+</sup> entry into the mitochondria occurs due to the highly negative membrane potential (&Delta;&Psi;<sub>m</sub>)
 through a selective inward rectifying MCU channel (<span id='ref34550288.29980025' class='cite'>Nemani <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). In addition to being
 regulated by various mitochondrial matrix resident proteins such as 
MICUs, MCUb, MCUR1 and EMRE, the channel is transcriptionally regulated 
by an upstream Ca<sup>2+</sup> cascade, post transnational modification and divalent cations. The mode of regulation either inhibits or enhances
 MCU channel activity and thus regulates mitochondrial metabolism and 
cell fate (<span id='ref34550288.29980025' class='cite'>Nemani <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>)</p>
<p>The <em>atp</em> operon of alkaliphilic <em>Bacillus pseudofirmus</em> OF4, as in most prokaryotes, contains the eight structural genes for the F-ATPase (ATP synthase), which are preceded by an <em>atpI</em> gene that encodes a membrane protein with 2 TMSs. A tenth gene, <em>atpZ</em>, has been found in this operon, which is upstream of and overlapping with <em>atpI</em> (<span id='ref7965.12917488' class='cite'>Hicks <em>et al.</em>, 2003</span>). Most <em>Bacillus</em> species, and some other bacteria, possess atpZ homologues. AtpZ is predicted to be a membrane protein with a hairpin topology. Deletion of <em>atpZ</em>, <em>atpI</em>, or <em>atpZI</em> from B.&nbsp;<em>pseudofirmus</em> OF4 led to a requirement for a greatly increased concentration of Mg2 for growth at pH 7.5. Either <em>atpZ</em>, <em>atpI</em>, or <em>atpZI</em> complemented the similar phenotype of a triple mutant of <em>Salmonella typhimurium</em>(MM281), which is deficient in Mg2+ uptake. atpZ and atpI, separately and together, increased the Mg<sup>2+</sup> -sensitive <sup>45</sup>Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake by vesicles of an <em>Escherichia coli</em> mutant that is defective in Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> efflux. Hicks <em>et al.</em> (<span id='ref7965.12917488' class='cite'>2003</span>) hypothesized that AtpZ and AtpI, as homooligomers, and perhaps as heterooligomers, are Mg<sup>2+</sup> transporters, Ca<sup>2+</sup> transporters, probable Mg<sup>2+</sup>/Ca<sup>2+</sup> channel proteins. Such proteins could provide Mg<sup>2+</sup> , which is required by ATP synthase, and also support charge compensation, when the enzyme is functioning in the hydrolytic direction e.g., during cytoplasmic pH regulation. AtpZ and AtpI have 2 and 4 TMSs respectively.</p>
<p>The Na<sup>+</sup> F<sub>1</sub>F<sub>O</sub> ATP synthase of the anaerobic acetogenic bacterium <em>Acetobacterium woodii</em> has a unique hybrid rotor that contains nine copies of a F<sub>O</sub>-like c subunit with 1 ion binding site in the 2TMS protein, and one copy of a V<sub>O</sub>-like c(1) subunit with one ion binding site in four transmembrane helices. Brandt<em> et al. </em>(<span id='ref9972.23054076' class='cite'>2013</span>) cloned and expressed its Na<sup>+</sup> F<sub>1</sub>F<sub>O</sub> ATP synthase operon in <em>E. coli</em>. A <em>&Delta;atp</em> mutant of <em>E. coli</em> produced a functional, membrane-bound Na<sup>+</sup> F<sub>1</sub>F<sub>O</sub> ATP synthase that was purified in a single step after inserting a His(6)-tag to its &beta; subunit. The purified enzyme was competent in Na<sup>+</sup> transport and contained the F<sub>O</sub>V<sub>O </sub>hybrid rotor in the same stoichiometry as in <em>A. woodii</em>. Deletion of the <em>atpI</em> gene from the <em>A. woodii</em> operon resulted in a loss of the c ring and a mis-assembled Na<sup>+</sup> F<sub>1</sub>F<sub>O</sub> ATP synthase. AtpI from <em>E. coli</em> did not substitute for AtpI from <em>A. woodii</em>. Thus, the native AtpI is required for assembly of the hybrid rotor.</p>
<p>The uniporter is membrane potential
dependent and sensitive to ruthenium red and its derivative Ru360. It has high conductance and
selectivity. Ca<sup>2+</sup> entry into mitochondria activates the tricarboxylic acid cycle
and seems to be crucial for matching the production of ATP in mitochondria with its cytosolic
demand. MCU is the pore-forming subunit of
the uniporter holocomplex. <span id='ref34545397.27135929' class='cite'>Oxenoid <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> reported the structure of the pore domain of MCU from <em>Caenorhabditis elegans</em>, determined
using nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and electron microscopy (EM). MCU is a homo-oligomer in which
the second transmembrane helix forms a hydrophilic pore. The channel assembly displays a unique ion channel architecture and is stabilized by a coiled-coil motif
protruding into the mitochondrial matrix. The critical DXXE motif forms the pore entrance, which
features two carboxylate rings; these rings
appear to form the selectivity filter (<span id='ref34545397.27135929' class='cite'>Oxenoid <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
<p>Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake into mitochondria regulates bioenergetics, apoptosis, and Ca<sup>2+</sup> signaling. The primary pathway for mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake is MCU. Mitochondrial Ca<sup>2+</sup> uptake is tightly regulated to maintain low matrix [Ca<sup>2+</sup>] and prevent opening of the permeability transition pore and cell death, while meeting dynamic cellular energy demands. <span id='ref34552026.32801213' class='cite'>Vais <em>et al.</em> 2020 </span>defined a regulatory mechanism in which cytoplasmic Ca<sup>2+</sup> regulation of intermembrane space-localized MICU1/2 is controlled by Ca<sup>2+</sup>-regulatory mechanisms localized across the membrane in the mitochondrial matrix. Ca<sup>2+</sup> that permeates through the channel pore regulates Ca<sup>2+</sup> affinities of coupled inhibitory and activating sensors in the matrix. Ca<sup>2+</sup> binding to the inhibitory sensor within the MCU amino terminus closes the channel despite Ca<sup>2+</sup> binding to MICU1/2. Conversely, disruption of the interaction of MICU1/2 with the MCU complex disables matrix Ca<sup>2+</sup> regulation of channel activity. These results demonstrate how Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx into mitochondria is tuned by coupled Ca<sup>2+</sup>-regulatory mechanisms on both sides of the inner mitochondrial membrane (<span id='ref34552026.32801213' class='cite'>Vais <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p>As noted above, MCU controls mitochondrial 
bioenergetics, and its activity varies greatly between tissues.&nbsp;<span id='ref34556006.36517314' class='cite'>Xue <em>et al.</em> 2023</span> highlighted a recently identified MCU-EMRE-UCP1 complex, named 
thermoporter, in the adaptive thermogenesis of brown adipose tissue 
(BAT). The thermoporter enhances MCU activity to promote thermogenic 
metabolism, demonstrating a BAT-specific regulation for MCU activity.</p>
