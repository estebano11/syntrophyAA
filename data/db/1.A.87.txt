<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>1.A.87 The Mechanosensitive Calcium Channel (MCA) Family</strong></p>
<p style='text-align: left;'>Mechano-sensitive channels of plants sense increases in tension induced by mechanical
stimuli, such as touch, wind, turgor pressure and gravitation. Plant homologues of MscS bacterial mechano-sensitive channels are known which are gated by
membrane tension. Two of them have been shown to be involved in the protection of osmotically stressed
plastids in <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> (see TC# 1.A.23.4.4). Membrane tension is not a mediator of long-range intracellular 
signaling, but local variations in tension mediate distinct processes in
 sub-cellular domains (<span id='ref34550237.30392960' class='cite'>Shi <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). Lipid bilayer tensiometers have been used for the study of mechanosensitive ion channels (<span id='ref34556070.36913590' class='cite'>P&eacute;rez-Mitta and MacKinnon 2023</span>). Heavy metal tolerance mechanisms of <em>Brassica </em>species have been reviewed (<span id='ref34557514.37924172' class='cite'>Shehzad <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p><a class='reflink' href='#ref10061'>Iida <em>et al.</em> (2013) </a>identified another group of candidates for mechano-sensitive
channels in <em>Arabidopsis</em>, named MCA1 and MCA2, whose homologues are exclusively found in plant
genomes. MCA1 and MCA2 are composed of 421 and 416 amino acyl residues, respectively, share 73% identity in their amino acid sequences, and are not homologous to any other known ion channels or
transporters. A structural study revealed that the N-terminal region (~173 amino acids) of
both proteins is necessary and sufficient for Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx activity. This region has
one putative transmembrane segment containing an Asp residue whose substitution mutation abolished activity.Their physiological study suggested that MCA1, expressed at the root tip, is required
for sensing the hardness of the agar medium or soil. In addition, MCA1 and MCA2 were shown to be
responsible for hypo-osmotic shock-induced increases in [Ca<sup>2+</sup>]<sub>cyt </sub>. Thus, both proteins appear to
be involved in the process of sensing mechanical stresses. <a class='reflink' href='#ref10061'>Iida <em>et al.</em> (2013)</a> discussed the possible roles of both
proteins in sensing mechanical and gravitational stimuli.&nbsp; Several homologues may serve as receptors and regulatory proteins rather than ion channels, and several of these are included in this family in TCDB.&nbsp; Their roles as <span class='highlight'>mechanosensitive</span> plasma membrane Ca<sup>2+</sup>-permeable <span class='highlight'>channel</span>s, such as OsMCA1and OsMCA2 in rice seems to allow them to play roles in the generation of reactive oxygen species and in hypo-osmotic signaling (<span id='ref34549839.22080252' class='cite'>Kurusu <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>; <span id='ref34549840.22264357' class='cite'>Kurusu <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>; <span id='ref34549841.22751305' class='cite'>Kurusu <em>et al.</em> 2012).</span></p>
<p style='text-align: left;'>MCA proteins show various topologies.&nbsp; Several show a 1 + 3 TMS topology (subfamily 1) while others (subfamily 2) appear to have a 1 + 3 + 3 TMS topology, and still others have just 3 TMSs (subfamily 3).&nbsp; The 3 TMSs in these last mentioned proteins appear to correspond to the last 3 TMSs in subfamilies 1 and 2. The topologies of subfamilies 4 and 5 are not clear.&nbsp; There may be additional topological variations.</p>
<p>The FW2.2 gene is associated with the major Quantitative Trait Locus (QTL) governing fruit size in the tomato, and it acts by negatively controlling cell division during fruit development. FW2.2 belongs to a multigene family named the CELL NUMBER REGULATOR (CNR) family (<span id='ref34553119.33974684' class='cite'>Beauchet <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>). The CNR proteins harbour the uncharacterized PLAC8 motif made of two conserved cysteine-rich domains separated by a variable region that are predicted to be transmembrane segments, and indeed FW2.2 localizes to the plasma membrane.&nbsp; <span id='ref34553119.33974684' class='cite'>Beauchet <em>et al.</em> 2021</span> reviewed the knowledge on PLAC8-containing CNR/FWL proteins in plants, which participate in plant organogenesis and the regulation of organ size, especially in fruits, and in cadmium resistance, ion homeostasis and/or Ca<sup>2+</sup> signalling. Within the plasma membrane, FW2.2 and some CNR/FWL proteins are localized in microdomains. Hence FW2.2 and CNR/FWL could be involved in a transport function of signalling molecules across membranes, thus influencing organ growth via a cell-to-cell trafficking mechanism (<span id='ref34553119.33974684' class='cite'>Beauchet <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>The generalized reaction reported to be catalyzed by MCA1 and MCA2 is:</p>
<p style='text-align: center;'>Ca<sup>2+</sup>(out)&nbsp; &rarr;&nbsp; Ca<sup>2+</sup> (in)</p>
<p>Nucleotide-binding, leucine-rich repeat receptors (NLRs) are major 
immune receptors in plants and animals. Upon activation, the <em>Arabidopsis</em> NLR protein ZAR1 forms a pentameric resistosome in vitro and triggers 
immune responses and cell death in plants.&nbsp;<span id='ref34557203.33984278' class='cite'>Bi <em>et al.</em> 2021</span> employed 
single-molecule imaging to show that the activated ZAR1 protein can form
 pentameric complexes in the plasma membrane. The ZAR1 resistosome 
displayed ion channel activity in <em>Xenopus</em> oocytes in a manner dependent 
on a conserved acidic residue Glu11 situated in the channel pore. 
Pre-assembled ZAR1 resistosome was readily incorporated into planar 
lipid-bilayers and displayed calcium-permeable cation-selective channel 
activity. The authors showed that activation of ZAR1 in the plant cell
 led to Glu11-dependent Ca<sup>2+</sup> influx, perturbation of 
subcellular structures, production of reactive oxygen species, and cell 
death. Cations transported include The results thus support that the ZAR1 resistosome acts as a 
calcium-permeable cation channel to trigger immunity and cell death.</p>
<p id='p0030'><span><span>The plant innate immune system is composed of cell surface receptors,</span> which perceive immunogenic molecular patterns derived from invading 
pathogens, and intracellular nucleotide-binding, leucine-rich repeat 
receptors (NLRs), which sense pathogen effectors that are delivered into
 the host cell intended to promote pathogenesis</span><span>. Plant NLRs
 can be classified based on their variable N-terminal domains, thus 
those carrying a coiled coli (CC) domain are called CNLs, those carrying
 a Toll-interleukin 1 
receptor (TIR) domain are referred to as TNLs, and those carrying an 
RPW8-like CC domain (CC</span><sub>R</sub>) are called <span>RNLs. NLRs are also important innate immune sensors in animals</span><span>. Upon activation, NLRs often form oligomeric complexes referred to as inflammasomes in animals and resistosomes in plants</span><span>. Animal inflammasomes activate caspases<span> to promote the maturation of gasdermin proteins, which form pores in 
the plasma membrane (PM) to trigger pyroptosis and immune responses</span></span><span>.&nbsp; Activation of plant NLRs also leads to regulated cell death called the hypersensitive response (HR)</span>. ZAR1
 is a CNL with a canonical CC domain that acts both as a sensor for 
pathogen effectors and an executor for signaling. ZAR1 is 
an ancient NLR that emerged more than 100 million years ago and is able 
to sense a growing number of pathogen effector proteins (<span id='ref34557203.33984278' class='cite'>Bi <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p><span>ZAR1 senses diverse effector proteins by associating with a class of homologous pseudokinases <span>called ZRKs and a second class of kinases, PBLs.</span></span><span> Prior to pathogen infection, ZAR1 interacts with various ZRKs in the 
resting state. Upon pathogen delivery of effectors into the plant cell, 
ZRKs in the pre-formed complexes further recruit PBL proteins that have 
been post-translationally modified by effectors to form ternary complexes.&nbsp; For example, AvrAC uridylylates the </span><em>Arabidopsis</em> PBL2 protein, giving rise to PBL2<sup>UMP</sup>, which is then recruited by RKS1/ZRK1 to form a ZAR1-RKS1-PBL2<sup>UMP</sup> complex which transports cations including Ca<sup>2+</sup> (<span id='ref34557203.33984278' class='cite'>Bi <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>). The channel is permeable to Na<sup>+</sup>, K<sup>+</sup>, Cs<sup>+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> and Ca<sup>2+</sup>.
 The three proteins that comprise this complex are all members of TC 
family 1.A.87 as is ZRK1 of 351 aas and 2 TMSs (see TC# 1A 87.2.17).</p>
<p>The reaction catalyzed by the ZAR1-RKS1-PBL2<sup>UMP</sup> complex is:</p>
<p style='text-align: center;'>cation (out) ⇋ cation (in)</p>
