<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>2.A.103  The Bacterial Murein Precursor Exporter (MPE) Family</strong></p>
<p>Members of the MPE family are found in a large variety of 
Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria . They consist of 370-420 amino
 acyl residues with 9 (RodA) or 10 (FtsW) putative transmembrane 
&alpha;-helical spanners.  Experimental evidence for a 10 TMS model has been 
reported for FtsW of <em>Streptococcus pneumoniae</em> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7579'>G&eacute;rard <em>et al.,</em> 2002</a>).  The <em>S. pneumoniae</em> protein has both its N- and C-termini in the cytoplasm, a large (~60 
residue) cytoplasmic domain between TMSs 4 and 5, and a large (~80 
residue) extracytoplasmic loop between TMSs 7 and 8.</p>
<p>The best characterized members of the family are the FtsW cell 
division protein, the RodA rod shape determining protein (both of <em>E. coli</em>) and the SpoVE protein of <em>B. subtilis </em>(<a class='reflink' href='#ref7581'>Boyle <em>et al.</em> 1997</a>; <span id='ref7605.' class='cite'>Errington, 2003</span>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref7584'>Matsuzawa <em>et al.</em>, 1989</a>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref7585'>Sato <em>et al.</em>, 1990</a>). They have been shown to function in the translocation (export) of 
lipid-linked murein precursors such as NAG-NAM-pentapeptide 
pyrophosphoryl undecaprenol (lipid II) (<span id='ref11061.24711460' class='cite'>Mohammadi <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).  They interact with murein 
synthases as well as two transpeptidases (PBP2 and PBP3).  In 
Gram-negative bacteria the <em>ftsW</em> gene is physically linked to <em>murG</em> (TC# 9.B.146) which is responsible for the final cytoplasmic step in the synthesis of
 lipid II before it is flipped to the periplasmic side of the membrane. 
 They may therefore be part of a tunneling device directing the flow of 
murein precursors to the membrane enzymes that insert the precursors 
into the preexisting murein sacculus.</p>
<p>Bacterial cell growth necessitates synthesis of peptidoglycan. 
Assembly of peptidoglycan is a multistep process starting in the 
cytoplasm and ending in the exterior cell surface. The intracellular 
part of the pathway results in the production of the membrane-anchored 
cell wall precursor, Lipid II. After synthesis, this lipid intermediate 
is translocated across the cell membrane. The translocation (flipping) 
step of Lipid II requires a specific protein (flippase). Mohammadi et <em>al</em>.
 (<span id='ref7580.21386816' class='cite'>2011</span>) showed that the integral membrane protein FtsW, an essential 
protein for cell division,  is a transporter of the lipid-linked 
peptidoglycan precursors across the cytoplasmic membrane. Using <em>E. coli</em> membrane vesicles, they found that transport of Lipid II requires the 
presence of FtsW, and purified FtsW induced the transbilayer movement of
 Lipid II in model membranes.</p>
<p>The <em>E. coli</em> FtsW and peptidoglycan synthase, PBP3, form a 
subcomplex (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7586'>Derouaux <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref7587'>Fraipont <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref7588'>Maggi <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). The same is observed for SpoVE and SpoVD sporulation 
proteins in <em>B. subtilis</em> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7593'>Fay <em>et al.</em>, 2010</a>). Interaction networks (interactomes) have also been 
identified in<em> Synechocystis</em> strain PCC6803 (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7589'>Marbouty <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>) and <em>Streptococcus 
pneumoniae</em> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7592'>Maggi <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). A large complex involving RodA and the cytoskeletal ring 
in <em>E. coli</em> has been identified (<span id='ref7594.18390656' class='cite'>Uehara and Park, 2008</span>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref7591'>Vats <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>).</p>
