<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><span style='font-weight: bold;'>2.A.106 Ca</span><sup>2+</sup><span style='font-weight: bold;'>:H</span><sup>+</sup><sup>&nbsp;</sup><span style='font-weight: bold;'>Antiporter-2 (CaCA2) Family</span></p>
<p style='text-align: left;'>The PF27 family (SwissProt family UPF0016; Prosite entry PDOC00934) is a small family of proteins found in bacteria, archaea, yeast, plants and animals. They are usually 200-350 amino acyl residues long and exhibit 5-7 TMSs. Congenital Disorders of <span class='highlight'>Glycosylation</span> (CDG) are rare inherited diseases causing <span class='highlight'>glycosylation</span> defects responsible for severe growth and psychomotor retardations in 
patients.  TMEM165 has been implicated in both Golgi vesicular trafficking and ion homeostasis. TMEM165 deficient mammalian 
cells or yeast cells deficient in Gtd1p, the yeast TMEM165 ortholog suggest that TMEM165 is a transporter involved in ion homeostasis (<span id='ref34545691.27401145' class='cite'>Dulary <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). Several 
studies indicate that TMEM165 is a Golgi localized Ca<sup>2+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> and/or Mn<sup>2+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporter. Possibly TMEM165 proteins are Mn<sup>2+</sup> transporters essential to achieve the correct N-<span class='highlight'>glycosylation</span> process of <span class='highlight'>proteins</span> in the secretory pathway.</p>
<p style='text-align: -webkit-auto;'>Defects in the human TMEM165 homologue are the cause of congenital disorder of glycosylation type 2K (CDG2K), an autosomal recessive disorder with variable phenotypes (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9796'>Foulquier&nbsp;<em>et al.</em>2012</a>). Affected individuals show psychomotor and growth retardation, and most have short stature. Other features include dysmorphism, hypotonia, eye abnormalities, acquired microcephaly, hepatomegaly, and skeletal dysplasia. Congenital disorders of glycosylation are caused by a defect in glycoprotein biosynthesis and are characterized by under-glycosylated serum glycoproteins and a wide variety of clinical features. The broad spectrum of features may reflect the critical role of N-glycoproteins during embryonic development, differentiation, and maintenance of cell functions (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9796'>Foulquier&nbsp;<em>et al.</em>&nbsp;2012</a>).</p>
<p style='text-align: -webkit-auto;'>The Ca<sup>2+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup><sup>&nbsp;</sup><sup>&nbsp;</sup>Antiporter (CaCA2) Family (previous called the uncharacterized Protein Family 0016 (UPF0016), is well conserved throughout prokaryotes and eukaryotes. The yeast golgi Gcr1-dependent translation factor 1 protein (Gdt1p) contributes to Ca<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>homeostasis. A<em> yeast gdt1</em> mutant was found to be sensitive to high concentrations of Ca<sup>2+</sup>, and this sensitivity was suppressed by expression of human TMEM165 in yeast (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9797'>Demaegd&nbsp;<em>et al.</em>2013</a>). Patch-clamp analyses on human cells indicated that TMEM165 catalyzes Ca<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>transport, and defects in TMEM165 affected both Ca<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>and pH homeostasis.Gdt1p and TMEM165 are probably Golgi-localized Ca<sup>2+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup><sup> </sup><sup>&nbsp;</sup>antiporters. Modification of the Golgi Ca<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>and pH balance could explain the glycosylation defects observed in TMEM165-deficient patients.&nbsp;</p>
<p style='text-align: -webkit-auto;'>The UPF0016 family is defined by the presence of 1 or 2 copies of the
E-phi-G-D-[KR]-[TS] consensus motif in their transmembrane domain.&nbsp;<span id='ref34546425.28114750' class='cite'>Colinet <em>et al.</em> 2017</span> showed that 2 members of this
family, the human TMEM165 and the budding yeast Gdt1p, are functionally related Ca<sup>2+</sup> transporters. Mutations in TMEM165 cause a
type of rare human genetic diseases called Congenital Disorders of Glycosylation. Mutations in the yeast Golgi-localized Ca<sup>2+</sup> transporter,
Gdt1, revealed that acidic and polar uncharged residues of the consensus
motifs are involved in calcium tolerance and calcium transport activity. The E53 residue corresponds to the mutation in humans that triggers congenital disorders of
glycosylation (<span id='ref34546425.28114750' class='cite'>Colinet <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).</p>
<p style='text-align: -webkit-auto;'>The generalized reaction catalyzed by CaCA2 family members is:</p>
