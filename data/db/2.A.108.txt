<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>2.A.108 The Iron/Lead Transporter (ILT) Family</strong></p>
<p>The ILT family includes two families, the iron-transporting OFeT
 family and the lead-transporting PbrT family (see below). All sequenced
 homologues of the ILT family have been multiply aligned, and 
conserved motifs, including fully conserved acidic residues in putative 
transmembrane segments (TMSs) 1 and 4, previously implicated in heavy 
metal binding, have been identified (<span id='ref9864.16825785' class='cite'>Debut <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>).
 Topological analyses confirmed the presence of 7 conserved TMSs in a 3 +
 3 + 1 arrangement where the two 3 TMS elements are internally repeated.
 Phylogenetic analyses revealed the presence of several sequence 
divergent clusters of orthologous proteins that group roughly according 
to the phylogenes of the organisms of origin (<span id='ref9864.16825785' class='cite'>Debut <em>et al.</em>, 2006</span>).</p>
<p>Yeast (<em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em>, <em>Candida albicans</em> and <em>Schizosaccharomyces pombe</em>) and other fungi possess high affinity (Km &asymp;0.1 &micro;M) Fe<sup>2+</sup> uptake systems. These systems depend on cell surface ferroxidases to convert extracellular Fe<sup>2+</sup> to Fe<sup>3+ </sup>which
 can then be taken up either via a low affinity (30 &micro;M) transporter of 
the FeT family (TC #9.A.9) or a high affinity OFeT family transporter 
described here. Two gene products are required for high affinity Fe<sup>2+</sup> transport, Fet3p which is the oxidase, and Ftr1p which is the permease component. The oxidase is homologous to copper oxidases (CopD; TC# 1.B.76.1.4 and 9.B.62.1.5) that do not transport copper ions but may be required for copper uptake.</p>
<p>Fet3p of <em>S. cerevisiae</em> is a multicopper oxidase (636 amino 
acyl residues) which spans the plasma membrane once (residues 561-584) 
and has two multicopper oxidase domains (residues 121-141 and 483-494), 
which possess the ferroxidase activity on the external surface of the 
plasma membrane. It is a member of the multicopper oxidase family and is
 therefore homologous to laccase (benzenediol:oxygen oxidoreductase or 
ligninolytic phenol oxidase), as well as L-ascorbate oxidase, 
ceruloplasmin and dihydrogeodin oxidase. Its copper binding domain is 
homologous to that of the PcoA copper binding protein of <em>E. coli</em>.</p>
<p>Ftr1p is a protein of 404 amino acyl residues which may span the membrane seven times (<span id='ref9864.16825785' class='cite'>Debut <em>et al.</em>, 2006</span>).
 It exhibits homology with other yeast ORFs as well as algal, bacterial 
and archaeal ORFs. The bacterial and archaeal ORFs are highly divergent 
from the yeast proteins and may therefore serve dissimilar functions. 
Recently a bacterial iron transporter has been characterized from a 
marine magnetotactic &alpha;-proteobacterium (<span id='ref9865.15347752' class='cite'>Dubbels <em>et al.</em>, 2004</span>), but errors in the sequence precluded inclusion of this protein in TCDB.</p>
<p>Simultaneous expression of Fet3p and Ftr1p in yeast is required for 
proper localization of either protein at the cell surface, suggesting 
that a complex of the two proteins is formed. Both proteins are 
coordinately regulated, being expressed at high levels when iron is 
absent and repressed when iron is replete.</p>
<p>A group translocation reaction in which Fe<sup>2+</sup> is simultaneously oxidized and transported to Fe<sup>3+</sup> has been suggested but not demonstrated. Alternatively, Fe<sup>2+</sup> may be oxidized by Fet3p to Fe<sup>3+</sup> which may be passed from the Fet3p active site directly to the binding site for Fe<sup>3+</sup> in Ftr1. Still another possibility is that Fet3p functions only 
indirectly in transport by allowing membrane insertion, localization or 
stability of Ftr1p due to the formation of a complex between these two 
proteins. Regardless of these possibilities, it is not known if a 
channel or carrier mechanism operates, and the nature of the energy 
coupling process for transport is not established.&nbsp;</p>
<p>A dipartite iron uptake system, FetM (646 aas; 8 TMSs in a 1   7 
arrangement)/FetP (a periplasmic protein that enhances iron uptake by 
FetM) has been characterized (<a class='reflink' href='../../search/#ref11235'>Koch <em>et al.</em> 2011</a>).&nbsp; FetP binds Cu<sup>++</sup> and Mn<sup>++</sup> at two different sites, 1.3&nbsp;&Aring; apart, in this homodimeric protein.&nbsp; The 3-d structure with two Cu<sup>++</sup> bound to each of the two subunits revealed different geometries at the 
two sites. FetMP may be a iron permease with an iron scavenging 
function, and possibly also an iron reducing function (<a class='reflink' href='../../search/#ref11235'>Koch <em>et al.</em> 2011</a>).</p>
<p>The generalized transport reactions for the OFeT family are:</p>
<p style='text-align: center;'>(1) Fe<sup>3+</sup> (out) &rarr; Fe<sup>3+</sup> (in)</p>
<p style='text-align: center;'>(2) Fe<sup>2+</sup> (out) + 1/4 O<sub>2</sub> (out) &rarr; Fe<sup>3+</sup> (in) + 1/2 H<sub>2</sub>O (out)</p>
<p>A single protein, PbrT, encoded within the lead resistance locus of <em>Ralstonia metallidurans</em> CH34, serves as the prototype for the PbrT family. This protein, when overexpressed, increases sensitivity to Pb<sup>2+</sup>.
 The protein exhibits a single N-terminal hydrophobic segment (a 
putative TMS), plus 6 additional putative TMSs in the C-terminal region 
(residues 420-650) of this 652 aas protein (<span id='ref9921.16825785' class='cite'>Debut <em>et al.</em>, 2006</span>).
 An N-terminal region (residues 100-218) shows sequence similarity to 
the C-terminal cytochrome C6 domain of the diheme c-type cytochrome, 
FixP, of <em>Azorhizobium caulinodans</em> (30% identity). The C-terminal 
transmembrane domain (residues 223-619) shows sequence similarity to 
members of the oxidase-dependent Fe2+ transporter, OFeT, family (TC 
#9.A.10) including the Ftr1 iron transporter of <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em> (TC #9.A.10.1.1) (30% identity). Thus, PbrT is related to the OFeT 
family, both structurally and functionally. An N-terminal domain 
(residues 100-218 in the <em>R. metallidurans</em> protein) shows similarity to the C-terminal cytochrome C6 domain in the diheme c-type cytochrome, FixP of <em>Azorhizobium caulinodans</em> (<span id='ref9921.16825785' class='cite'>Debut <em>et al.</em>, 2006</span>). We designate the family including both the OFeT and PbrT families the Fe/Pb-Transporter (ILT) superfamily.</p>
<p>Some bacteria have Fe<sup>2+</sup> transporters that resemble PbrT 
more than members of the OFeT family. Members of both the OFeT and PbrT 
families contain two REXXE motifs in their TMSs. In EfeU of <em>E. coli</em> (TC# 9.A.10.2.3) the first motif is required for Fe<sup>2+</sup> transport (<span id='ref9923.16987175' class='cite'>Grosse <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>).</p>
