<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>2.A.109 The Tellurium Ion Resistance (TerC) Family</strong></p>
<p style='text-align: left;'>The TerC family (Pfam 03741) includes the <em>E. coli</em> TerC protein which has been implicated in tellurium resistance (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9881'>Burian <em>et al.</em> 1998</a>). It is hypothesized to catalyze efflux of tellurium ions (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9881'>Burian <em>et al.</em>, 1998</a>; <a class='reflink' href='#ref9882'>Kormutakova <em>et al.</em> 2000</a>). TerC is encoded by plasmid pTE53 from a clinical isolate of <em>E. coli</em> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9881'>Burian <em>et al.</em>, 1998</a>). It has 346 aas and 9 putative TMSs with a large hydrophilic loop between TMSs 5 and 6. A homologue in <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> (TC# 9.A.30.2.1) may function in prothylakoid membrane biogenises during early chloroplast development (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9883'>Kwon and Cho 2008</a>). It has 384 aas and 7-8 putative TMSs.  In <em>E. coli</em>, TerC forms a membrane complex with TerB as well as DctA, PspA, HslU, and RplK. The TerB/TerC complex may link different
functional modules with biochemical activities of C4-dicarboxylate transport, inner membrane stress
response (phage shock protein regulatory complex), ATPase/chaperone activity, and proteosynthesis (<span id='ref34545022.26778143' class='cite'>Turkovicova <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>). It may be part of a metal sensing stress response system (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9884'>Anantharaman <em>et al.</em> 2012</a>).  The co-presence of TerC and TerE but not TerF correlates with tellurite resistance when several hundred bacterial strains were assayed (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9885'>Orth <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). Some of these proteins have C-terminal CBS domains.</p>
