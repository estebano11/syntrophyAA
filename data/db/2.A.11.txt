<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.11 The Citrate-Mg<sup>2+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup>, CitM, Citrate-Ca<sup>2+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup>, CitH, Symporter (CitMHS) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong></strong> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The two initially characterized members of the CitMHS family are both citrate uptake permeases from <em>Bacillus subtilis.</em> CitM is believed to transport a citrate<sup>2-</sup></span>-Mg<sup>2+</sup> complex in symport with one H<sup>+</sup> per Mg<sup>2+</sup>-citrate while CitH apparently transports a citrate<sup>2-</sup>-Ca<sup>2+</sup> complex in symport with protons (<span id='ref761.8892821' class='cite'>Boorsma <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>; <span id='ref763.11053381' class='cite'>Krom <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  The cation specificity of CitM is: Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Ba<sup>2+</sup>, Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Zn<sup>2+</sup> with an order of preference in this order. However,&nbsp;<span id='ref34557244.37715733' class='cite'>Panchal <em>et al.</em> 2023</span> have concluded that there are three types of CitMHS transporters, oall that transports citate, but one preferentially with Mg<sup>2+</sup>, one with Ca<sup>2+</sup>, and one with Fe<sup>2+</sup>.&nbsp; CitM is highly specific for citrate and D-isocitrate and does not transport other di- and tri-carboxylates including succinate, L-isocitrate, cis-aconitate and tricarballylate (<span id='ref1738.11891560' class='cite'>Li and Pajor, 2002</span>; <span id='ref358.12427932' class='cite'>Warner and Lolkema, 2002</span>). For CitH, the cation specificity (in order of preference) is: Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Ba<sup>2+</sup> and Sr<sup>2+</sup> (<span id='ref763.11053381' class='cite'>Krom <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  The two proteins are 60% identical, contain about 400 amino acyl residues and possess twelve putative transmembrane spanners. A CitM homologue in <em>S. mutans</em> transports citrate conjugated to Fe<sup>2+</sup> or Mn<sup>2+</sup> but not Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> or Ni<sup>2+</sup> (<span id='ref3120.15968054' class='cite'>Korithoski <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>).</p>
<p>The CitMHS family belongs within the IT superfamily (<span id='ref3121.14643936' class='cite'>Prakash <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>; <span id='ref762.10627041' class='cite'>Rabus <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).  Members of this family are found in Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes.  These proteins all probably arose by an internal gene duplication event. Lensbouer &amp; Doyle (<span id='ref7522.20735204' class='cite'>2010</span>) have reviewed these systems. They classify the porters with three superfamilies, according to ion-preference: 1) Mg<sup>2+</sup>-preferring, 2) Ca<sup>2+</sup>-preferring, and 3) Fe<sup>2+</sup>-preferring . These authors provide information about transcriptional control, putative structure, predicted
 family members, members characterized to date and potential use in 
bioremediation.</p>
<p>The transport reactions catalyzed by (1) CitM and (2) CitH, and (3) other members of the family, respectively, are:</p>
<p align='center'>(1)  Citrate &bull;&nbsp;Mg<sup>2+</sup> (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out)  ⇌&nbsp;Citrate  &bull;&nbsp;Mg<sup>2+</sup> (in) + nH<sup>+</sup> (in)</p>
<p align='center'>(2)  Citrate (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out)  ⇌&nbsp;Citrate (in) + nH<sup>+</sup></p>
<p align='center'>(3) Citrate &bull;&nbsp;Ca<sup>2+</sup> (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out)  ⇌&nbsp;Citrate &bull;&nbsp;Ca<sup>2+</sup> (in) + nH<sup>+</sup> (in)</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><HR>This family belongs to the <A HREF="superfamily.php?id=1">IT Superfamily</A>.<BR><HR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Boorsma, A., M.E. van der Rest, J.S.
