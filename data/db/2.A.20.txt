<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong>2.A.20  The Inorganic Phosphate Transporter (PiT) Family</strong></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>The proteins of the PiT family are derived from Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria, archaea, and eukaryotes.  Functionally-characterized members of the family appear to catalyze inorganic phosphate (P<sub>i</sub></span>) or inorganic sulfate uptake either by H<sup>+</sup> or Na<sup>+</sup> symport.  Both PitA and PitB of <em>E. coli</em> probably catalyze metal ion&middot;phosphate:H<sup>+ </sup> symport, where Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup> or Zn<sup>2+</sup> (and probably other divalent cations) can complex P<sub>i</sub>.  The mammalian proteins have been reported to function as viral receptors, but they undoubtedly function as transport proteins as well.&nbsp; For numerous gammaretroviruses, such as the gibbon ape leukemia virus, woolly monkey virus, feline
leukemia virus subgroup B, feline leukemia virus subgroup T, and 10A1 murine leukemia virus, this
receptor is the human type III sodium-dependent inorganic phosphate transporter, SLC20A1, also
known as PiT1 (<span id='ref11380.19717569' class='cite'>Farrell <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>). Members of this family have the PiT fold (<span id='ref34555317.36164651' class='cite'>Ferrada and Superti-Furga 2022</span>).</p>
<p>The molecular sizes of Pit family members are reported to vary from 354 to 681 residues (10-12 TMSs) with the mammalian and <em>Plasmodium</em> proteins exhibiting the largest sizes.  The sulfate permease of <em>B. subtilis</em>, CysP, is of 354 residues with 11 putative TMSs (Mansilla and de <span id='ref850.10784039' class='cite'>Mendoza, 2000</span>).</p>
<p>Phylogenetic grouping of the phosphate transport proteins generally correlates with organismal phylogeny.  Thus the fungal, plant, animal and archaeal proteins each cluster separately (<span id='ref849.10082980' class='cite'>Saier <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).  However, the tree exhibits two clusters of bacterial phosphate transport proteins.  One bacterial cluster is distant from the eukaryotic proteins while the other cluster is close to the plant proteins.  Both clusters include proteins from Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria.  The sulfate permease, CysP, is distantly related to the phosphate permeases.</p>
<p>Members of the PiT family arose by a tandem internal gene duplication event.  Surprisingly, TopPred predicts a 12 TMS topology for the yeast Pho89 protein, but the homologous regions are not predicted to show similar topological features.  Thus, for example TMS 1 is homologous to TMS 9, and TMS 4 is predicted to correspond to the loop between TMSs 11 and 12 (<span id='ref846.9693717' class='cite'>Persson <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>, <span id='ref848.10548719' class='cite'>1999</span>).</p>
<p>The malaria parasite, <em>Plasmodium falciparum</em>, grows within its host erythrocyte and induces an increase in the permeability of the erythrocyte membrane to a range of solutes including Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup>.  This results in a progressive increase in the concentration of Na<sup>+</sup> in the erythrocyte cytosol.  The parasite cytosol has a relatively low Na<sup>+</sup> concentration, generating a large inward Na<sup>+</sup> gradient across the parasite plasma membrane.  Saliba et al. (<span id='ref3643.17006451' class='cite'>2006</span>) showed that the parasite exploits the Na<sup>+</sup> electrochemical gradient to energize the uptake of inorganic phosphate (P<sub>i</sub>) with a stoichiometry of 2Na<sup>+</sup>:1P<sub>i</sub> and with an apparent preference for the monovalent over the divalent form of P<sub>i</sub> (see TC #2.A.20.2.5).</p>
