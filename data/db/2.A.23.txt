<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong> 2.A.23  The Dicarboxylate/Amino Acid:Cation (Na<sup>+</sup><strong> or H<sup>+</sup><strong>) Symporter (DAACS) Family</strong></strong></strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The members of the DAACS family catalyze Na<sup>+</sup></span> and/or H<sup>+</sup> symport together with (a) a Krebs cycle dicarboxylate (malate, succinate, or fumarate), (b) a dicarboxylic amino acid (glutamate or aspartate), (c) a small, semipolar, neutral amino acid (Ala, Ser, Cys, Thr), (d) both neutral and acidic amino acids or (e) most zwitterionic and dibasic amino acids.  The bacterial members are of about 450 (420-491) amino acyl residues while the mammalian proteins are of about 550 (503-574) residues in length.  These proteins possess between ten and twelve hydrophobic segments per polypeptide chain.  Two of them, human EAAT2 (TC #2.A.23.2.2) and <em>E. coli</em> GltP (TC #2.A.23.1.1) have been shown to be homotrimers (<span id='ref3017.15265858' class='cite'>Gendreau <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).  A specific topological model in which 7 &alpha;-helical TMSs are followed by a reentrant loop-pore structure followed by one final TMS is presented in Slotboom et al. (<span id='ref884.10357852' class='cite'>1999</span>) and Leighton et al. (<span id='ref2227.12015317' class='cite'>2002</span>).  Possibly, the transporter consists of eight TMSs, and one or two pore-loop structures that dip into the membrane (one between TMSs 6 and 7, the other between TMSs 7 and 8) in a fashion reminiscent of pore-loop structures found in VIC family ion channels (TC#1.A.1) (<span id='ref2226.11994293' class='cite'>Grunewald <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).&nbsp; This family of transporters has been reviewed (<span id='ref10441.24240778' class='cite'>Grewer <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). Functional up-regulation of GluTs may provide a pharmacotherapeutic approach for the management of chronic pain using pyridazine derivatives and beta-lactams (<span id='ref34549738.31047920' class='cite'>Gegelashvili and Bjerrum 2019</span>). VGLUTs (TC# 2.A.1.14) and EAATs (TC# 2.A.23.2) may be targets for the treatment of Parkinson's Disease (PD). VGLUTs
 and EAATs can be used as clinical drug targets to achieve better 
efficacy (<span id='ref34553834.34220434' class='cite'>Li <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>All of the bacterial proteins cluster together on the phylogenetic tree as do the mammalian proteins.  The mammalian permeases that transport neutral amino acids cluster separately from those that are specific for the acidic amino acids.  Among the mammalian proteins are neuronal excitatory amino acid neurotransmitter permeases.  One of these (the GLT-1 L-glutamate/L-aspartate/D-aspartate transporter) has been shown to cotransport the neurotransmitter with 3 Na<sup>+</sup> and 1 H<sup>+</sup> and to countertransport 1 K<sup>+</sup>.  The EAAT3 carrier (also called the EAAC1 carrier) uses Arg-447 to bind dicarboxylic amino acids in the presence of K<sup>+</sup> but not monocarboxylic amino acids (<span id='ref887.10978338' class='cite'>Bendahan <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>). Larsson et al. (<span id='ref7236.20634426' class='cite'>2010</span>) have identified the 3rd Na<sup>+</sup> binding site and provided evidence for the mechanism of transport. Glutamate and Na<sup>+</sup> binding activates an uncoupled chloride conductance in EAAT proteins, showing that they can function both as carriers and channels, and the two functions may arise from separate transmembrane domains (<span id='ref34550396.15730426' class='cite'>Ryan and Vandenberg 2006</span>).</p>
<p>Some members of the DAACS family from animals, such as EAAT1, EAAT2, EAAT3 and EAAT4, can apparently be induced to function in a 'channel mode' wherein the transporter allows ion passage without being coupled to substrate translocation.  This effect may involve a chloride-permeable, anion-selective channel.  Some evidence suggests that the N- and C-termini of EAAT3 as well as two histidyl residues (in EAAT4) in the extracellular loop between TMSs 3 and 4 play a role in conversion to the channel mode (<span id='ref885.11030592' class='cite'>Li <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  The loop between TMSs 3 and 4 functions to allow regulation of this current by Zn<sup>2+</sup> (<span id='ref1946.11352900' class='cite'>Mitrovic <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  Distinct conformational states mediate carrier versus channel function, and a dynamic equilibrium exists between the two forms (<span id='ref2225.11823462' class='cite'>Borre <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>; <span id='ref2228.11815608' class='cite'>Ryan <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  It is possible to isolate anion permeability mutants in TMS2 that show no change in glutamate transport (<span id='ref2836.14982939' class='cite'>Ryan <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).  EAAT4 but not EAAT2 anion channels display voltage-dependent gating that is modified by glutamate (<span id='ref810.14506254' class='cite'>Melzer <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  Possibly the channel activity is related to their trimeric structures (<span id='ref3017.15265858' class='cite'>Gendreau <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>). Torres-Salazar and Fahlke (<span id='ref4776.17908688' class='cite'>2007</span>) have reported that neuronal glutamate transporters (EAATs) vary in substrate transport rate but not in unitary anion channel conductance.</p>
<p>The 3-D structure of a member of the DAACS family has been determined (<span id='ref3858.17230192' class='cite'>Boudker <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>; <span id='ref3018.15483603' class='cite'>Yernool <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>) (see 2.A.23.1.5).  The putative transporter is a bowl-shaped trimer with a solvent-filled extracellular basin extending halfway across the membrane bilayer.  Each protomer harbors 8 TMSs and two reentrant helical hairpins.  At the bottom of the basin are three independent binding sites, each cradled by two helical hairpins, reaching from opposite sides of the membrane.  There are 3 independent translocation pathways. The first six transmembrane segments form a distorted 'amino-terminal cylinder' and provide all interprotomer contacts, whereas transmembrane segments TM7 and TM8, together with hairpins HP1 and HP2, coalesce to form a highly conserved core within the amino-terminal cylinder.  It is proposed that transport of aspartate or glutamate is achieved by movements of the hairpins that allow alternating access to either side of the membrane.  Helical hairpin 2 is the extracellular gate that controls access of aspartate and the ions to the internal binding site (<span id='ref3858.17230192' class='cite'>Boudker <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Molecular simulations have provided evidence for the substrate translocation pathway (<span id='ref6551.19202063' class='cite'>Gu <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The central cavity in trimeric glutamate transporters restricts ligand diffusion (<span id='ref8303.21873219' class='cite'>Leary <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Excitatory amino acid transporters (EAATs) are essential for terminating
 glutamatergic synaptic transmission. They are not only coupled 
glutamate/Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> transporters but also function as 
anion-selective channels. EAAT anion channels regulate neuronal 
excitability, and gain-of-function mutations in these proteins result in
 ataxia and epilepsy.&nbsp;<span id='ref34544883.25635461' class='cite'>Machtens <em>et al.</em> 2015</span> examined the prokaryotic homolog GltPh (TC# 2.A.23.1.5) and mammalian EAATs to determine how these 
transporters conduct anions. Whereas outward- and inward-facing GltPh 
conformations are nonconductive, lateral movement of the glutamate 
transport domain from intermediate transporter conformations results in 
formation of an anion-selective conduction pathway. Entry of anions 
into this pathway, and mutations of homologous pore-forming residues had
 analogous effects on GltPh simulations and EAAT2/EAAT4 measurements of 
single-channel currents and anion/cation selectivities. These findings 
provide a mechanistic framework of how neurotransmitter transporters can
 operate as anion-selective and ligand-gated ion channels (<span id='ref34544883.25635461' class='cite'>Machtens <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).</p>
<p>As noted above, EAATs 
couple the transport of glutamate to the co-transport of three Na<sup>+</sup> ions and one H<sup>+</sup> ion into the cell, and the counter-transport of one 
K<sup>+</sup> ion out of the cell. The <span class='highlight'>EAAT</span> Cl<sup>-</sup> channel is activated by the binding of glutamate and Na<sup>+</sup>, but 
is thermodynamically uncoupled from glutamate transport and involves 
molecular determinants distinct from those responsible for glutamate 
transport (<span id='ref34549602.30802031' class='cite'>Qu <em>et al.</em> 2019</span>).</p>
<address> Several O-Benzylated l-threo-beta-hydroxyaspartate derivatives have been developed as highly potent inhibitors of EAATs
with TFB-TBOA ((2S,3S)-2-amino 3-((3-(4-(trifluoromethyl)benzamido)benzyl)oxy)succinic acid)
standing out as a low-nanomolar inhibitor<span id='ref34545136.26918289' class='cite'> (Leuenberger <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>). </address>
