<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.37  The Monovalent Cation:Proton Antiporter-2 (CPA2) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The CPA2 family is a moderately large from bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes.  Among the functionally well-characterized members of the family are (1) the KefB/KefC K<sup>+</sup></span> efflux proteins of <em>E. coli</em> which may be capable of catalyzing both K<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiport and K<sup>+</sup> uniport, depending on conditions (<span id='ref1014.2957359' class='cite'>Bakker <em>et al</em>., 1987</span>; <span id='ref1020.' class='cite'>Booth <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>; <span id='ref1015.2046548' class='cite'>Munro <em>et al</em>., 1991</span>), (2) the Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporter of <em>Enterococcus hirae</em> (<span id='ref1017.1312090' class='cite'>Waser <em>et al</em>., 1992</span>) and (3) the K<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporter of <em>S. cerevisiae.</em> It has been proposed that under normal physiological conditions, these proteins may function by essentially the same mechanism (<span id='ref1016.1325937' class='cite'>Reizer <em>et al</em>., 1992</span>).</p>
<p>KefC and KefB of <em>E. coli</em> are responsible for glutathione-gated K<sup>+</sup> efflux (<span id='ref1018.7934942' class='cite'>Ferguson <em>et al</em>., 1993</span>, <span id='ref1024.9023177' class='cite'>1997</span>).  Each of these proteins consists of a transmembrane hydrophobic N-terminal domain, and a less well-conserved C-terminal hydrophilic domain.  Each protein interacts with a second protein encoded by genes that overlap the gene encoding the primary transporter.  The KefC ancillary protein is YabF while the KefB ancillary protein is YheR.  These ancillary proteins stimulate transport activity about 10-fold (<span id='ref1031.11053405' class='cite'>Miller <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  These proteins are important for cell survival during exposure to toxic metabolites, possibly because they can release K<sup>+</sup>, allowing H<sup>+</sup> uptake.  Activation of the KefB or KefC K<sup>+</sup> efflux system only occurs in the presence of glutathione and a reactive electrophile such as methylglyoxal or N-ethylmaleimide.  Formation of the methylglyoxal-glutathione conjugate, S-lactoylglutathione, is catalyzed by glyoxalase I, and S-lactoylglutathione activates KefB and KefC (Mac<span id='ref1027.9489668' class='cite'>Lean <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>).  H<sup>+</sup> uptake (acidification of the cytoplasm) accompanying or following K<sup>+</sup> efflux may serve as a further protective mechanism against electrophile toxicity (<span id='ref1020.' class='cite'>Booth <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>; <span id='ref1018.7934942' class='cite'>Ferguson <em>et al</em>., 1993</span>, <span id='ref1024.9023177' class='cite'>1997</span>; <span id='ref1021.' class='cite'>Stumpe <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>). Inhibition of transport by glutathione was enhanced by NADH (<span id='ref5459.17679694' class='cite'>Fujisawa <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).</p>
<p>Gram negative bacteria are protected against toxic electrophilic compounds by glutathione-gated potassium efflux systems (Kef) that modulate cytoplasmic pH. Roosild et al. (<span id='ref7633.21041667' class='cite'>2010</span>) have elucidated the mechanism of gating through structural and functional analysis of the&nbsp;<em>E. coli</em> KefC. The revealed mechanism can explain how subtle chemical differences in glutathione derivatives can produce opposite effects on channel function. Kef channels are regulated by potassium transport and NAD-binding (KTN) domains that sense both reduced glutathione, which inhibits Kef activity, and glutathione adducts that form during electrophile detoxification and activate Kef. Roosild et al. (<span id='ref7633.21041667' class='cite'>2010</span>) found that reduced glutathione stabilizes an interdomain association between two KTN folds, whereas large adducts sterically disrupt this interaction. F441 is identified as the pivotal residue discriminating between reduced glutathione and its conjugates. They demonstrated a major structural change on the binding of an activating ligand to a KTN-domain protein.</p>
<p>The MagA protein of <em>Magnetospirillum</em> sp. strain AMB-1 has been reported to be required for synthesis of bacterial magnetic particles.  The <em>magA</em> gene is subject to transcriptional activation by an iron deficiency (<span id='ref1019.8537318' class='cite'>Nakamura <em>et al</em>., 1995</span>).&nbsp; However, are more recent report has shown that <em>magA</em> mutants of both Magnetospirillum magneticum AMB-1 and M. gryphiswaldense MSR-1 formed wild-type-like magnetosomes without a growth defect (<span id='ref9161.22194451' class='cite'>Uebe <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). Its transport function is not known.  The GerN and GrmA proteins of <em>Bacillus cereus</em> and <em>Bacillus megaterium</em>, respectively, are spore germination proteins that can exchange Na<sup>+</sup> for H<sup>+</sup> and/or K<sup>+</sup> (<span id='ref1022.11566988' class='cite'>Southworth <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  The AmhT homologue of <em>Bacillus pseudofirmus</em> transports both K<sup>+</sup> and NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, influences ammonium homeostasis, and is required for normal sporulation and germination.  The identification of these proteins as members of the CPA2 family reveals that monovalent cation transport is required for <em>Bacillus</em> spore formation and germination (<span id='ref1023.8867604' class='cite'>Tani <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>).</p>
<p>The proteins of the CPA2 family consist of between 333 and 900 amino acyl residues.  They exhibit 10-14 transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs).  Several organisms possess multiple CPA2 paralogues.  Thus, <em>E. coli</em> has three, <em>Methanococcus jannaschii</em> has four and <em>Synechocystis</em> sp. has five paralogues. The potassium efflux system, Kef, protects bacteria against the 
detrimental effects of electrophilic compounds via acidification of the 
cytoplasm. Kef is inhibited by glutathione (GSH) but activated by 
glutathione-S-conjugates (GS-X) formed in the presence of electrophiles.
 GSH and GS-X bind to overlapping sites on Kef, which are located in a 
cytosolic regulatory domain (<span id='ref11174.24601535' class='cite'>Healy <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).</p>
<p>Interestingly, this family proliferated in plants such as <em>Araidopsis thaliana </em>where there are 28 members that may function in pollen development, germination, and tube growth (<span id='ref34549244.15347787' class='cite'>Sze <em>et al.</em> 2004</span>), but they are rarely found in metazoans (<span id='ref6836.16607029' class='cite'>Bock <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>). These plant proteins probably arose from their bacterial homologues (<span id='ref34549243.22639643' class='cite'>Chanroj <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>) and privide functions such as osmotic adjustment and K<sup>+</sup> homeostasis during pollen development (<span id='ref34549244.15347787' class='cite'>Sze <em>et al.</em> 2004</span>). These family members show very similar 3-D folds to CPA1 family members, and this structure is called the NhaA-fold (<span id='ref34549245.27179641' class='cite'>Czerny <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).  H<sup>+</sup> pumps, counter-ion fluxes, and cation/H<sup>+</sup> antiporters, including CPA2 family members, are probably interlinked with signaling and membrane trafficking to remodel membranes and cell walls (<span id='ref34549246.29691301' class='cite'>Sze and Chanroj 2018</span>).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction catalyzed by members of the CPA2 family is:</p>
<p align='center'>M<sup>+</sup> (in) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out) ⇌&nbsp;M<sup>+</sup> (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (in).</p>
<p align='center'>(The carrier-mediated mode)</p>
<p>Some members may also catalyze:</p>
<p align='center'>M<sup>+</sup> (in) ⇌&nbsp;M<sup>+</sup> (out).</p>
