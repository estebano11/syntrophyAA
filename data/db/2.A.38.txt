<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong> 2.A.38  The K<sup>+</sup><strong> Transporter (Trk) Family </strong></strong></p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p>The proteins of the Trk family are derived from Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria, yeast and plants.  The proteins of <em>E. coli</em> K12 (TrkH and TrkG) as well as several yeast and plant proteins have been functionally and topologically characterized.  While TrkH is generally present in <em>E. coli</em> and other enteric bacteria, TrkG is encoded by a foreign gene located within the prophage <em>rac</em> region of the <em>E. coli</em> K12 chromosome.  TrkG is not present in several other <em>E. coli</em> strains.  The sizes of the Trk family members vary from 423 residues to 1235 residues.  The bacterial proteins are of 423-558 residues, the <em>Triticum aestivum</em> protein is 533 residues, and the yeast proteins vary between 841 and 1241 residues.  These proteins possess 8 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners.  An 8 TMS topology with N- and C-termini on the inside, has been established for AtHKT1 of <em>A. thaliana</em> (<span id='ref1969.11344270' class='cite'>Kato <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>) and Trk2 of <em>S. cerevisiae</em> (<span id='ref2661.14570869' class='cite'>Zeng <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).  This folding pattern resembles quadruplicated primitive K<sup>+</sup> channels of the VIC superfamily (TC #1.A.1) instead of typical 12 TMS carriers (<span id='ref7988.15459199' class='cite'>Matsuda <em>et al.</em>, 2004</span>).  As homology has been established between Trk carriers and VIC family channels, the latter were presumably the precursors of the former.</p>
<p>The phylogenetic tree reveals that the proteins cluster according to phylogeny of the source organism with (1) the Gram-negative bacterial Trk proteins, (2) the Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacterial Ktr proteins, (3) the yeast proteins and (4) the plant proteins comprising four distinct clusters (<span id='ref1039.10082980' class='cite'>Saier <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>). <em>S. cerevisiae</em> possesses two paralogues, high- and low-affinity K<sup>+</sup> transporters.</p>
<p>The KtrAB system of <em>Vibrio alginolyticus</em> consists of a cytoplasmic ATP-binding regulatory subunit, KtrA (<span id='ref3908.17344221' class='cite'>Kroning <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>), shared by several other K<sup>+</sup> transporters and channels including an integral membrane protein, KtrB, which is very similar to NtpJ of <em>E. hirae</em> and distantly related to TrkH of <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref1037.9642210' class='cite'>Nakamura <em>et al</em>., 1998b</span>). KtrA binds ATP to a &beta;-&alpha;-&beta;-Rossman fold. Other nucleotides bound, but only ATP bound with high affinity and changed the conformation of KtrA so that KtrA and KtrB associate (<span id='ref3908.17344221' class='cite'>Kroning <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>) <em>in vivo</em>, both ATP and &Delta;&Psi; were required for transport activity.   All of these systems may be multicomponent.   However, the integral membrane constituents have been proposed to exhibit a basic structure of four consecutive M1-P-loop-M2 motifs analogous to the KcsA K<sup>+</sup> channel of <em>Streptomyces lividans</em> (see TC #1.A.1).  Supporting this model, modification of the four putative selectivity filter glycine residues (one in each P-loop) in KtrB of <em>V. alginolyticus</em> altered the K<sup>+</sup> affinity (100 fold) and selectivity, altering the K<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> selectivity (<span id='ref3242.16210320' class='cite'>Tholema <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>).  Two homologues of the <em>V. alginolyticus</em> KtrAB are found in <em>B. subtilis.</em> Both systems catalyze K<sup>+</sup> uptake but with differing Kms: 1 mM for KtrAB and 10 mM for KtrCD (<span id='ref2330.12562800' class='cite'>Holtmann <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).</p>
<p>The <em>E. coli</em> TrkH and TrkG proteins are complexed to two peripheral membrane proteins, TrkA, the ATP/NAD<sup>+</sup>-binding protein, and TrkE, an ATP-binding protein.  The peripheral membrane proteins are thought to function in regulation rather than energy coupling.  TrkE maps to the <em>sapABCDF</em> operon which encodes an ABC transporter (TC #3.A.1.5.5), and the SapD ATP binding cassette (ABC) protein of <em>E. coli</em> can stimulate K<sup>+</sup> uptake via either Trk<sup>H</sup> or Trk<sup>G</sup> (<span id='ref2052.11700350' class='cite'>Harms <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  Therefore, SapD is probably TrkE.  ATP binding to SapD, rather than ATP hydrolysis, appears to activate.  Thus, the pmf drives transport while ATP binding activates transport.  At least one other ATP-activating protein is probably present in the <em>E. coli</em> cell (<span id='ref2052.11700350' class='cite'>Harms <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>TrkA family members regulate various K<sup>+</sup></span> transporters in all three domains of life.  These regulatory subunits are generally called K<sup>+</sup> transport/nucleotide binding subunits (<span id='ref3296.10592242' class='cite'>Bateman <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  Systems regulated include TrkG/H (TC #2.A.38.1.1) regulated by TrkA (with two KTN domains) and KtrB (TC #2.A.38.4.2) regulated by KtrA (<span id='ref3297.12086676' class='cite'>Roosild <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  In addition, homologous regulatory domains are sometimes incorporated into the transporter polypeptide chains as in the case of the KefC and KefB proteins of <em>E. coli</em> (TC #2.A.37.1.1 and 2.A.37.1.2, respectively).</p>
<p>TrkA domains can bind NAD<sup>+</sup> and NADH, possibly allowing K<sup>+</sup> transporters to be responsive to the redox state of the cell.  The ratio of NADH/NAD<sup>+</sup> may control gating.  Multiple crystal structures of two KTN domains complexed with NAD<sup>+</sup> or NADH reveal that these ligands control the oligomeric (tetrameric) state of KTN.  The results suggest that KTN is inherently flexible, undergoing a large conformational change through a hinge motion (<span id='ref3297.12086676' class='cite'>Roosild <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  The KTN domains of Kef channels interact dynamically with the transporter.  The KTN conformation then controls permease activity (<span id='ref3297.12086676' class='cite'>Roosild <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Both yeast transport systems are believed to function by K<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> symport, but the wheat protein functions by K<sup>+</sup>:Na<sup>+</sup> symport.  It is possible that some of these proteins can function by a channel-type mechanism. Positively charged residues in TMS8 of several ktr/Trk/HKT transporters probably face the channel and block a conformational change that is essential for channel activity while allowing secondary active transport (<span id='ref7987.18690031' class='cite'>Kato <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>).</p>
<p>KtrB is the K<sup>+</sup>-translocating subunit of the K<sup>+</sup>-uptake system KtrAB. It is a member of the superfamily of K<sup>+</sup>transporters (SKT proteins) with other sub-families occurring in archaea, bacteria, fungi, plants and trypanosomes (<span id='ref8590.21680052' class='cite'>H&auml;nelt <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). SKT proteins may have originated from small K<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;channels by at least two gene duplication and two gene fusion events. They contain four covalently linked M(1)PM(2) domains, in which M(1) and M(2) stand for transmembrane stretches, and P for a P-loop, which folds back from the external medium into the membrane. SKT proteins distinguish themselves in two important aspects from K<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;channels: first, with just one conserved glycine residue in their P-loops they contain a much simpler K<sup>+</sup>-selectivity filter sequence than K<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;channels with their conserved Thr-Val-Gly-Tyr-Gly sequence. Secondly, the middle part M(2C2) from the long transmembrane stretch M(2C) of KtrB from the bacterium<em> Vibrio alginolyticus</em> forms a gate inside the membrane, which prevents K<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;permeation to the cytoplasm. H&auml;nelt et al. (<span id='ref8590.21680052' class='cite'>2011</span>) discuss the mechanism of K<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;transport via KtrB and other SKT proteins and how the KtrA protein regulates the transport activity.</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction catalyzed by the Trk family members is therefore probably:</p>
