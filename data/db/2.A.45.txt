<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.45  The Arsenite-Antimonite (ArsB) Efflux Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Arsenite resistance (Ars) efflux pumps of bacteria consist either of two proteins (ArsB, the integral membrane constituent with twelve transmembrane spanners), and ArsA (the ATP-hydrolyzing, transport energizing subunit, as for the chromosomally-encoded <em>E. coli</em> system), or of one protein (the ArsB integral membrane protein of the plasmid-encoded <em>Staphylococcus</em> system) (<span id='ref2019.10498699' class='cite'>Rensing <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>; <span id='ref2023.10730185' class='cite'>Rosen, 1996</span>; <span id='ref2022.9504403' class='cite'>Xu <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>).  ArsA proteins have two ATP binding domains and probably arose by a tandem gene duplication event. ArsB proteins all possess twelve transmembrane spanners and may also have arisen by a tandem gene duplication event.  Structurally, the Ars pumps resemble ABC-type efflux pumps, but there is no significant sequence similarity between the Ars and ABC pumps.  When only ArsB is present, the system operates by a pmf-dependent mechanism, and consequently belongs in TC subclass 2.A.  When ArsA is also present, ATP hydrolysis drives efflux, and consequently the system belongs in TC subclass 3.A.  ArsB therefore appears twice in the TC system but ArsA appears only once.  These pumps actively expel both arsenite and antimonite. </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Homologues of ArsB are found in Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria as well as cyanobacteria, and several paralogues are sometimes found in a single organism.   Homologues are also found in archaea and eukarya.  Among the distant homologues found in eukaryotes are members of the DASS family (TC #2.A.47) including the rat renal Na<sup>+</sup></span>:sulfate cotransporter (spQ07782) and the human renal Na<sup>+</sup>:dicarboxylate cotransporter (gbU26209).  ArsB proteins are therefore members of a superfamily (called the IT (ion transporter) superfamily) (<span id='ref2878.14643936' class='cite'>Prakash <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>; <span id='ref2018.10627041' class='cite'>Rabus <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).  However, ArsB has uniquely gained the ability to function in conjunction with ArsA in order to couple ATP hydrolysis to anion efflux.</p>
<p>A unique member of the ArsB family is the rice silicon (silicate) efflux pump, Lsi2 (2.A.45.2.4). The silicon uptake systems, Lsi1 (1.A.8.12.2), and Lsi2 are expressed in roots, on the plasma membranes of cells in both the exodermis and the endodermis. In contrast to Lsi1, which is localized on the distal side, Lsi2 is localized on the proximal side of the same cells. Thus these cells have an influx transporter on one side and an efflux transporter on the other side of the cell to permit the effective transcellular transport of the nutrient.</p>
<p>ArsA homologues are found in bacteria, archaea and eukarya (both animals and plants), but there are far fewer of them in the databases than ArsB proteins, suggesting that many ArsB homologues function by a pmf-dependent mechanism, probably an arsenite:H<sup>+</sup> antiport mechanism (<span id='ref2877.14970228' class='cite'>Meng <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>). ArsA proteins are homologous to nitrogenase iron (NifH) proteins 2 of bacteria and to protochlorophyllide reductase iron sulfur ATP-binding proteins of cyanobacteria, algae and plants.</p>
