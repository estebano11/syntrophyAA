<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.46  The Benzoate:H</strong></span><strong><sup><span style='font-family: Times; font-size: x-small;'> </span></sup><span style='font-family: Times;'> Symporter (BenE) Family</span></strong></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p align='JUSTIFY'><span style='font-family: Times;'> The BenE family contains several functionally characterized and sequenced members.&nbsp; Two of these are the benzoate permeases of <em>Acinetobacter calcoaceticus</em> (</span><span style='font-family: Times;'><em>Acinetobacter baylyi</em></span><span style='font-family: Times;'>)</span><span style='font-family: Times;'> and <em>E. coli</em>. These proteins are about 400 residues in length and possibly span the membrane 12 times. They exhibit about 30% identity to each other and limited sequence similarity to members of the aromatic acid:H</span><span style='font-family: Times;'><sup>+</sup> symporter (AAHS) family (2.A.1.15) of the major facilitator superfamily (MFS). However the degree of similarity with the latter proteins is insufficient to establish homology. How</span><span style='font-family: Times;'>ever, the BenE family has been shown to be a member of the APC superfamily. BenE is a Na<sup>+</sup> symporter (</span><span style='font-family: Times;'>). The ortholog in P<em>seudomonas putida</em> (<em>Arthrobacter siderocapsulatus</em>) &nbsp;</span><span style='font-family: Times;'> has also been shown to be a benzoate transporter (<span id='ref34547860.28591829' class='cite'>Choudhary <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).<br /></span></p>
<p align='JUSTIFY'>The purine transporter, PurT<sub>Cp</sub> of <em>Colwellia psychrerythraea, </em>forms a homodimer, and each protomer has 14 TMSs folded into a transport domain (core domain) and a
 scaffold domain (gate domain) (<span class='highlight'><span id='ref34557007.37549264' class='cite'>Weng <em>et al.</em> 2023</span></span>). A purine base is present in the 
structure and defines the location of the substrate binding site. 
Functional studies reveal that PurT<sub>Cp</sub> transports purines but 
not pyrimidines and that purine binding and transport is dependent on 
the pH. Mutation of a conserved aspartate residue close to the substrate
 binding site reveals the critical role of this residue in H<sup>+</sup>-dependent transport of purines. Comparison of the PurT<sub>Cp</sub> structure with transporters of the same structural fold suggests that 
rigid-body motions of the substrate-binding domain are central for 
substrate translocation across the membrane (<span class='highlight'><span id='ref34557007.37549264' class='cite'>Weng <em>et al.</em> 2023</span></span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>

<p align='CENTER'>purine (out) + H<sup>+</sup> (out) &rarr; purine (in) + H<sup>+</sup>(in)</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><HR>This family belongs to the <A HREF="superfamily.php?id=5">APC Superfamily</A>.<BR><HR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Choudhary, A., H.
