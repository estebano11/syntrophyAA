<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.47 The Divalent Anion:Na<sup>+</sup></strong></span><strong> Symporter (DASS) Family</strong></p>
<p>Functionally characterized proteins of the DASS family (also called the SLC13 family) transport (1) organic di- and tricarboxylates of the Krebs Cycle as well as dicarboxylate amino acid, (2) inorganic sulfate and (3) phosphate.  These proteins are found in Gram-negative bacteria, cyanobacteria, archaea, plant chloroplasts, yeast and animals.  They vary in size from 432 amino acyl residues (<em>M. jannaschii</em>) to 923 residues (<em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em>).  The three <em>S. cerevisiae</em> proteins are large (881-923 residues); the animal proteins are substantially smaller (539-616 residues), and the bacterial proteins are still smaller (461-612 residues).  They exhibit 11-14 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs).  An 11 TMS model for the animal NaDC-1 and hNaSi-1 carriers has been proposed (<span id='ref2612.12857732' class='cite'>Li and Pajor, 2003</span>; <span id='ref1107.10099705' class='cite'>Pajor, 1999</span>).  Two serine residues in the human sulfate transporter, hNaSi-1 (Q9BZW2), one in TMS 5 and one in TMS 6, are required for sulfate transport (<span id='ref2612.12857732' class='cite'>Li and Pajor, 2003</span>).  The former carrier and the other NaDC isoforms cotransport 3 Na<sup>+</sup> with each dicarboxylate.  Protonated tricarboxylates are also cotransported with 3 Na<sup>+</sup>.  Several organisms possess multiple paralogues of the DASS family (e.g., 4 for <em>E. coli;</em> 2 for <em>H. influenzae</em>, 3 for <em>S. cerevisiae</em>, and at least 4 for <em>C. elegans</em>). Members of this family have been reported to have this IT fold (<span id='ref34555317.36164651' class='cite'>Ferrada and Superti-Furga 2022</span>).</p>
<p>Proteins of the DASS family are divided into two groups of transporters with distinct anion specificities: the Na<sup>+</sup>-sulfate (NaS) cotransporters and the Na<sup>+</sup>-carboxylate (NaC) cotransporters. Mammalian members of this family &nbsp;are: SLC13A1 (NaS1), SLC13A2 (NaC1), SLC13A3 (NaC3), SLC13A4 (NaS2) and SLC13A5 (NaC2) (<span id='ref9889.23177988' class='cite'>Markovich 2012</span>). DASS family proteins encode plasma membrane polypeptides with 8-13 putative transmembrane domains, and are expressed in a variety of tissues. They are all Na<sup>+</sup>-coupled symporters. The Na<sup>+</sup>:anion coupling ratio is 3:1, indicative of electrogenic properties. They have a substrate preference for divalent anions, which include tetra-oxyanions for the NaS cotransporters or Krebs cycle intermediates (including mono-, di- and tricarboxylates) for the NaC cotransporters. The molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying the biochemical, physiological and structural properties of DASS family members have been reviewed (<span id='ref9889.23177988' class='cite'>Markovich, 2012</span>).</p>
<p>The phylogenetic tree for the DASS family reveals six clusters as follows: (1) all animal homologues; (2) all yeast proteins; (3) a functionally uncharacterized protein from <em>Ralstonia eutrophus</em>; (4) three <em>E. coli</em> proteins plus one from <em>H. influenzae</em> and one from spinach chloroplasts (the SodiT1 oxoglutarate:malate translocator); (5) an <em>E. coli</em> Orf that clusters loosely with a sulfur deprivation regulated protein of <em>Synechocystis</em>, and (6) an <em>M. jannaschii</em> protein that clusters loosely with an <em>H. influenzae</em> Orf.</p>
<p>Distant homologues of DASS family proteins may include members of the Ars (arsenite exporter) (TC #3.A.4) family as well as the NhaB (TC #2.A.34) and NhaC (TC #2.A.35) Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporter families.  The DASS family is therefore a member of the ion transporter (IT) superfamily (<span id='ref1108.10627041' class='cite'>Rabus <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).</p>
