<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.5  The Zinc (Zn<sup>2+</sup>)-Iron (Fe<sup>2+</sup>) Permease (ZIP) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>Most members of the ZIP family consist of 220-650 amino acyl residues with 8 TMSs.  However, LIV1 of man has been reported to have only 6 TMSs, although it exhibits 8 hydrophobic peaks, and the IAA-alanine resistance protein 1 (Iar1 of <em>A. thaliana</em>) also exhibits 8 TMSs (<a class='reflink' href='#ref5653'>Lasswell <em>et al</em>., 2000</a>). They are derived from animals, plants, yeast, bacteria and archaea.  They comprise a diverse family, with several paralogues in any one organism (e.g., 14 in mammals such as humans, at least 5 in <em>Caenorhabditis elegans</em> and <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em>, 9 in maize and two in <em>Saccharomyces cervisiae</em>). Zinc homeostasis in plants has been reviewed (<span id='ref34544164.26025516' class='cite'>Ricachenevsky <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp; </span><span style='font-family: Times;'>ZIP proteins form homo- or 
heterodimers with 8 transmembrane domains and extra-/intracellular 
domains of various lengths. Several ZIP members show specific 
extracellular domains composed of two subdomains, a helix-rich domain 
and proline-alanine-leucine (PAL) motif-containing domain (<span id='ref34549163.30370308' class='cite'>Bin <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).&nbsp; </span>ZIP genes in peanuts play crucial roles in the uptake and transport of Fe, Zn and Mn (<span id='ref34554556.35336668' class='cite'>Zhang <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). The structural bases for zinc transport through ZIP and ZnT porters, 
including the molecular mechanisms of zinc binding and transport, have 
been reviewed (<span id='ref34554607.35258351' class='cite'>Yin <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). Genome-wide functional studies of the ZIP family proteins in wild emmer wheat have been summarized (<span id='ref34554609.35270007' class='cite'>Gong <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). These proteins have the ZIP fold (<span id='ref34555317.36164651' class='cite'>Ferrada and Superti-Furga 2022</span>). Zip family proteins have been reviewed (<span id='ref34557281.37666628' class='cite'>Ma and Gong 2023</span>). The oligomeric state of ZIP transporters in mammalian cells have been estimated with fluorescence correlation spectroscopy (<span id='ref34557461.37666629' class='cite'>Liu <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>The various mammalian paralogues fall into four subfamilies and are found in a variety of cell types, cell locations and tissues, and some are responsive to hormones and cytokines (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9205'>Dempski 2012</a>).  Some</span> mammalian Zip genes apparently do not&nbsp; play critical roles in
zinc homeostasis when zinc is replete, but they play important, noncompensatory roles when this
metal is deficient (<span id='ref34545227.18353881' class='cite'>Kambe <em>et al.</em> 2008</span>).<span style='font-family: Times;'>&nbsp; Zip6 (LIV1) is estrogen responsive in breast cancer cells and is related to metastasis in lymph nodes.  Zip8 (Big M103) is TNF&alpha; and endotoxin induced in monocytes.    The two <em>S. cerevisiae</em> proteins, Zrt1 and Zrt2, both probably transport Zn<sup>2+</sup></span> with high specificity, but Zrt1 transports Zn<sup>2+</sup> with ten-fold higher affinity than Zrt2. In cacao (<em>Theobroma cacao</em> L.) there are 11 ZIP homologs, and their physicochemical properties, evolution, duplication, 
gene structure, promoter region and TcZIP family three-dimensional 
protein structures are described in the plasma 
membrane and chloroplast (<span id='ref34556079.36925749' class='cite'>Pacheco <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).&nbsp; Zinc transporters serve as prognostic predictors, and their expression correlates with immune cell infiltration in specific typoes of cancer (<span id='ref34557739.38230214' class='cite'>Liu <em>et al.</em> 2024</span>).</p>
<p>Some members of the ZIP family have been shown to transport Zn<sup>2+</sup> while others transport Fe<sup>2+</sup>, and a few have been shown to transport a range of metal ions.  One human protein member of the ZIP family is designated ''growth arrest inducible gene product,'' but its presumed transport activity has not been identified.  A second human protein, Zip4, is a Zn<sup>2+</sup> uptake permease and a disease protein (<a class='reflink' href='#ref3766'>Cousins <em>et al</em>., 2006</a>).  Histidine-rich repeats are found in extracellular N- and C-termini as well as a long intracellular loop, and Zip14 has an extra extracellular his-rich  loop.  One family of mammalian Zip proteins (the LZT family) has a metaprotease motif (HEXPHEXGD) that may allow them to function as matrix metaloproteases.  Zip10 has  C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub> zinc finger and cytochrome c motifs in its first TMS (<a class='reflink' href='#ref3766'>Cousins <em>et al</em>., 2006</a>).</p>
<p>The energy source for transport has not been characterized, but these systems probably function as secondary carriers.  They do not require ATP (<a class='reflink' href='#ref3766'>Cousins <em>et al</em>., 2006</a>).  In one study, uptake of Zn<sup>2+</sup> via the hZip2 permease was energy independent, independent of Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> gradients, but stimulated by HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> (<a class='reflink' href='#ref1145'>Gaither and Eide, 2000</a>).  The authors propose a Zn<sup>2+</sup>:HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> symport mechanism.  hZip1 is the major Zn<sup>2+</sup> uptake system in many human tissues (<a class='reflink' href='#ref1939'>Gaither and Eide, 2001</a>). The N-terminal regions are novel substrate selectors in the ZIP family of transporters (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7481'>Nishida <em>et al.</em>, 2011</a>). An inward-open metal-free BbZIP structure differs substantially in the relative positions of the two separate domains of ZIPs. With accompanying coevolutional analyses, mutagenesis, and uptake assays, the data point to an elevator-type transport mechanism, likely shared within the ZIP family, unifying earlier functional data. Moreover, the structure reveals a previously unknown ninth transmembrane segment that is important for activity in vivo (<span id='ref34554963.35857505' class='cite'>Wiuf <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p>Mice deficient in Zn transporter Slc39a13/Zip13 show changes in bone, teeth, and connective tissues, reminiscent of the clinical spectrum of human Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS), of some features of osteogenesis imperfecta and Zn deficient disorders. The <em>Zip13 </em>knockout (<em>Zip13-</em>KO) mice show defects in the function of osteoblasts, chondrocytes, odontoblasts and fibroblasts. Zip13 protein is localized to the Golgi in the corresponding cells. Impairment in BMP and TGF-beta signaling were observed in <em>Zip13-</em>KO cells (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7041'>Fukada <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>).&nbsp; ZIP5, ZIP6, ZIP7, and ZIP10 in rat liver are regulated by iron. They may play a role in hepatic iron/metal homeostasis during iron deficiency and overload (<a class='reflink' href='#ref8711'>Nam and Knutson, 2012</a>).&nbsp; In maize, IRT1 is induced by zinc and iron deficiency, ZIP4 is induced during early embryogeneis, ZIP5 is induced during middle embryogeneis, and IRT1 and ZIP6 are induced during late embryogenesis (<span id='ref10222.23924433' class='cite'>Li <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>12 members of the Zn/Fe-regulated transporters (ZRT/IRT) (ZIP Family) have been identified and isolated from <em>Poncirus trifoliata</em>, and they were named PtZIPs according to the sequence and functional similarity to <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> ZIPs (<span id='ref34546660.28469631' class='cite'>Fu <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). The 12 PtZIPs are of 334-419 aas, harboring 6-9 putative TMSs. All contain the conserved ZIP signature sequences in TMS4, and nine of them showed a variable region rich in histidine residues between TMS3 and TMS4.&nbsp; PtZIPs fall into four phylogenetic groups as for ZIPs of<em> A. thaliana</em>. Expression analyses showed that PtZIP genes are differently induced in roots and leaves under conditions of Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> deficiency. PtIRT1, PtZIP1, PtZIP2, PtZIP3, and PtZIP12&nbsp; complement a <em>zrt1 zrt2</em> mutant, which was deficient in Zn<sup>2+</sup> uptake; PtIRT1 and PtZIP7 complement a <em>fet3 fet4 </em>mutant, deficient in Fe<sup>2+</sup> uptake, and PtIRT1 complements a <em>smf1</em> mutant, deficient in Mn<sup>2+</sup> uptake, suggesting their respective functions in Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup>, and Mn<sup>2+</sup> transport (<span id='ref34546660.28469631' class='cite'>Fu <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).</p>
<p>There are 10 ZnT (CDF)  (TC#2.A.4) and 15 Zip transporters in humans. They appear to play opposite roles in cellular zinc homeostasis. CDF transporters reduce intracellular zinc availability by promoting zinc efflux from cells or into intracellular vesicles, while Zip transporters increase intracellular zinc availability by promoting extracellular zinc uptake and vesicular zinc release into the cytoplasm. Both the ZnT and Zip transporter families exhibit unique tissue-specific expression, differential responsiveness to dietary zinc deficiency and excess, and differential responsiveness to physiologic stimuli via hormones and cytokines (<span id='ref34551766.15189117' class='cite'>Liuzzi and Cousins 2004</span>).</p>
<p>The apo state structure in an inward-facing conformation from <em>Bordetella bronchiseptica</em> revealed a disassembled transport site, altered inter-helical interactions, and a rigid body movement of a 4 TMS bundle relative to the other TMSs (<span id='ref34555906.36693843' class='cite'>Zhang <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>). The computationally generated and biochemically validated outward-facing conformation model revealed a slide of the 4-TMS bundle, which carries the transport site(s), by approximately 8&nbsp;&Aring; toward the extracellular side against the static TMSs which mediate dimerization. Thus, BbZIP is an elevator-type transporter. <span id='ref34556707.37296139' class='cite'>Pang <em>et al.</em> 2023 </span>reported a cryo-EM structure of a ZIP-family transporter from <em>Bordetella bronchiseptica</em> at 3.05 &Aring; resolution in an inward-facing, inhibited conformation. The transporter forms a homodimer, each protomer containing nine transmembrane helices and three metal ions. Two metal ions form a binuclear pore structure, and the third ion is located at an egress site facing the cytoplasm. The egress site is covered by a loop, and two histidine residues on the loop interact with the egress-site ion and regulate its release. Cell-based Zn<sup>2+</sup> uptake and cell growth viability assays revealed negative regulation of Zn<sup>2+</sup> uptake through sensing intracellular Zn<sup>2+ </sup>status using a built-in sensor. These analyses provided mechanistic insight into the autoregulation of zinc uptake across membranes (<span id='ref34556707.37296139' class='cite'>Pang <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
