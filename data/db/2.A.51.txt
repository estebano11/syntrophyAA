<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.51 The Chromate Ion Transporter (CHR) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Homologues of the CHR family have been identified in bacteria, eukaryotes and archaea. Two <em>Bacillus</em> homologues are half length with six putative TMSs each (<span id='ref1151.9791139' class='cite'>Nies <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>). These two proteins together (2.A.51.1.5), but not singly, cause chromate resistance due to formation of a heterodimer and consequent chromate efflux (<span id='ref6681.19581367' class='cite'>D&iacute;az-Maga&ntilde;a <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).  Half-sized homologues are found in several bacteria.  The functionally characterized chromate efflux pumps of <em>P. aeruginosa</em> and <em>A. eutrophus</em> are plasmid-encoded.  They are about 400 amino acyl residues long with 10 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs).  They arose by a tandem internal gene duplication event from a putative 6 TMS primordial precursor, but the first two TMSs in the <em>A. entrophus</em> ChrA protein have lost their hydrophobic character (<span id='ref1151.9791139' class='cite'>Nies <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>). </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>In a more recent study, (<span id='ref4591.17986256' class='cite'>Diaz-Perez <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>), 77 duplicated 'bidomains' (BDs) and 58 unduplicated 'monodomains' (MDs) were identified and analyzed. The MDs clustered separately from the N-terminal BDs, and both clustered separately from the C-terminal BDs. This suggests that the MDs, possibly present in inverted orientation in the membrane, may have a unique structure and mode of action (<span id='ref4591.17986256' class='cite'>Diaz-Perez <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>The membrane topology of the ChrA protein of <em>P. aeruginosa</em> was conducted using <em>lacZ</em> and <em>phoA</em> translational fusions (<span id='ref3704.16923073' class='cite'>Jiminez-Mejia <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  A 13 TMS topology was predicted with the N-terminus in the cytoplasm and the C-terminus in the periplasm. Predicted TMSs 1-6 proved to be homologous to predicted TMSs 8-13, but with opposite orientation in the membrane. </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> <em>Synechococcus</em> sp. PCC7942 bears an endogenous 50 Kb plasmid-encoded, sulfur-regulated CHR homologue that apparently confers chromate sensitivity (rather than chromate resistance) when grown in media containing a low sulfate concentration.  This protein, designated SrpC, may be a sulfate uptake permease that can also transport chromate, but this possibility has not been established. ChrA of <em>P. aeruginosa</em> is a secondary carrier which might function by chromate uniport, chromate:H<sup>+</sup></span> antiport, or chromate:anion antiport.  It catalyzes CrO<sub>4</sub> efflux with a Km of 80 &mu;M.  SO<sub>4</sub><sup>=</sup> and MoO<sub>4</sub><sup>=</sup> inhibit efflux but arsenate and vanadate do not inhibit (<span id='ref2171.12113942' class='cite'>Pimentel <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  A pmf dependency is likely since valinomycin, nigericin and protonophores such as CCCP inhibit. <span id='ref6798.17934697' class='cite'>Ram&iacute;rez-D&iacute;az <em>et al.</em> (2008)</span> have published a review concerning the mechanisms of bacterial resistance to chromium compounds. In <em>Neurosporal crassa</em>, a chromate uptake transporter, Chr-1, a member of the Chr family, has been described (<span id='ref34551813.23085746' class='cite'>Flores-Alvarez <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction catalyzed by prokaryotic CHRs may be:</p>
<p align='center'>CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> (in) [+ nH<sup>+</sup> (out)]  &rarr;  CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> (out) [+ nH<sup>+</sup> (in)]</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction catalyzed by SrpC or Chr-1 may be:</p>
<p align='center'>SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> or CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out)  &rarr;  SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> or CrO<sub>4</sub><sup>2-</sup> (in) + nH<sup>+</sup> (in)</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Aguilar-Barajas, E., E.
