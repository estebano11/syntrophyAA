<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong>2.A.55 The Metal Ion (Mn<sup>2+</sup>-iron) Transporter (Nramp) Family</strong></p>
<p><strong> </strong></p>
<p>Homologues of this family are found in various yeasts, plants, animals, archaea, and Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacteria termed ''natural resistance-associated'' macrophage protein (NRAMP) proteins because one of the animal homologues plays a role in resistance to intracellular bacterial pathogens such as <em>Salmonella enterica</em> serovar Typhimurium, <em>Leishmania donovani</em> and <em>Mycobacterium bovis</em><em>.</em><em>&nbsp;</em>The natural history of SLC11 genes in vertebrates has been discussed by Neves et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref8670'>2011</a>). Proposed to be a distant member of the DMT Superfamily (TC# 2.A.7), several human pathologies may result from defects in Nramp-dependent
Fe<sup>2+</sup> or Mn<sup>2+</sup> transport, including iron overload, neurodegenerative diseases and innate
susceptibility to infectious diseases (<span id='ref9874.23046654' class='cite'>Cellier 2012</span>). Nramp-driven transport of iron across membranes is selective for ferrous ions although iron is mostly present as ferric ions in the growth media and in engulfed bacteria (<span id='ref34554815.35550931' class='cite'>Peracino <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).&nbsp; These systems use a 3D network of residues articulating a Me<sup>2+</sup>-selective carrier conformational switch which is maintained in fast-evolving clades at the cost of divergent epistatic interactions, impacting carrier shape and dynamics (<span id='ref34557623.37894758' class='cite'>Cellier 2023</span>).</p>
<hr />
<p>Humans and rodents possess two distinct NRAMPs.  The broad specificity NRAMP2 (DMT1), which transports a range of divalent metal cations, transports Fe<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup> and H<sup>+</sup><sup>&nbsp;</sup>with a 1:1 stoichiometry and apparent affinities of 6 &mu;m and about 1 &mu;m, respectively.  Variable H<sup>+</sup>:Fe<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup> stoichiometry has also been reported.  The order of substrate preference for NRAMP2 is Fe<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Zn<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Mn<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Co<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Ca<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Cu<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Ni<sup>2+</sup>&gt; Pb<sup>2+</sup>.  Many of these ions can inhibit iron absorption.  Mutation of Nramp2 in rodents leads to defective endosomal iron export within the ferritin cycle, impaired intestinal iron absorption and microcytic anemia.  Symptoms of Mn<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>deficiency are also seen.  It is found in apical membranes of intestinal epithelial cells but also in late endosomes and lysosomes.</p>
<p>In contrast to the widely expressed NRAMP2, NRAMP1 is expressed primarily in macrophages and monocytes and appears to have a preference for Mn<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>rather than Fe<sup>2+</sup>. NRAMP1 (TC# 2.A.55.2.3) has been reported to function by metal:H<sup>+</sup><sup> </sup> antiport (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4408'>Techau <em>et al</em>., 2007</a>). It is hypothesized that a deficiency for Mn<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>or some other metal prevents the generation of reactive oxygenic and nitrogenic compounds that are used by macrophage to combat pathogens.  This hypothesis is supported by studies on the bacterial NRAMP homologues which exhibit extremely high selectivity for Mn<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;over Fe<sup>2+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>and other divalent cations.  Regulation of these transporters in bacteria can occur through Fur, OxyR, and most commonly a DtxR homolog, MntR.</p>
<p>The Smf1 protein of <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae</em> appears to catalyze high-affinity (K<sub>m</sub> = 0.3 &mu;m) Mn<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;uptake while the closely related Smf2 protein may catalyze low affinity (K<sub>m</sub> = 60 &mu;m) Mn<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;uptake in the same organism. Both proteins also mediate H<sup>+</sup>-dependent Fe<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>uptake.  These proteins are of 575 and 549 amino acyl residues in length and are predicted to have 8-12 transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners.  The <em>E. coli</em> homologue of 412 aas exhibits 11 putative and confirmed TMSs with the N-terminus in and the C-terminus out.  The yeast proteins may be localized to the vacuole and/or the plasma membrane of the yeast cell.  Indirect and some direct experiments suggest that they may be able to transport several heavy metals including Mn<sup>2+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, Cd<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;and Co<sup>2+</sup>.  A third yeast protein, Smf3p, appears to be exclusively intracellular, possibly in the Golgi. Nramp2 (Slc11A2) of <em>Homo sapiens </em>(TC #2.A.55.2.1) has a 12 TMS topology with intracellular N- and C-termini. Two-fold structural symmetry in the arrangement of membrane helices for TM1-5 and TM6-10 (conserved Slc2 hydrophobic core) is suggested (<a class='reflink' href='#ref6707'>Czachorowski <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>). The NRAMP family genes in tea plant (Camellia sinensis) have been identified (<span id='ref34553424.34070434' class='cite'>Li <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>The Nramp family of divalent metal transporters enables manganese import 
in bacteria and dietary iron uptake in mammals.&nbsp;<span id='ref34546068.27839948' class='cite'>Bozzi <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> determined the 
crystal structure of the <em>Deinococcus radiodurans</em> Nramp homolog 
(DraNramp) in an inward-facing apo state, including the complete 
transmembrane (TM) segment 1a (absent from a previous Nramp structure). Cysteine accessibility scanning results Allowed identification of the metal-permeation pathway in the alternate 
outward-open conformation. Two 
natural anemia-causing glycine-to-arginine mutations impaired 
transition metal transport in both human Nramp2 and DraNramp. The TM4 
G153R mutation perturbs the closing of the outward metal-permeation 
pathway and alters the selectivity of the conserved metal-binding site. 
In contrast, the&nbsp;TM1a G45R mutation prevents conformational change by 
sterically blocking the essential movement of that helix, thus locking 
the transporter in an inward-facing state (<span id='ref34546068.27839948' class='cite'>Bozzi <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
