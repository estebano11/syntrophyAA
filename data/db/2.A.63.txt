<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.63  The Monovalent Cation (K<sup>+</sup> or Na<sup>+</sup>):Proton Antiporter-3 (CPA3) Family</strong></span></p>
<p align='justify'><span style='font-family: Times;'> The CPA3 family may consist of bacterial multicomponent K<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> antiporters. The best characterized such systems are the PhaABCDEFG system of <em>Rhizobium meliloti</em> that functions in pH adaptation and as a K<sup>+</sup> efflux system, and the MnhABCDEFG system of <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> that functions as a Na<sup>+</sup> efflux Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> antiporter. A homologous but only partially sequenced system was earlier reported to catalyze Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> antiport in an alkalophilic <em>Bacillus </em>strain. PhaA and PhaD are respectively homologous to the ND5 and ND4 subunits of the H<sup>+</sup>-pumping NADH:ubiquinone oxidoreductase (TC #3.D.1). Homologous protein subunits from <em>E. coli</em> NADH:quinone oxidoreductase can functionally replace MrpA and MrpD in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>&nbsp;(<span id='ref7685.21236240' class='cite'>Moparthi <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). The seven Pha proteins are of the following sizes (in #aas) and exhibit the following putative numbers of transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs): A, 725 and 17; B, 257 and 5; C, 115 and 3; D, 547 and 13; E, 161 and 3; F, 92 and 3; G, 120 and 3. Thus, all are predicted to be integral membrane proteins. Corresponding values for the <em>S. aureus</em> Mnh system are: A, 801 and 18; B, 142 and 4; C, 113 and 3; D, 498 and 13; E, 159 and 4; F, 97 and 3; G, 118 and 3. In view of the complexity of the system and the homology with NDH family protein constituents, a complicated energy coupling mechanism, possibly involving a redox reaction, cannot be ruled out. Members of the </span>Cation/Proton Antiporter (CPA) superfamily, the Ion Transport (IT) superfamily, and the Na<sup>+</sup>-translocating Mrp transporter superfamily can catalyze Na+/H+ antiiport (<span id='ref34555428.36012428' class='cite'>Pati&ntilde;o-Ruiz <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). Transport mechanisms for Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> exchangers that explain their highly pH-regulated activity profiles have been considered (<span id='ref34555429.36012428' class='cite'>Pati&ntilde;o-Ruiz <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p align='justify'><span style='font-family: Times;'> Homologues of PhaA, B, C and D and Nha1, 2, 3 and 4 of an alkalophilic <em>Bacillus</em> strain are the Yuf(Mrp)T, U, V and D genes of <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>. In this system, YufT is believed to be responsible for Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> antiporter activity, but it does not have activity in the absence of other constituents of the operon. YufF (MrpF) appears to catalyze cholate efflux, possibly by a Na<sup>+</sup> symport mechanism (<span id='ref1263.11004162' class='cite'>Ito et. al, 2000</span>). It plays a major role in Na<sup>+</sup> extrusion (Kosono et al., 1998; <span id='ref1261.10198001' class='cite'>Ito et.al., 1999</span>) and is required for initiation of sporulation (<span id='ref1262.10648512' class='cite'>Kosono <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>), Additionally, another component of the operon, MrpF (equivalent to PhaF of <em>R. meliloti</em>) has been implicated in choline and Na<sup>+</sup> efflux (<span id='ref1261.10198001' class='cite'>Ito et. al., 1999</span>). The MrpA-G proteins of <em>B. subtilis</em> have been shown to be present in a single multicomponent complex (<span id='ref4644.17693497' class='cite'>Kajiyama <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). They provide Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiport activity and function in multiple compound resistance and pH homeostasis.</span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>Na<sup>+</sup> or Li<sup>+</sup> does, but K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, and Mg<sup>2+</sup> do not support significant antiport by the Gram-positive bacterial systems (2.A.63.1.2 and 3) (<span id='ref4200.17293423' class='cite'>Swartz <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Na<sup>+</sup>(Li<sup>+</sup>)/H<sup>+</sup> antiporters have alkaline pH optima and apparent K<sub>m</sub> values for Na<sup>+</sup> that are among the lowest reported for bacterial Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporters. Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiport consumes the pmf and therefore is probably electrogenic (<span id='ref4200.17293423' class='cite'>Swartz <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).</span></p>
<p>The <em>mrp</em> homolog gene cluster <em>mrpCD1D2EFGAB</em> (Ap-<em>mrp</em>) was found in a halotolerant cyanobacterium, <em>Aphanothece
halophytica</em>, amplified, and expressed in <em>Escherichia coli</em> mutant TO114 (<span id='ref6803.19700555' class='cite'>Fukaya <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).
Ap-<em>mrp</em> complemented the salt-sensitive phenotype of strain TO114 and exhibited
Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> and Li<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+</sup> exchange activities (<span id='ref6803.19700555' class='cite'>Fukaya <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).</p>
<p>Ap-MrpB is 220 aas long and has 6 putative TMSs. TC-BLAST searchers reveal that it shows significant (e<sup>-7</sup>) similarity with protein PF1428 of <em>Pyrococcus furiosus </em>(TC# 3.D.1.4.1 and PhaB of <em>Sinorhizobium meliloti </em>(2.A.63.1.1)). The CPA3 family is characterized as being multi component K<sup>+ </sup>or Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+ </sup>antiporters having seven different constituents, some resembling components of NADH dehydrogenase complexes. It is probable that this gene cluster belongs to the CPA3 family. However, the evidence presented by <span id='ref6803.19700555' class='cite'>Fukaya <em>et al.</em> (2009)</span> suggests that this subunit alone, rather than the entire complex, is sufficient for catalysis of Na<sup>+</sup>:H<sup>+ </sup>antiport. If this is the case, then the other subunits of the complex may be auxiliary subunits serving regulatory, catalytic or dissimilar functions. Several of these subunits in the presumed Mrp complex of <em>Aphanothece halophytica</em> are demonstrably homologous to corresponding subunits in the other CPA3 family members.</p>
<p>Mrp antiporters are broadly distributed among bacteria and archaea, not only in alkaliphiles. Generally, all Mrp subunits, <em>mrpA-G</em>, are required for enzymatic activity. Two exceptions are Mrp from the archaea <em>Methanosarcina acetivorans</em> and the eubacteria <em>Natranaerobius thermophilus</em>, which are reported to sustain Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiport activity with the MrpA subunit alone (<span id='ref34556472.29218041' class='cite'>Ito <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). Two large subunits of 
the Mrp antiporter, MrpA and MrpD, are homologous to membrane-embedded 
subunits of the respiratory chain complex I, NuoL, NuoM, and NuoN, and 
the small subunit MrpC has homology with NuoK. The functions of the Mrp 
antiporter include sodium tolerance and pH homeostasis in an alkaline 
environment, nitrogen fixation in <em>Schizolobium meliloti</em>, bile salt tolerance in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> and <em>Vibrio cholerae</em>, arsenic oxidation in <em>Agrobacterium tumefaciens</em>, pathogenesis in <em>Pseudomonas aeruginosa</em> and <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em>,
 and the conversion of energy involved in metabolism and hydrogen 
production in archaea (<span id='ref34556472.29218041' class='cite'>Ito <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). In addition, some Mrp antiporters transport K<sup>+</sup> and Ca<sup>2+</sup> instead of Na<sup>+</sup>,
 depending on the environmental conditions. Recently, the molecular 
structure of the respiratory chain complex I has been elucidated. Based on this, several hypotheses concerning the 
substrate transport mechanism in the Mrp antiporter have been proposed. 
The MrpA and MrpD subunits, which are homologous to the proton transport
 subunit of complex I, are involved in the transport of protons and 
their coupling cations.</p>
