<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong> 2.A.64  The Twin Arginine Targeting (Tat) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The TatABCE system of <em>E. coli</em> has been both sequenced and functionally characterized (<span id='ref1264.8939424' class='cite'>Berks, 1996</span>; <span id='ref1273.10652088' class='cite'>Berks <em>et al</em>., 2000a</span>,<span id='ref1274.11004447' class='cite'>b</span>).  This system forms a large (~600 kDa) complex which interacts with fully folded redox proteins that have an N-terminal S/TRRXFLK 'twin arginine' leader motif (<span id='ref1945.11422364' class='cite'>Sargent <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  It translocates several redox enzymes to the <em>E. coli</em> periplasm including nitrate reductase (NapA) and trimethylamine N-oxide reductase (TorA) which have this leader motif (<span id='ref1266.9660752' class='cite'>Bogsch <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>; <span id='ref1267.9427745' class='cite'>Santini <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>; <span id='ref1268.9649434' class='cite'>Sargent <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>).  Hydrogenases, formate dehydrogenases and several non-redox proteins (about two dozen in <em>E. coli</em>), including virulence factors, periplasmic binding proteins, and enzymes involved in envelope biogenesis, have the 'twin R' motif and probably use this pathway.  Among these substrate enzyme complexes are several that contain integral membrane proteins (<span id='ref2138.12115052' class='cite'>Sargent <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  These proteins associate with their cofactors in the cell cytoplasm before translocation.  TatABCE is also required for the assembly of the <em>E. coli</em> dimethyl sulfoxide reductase (DmsABC) (<span id='ref1270.9546395' class='cite'>Weiner <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>; <span id='ref1944.11389150' class='cite'>Sambasivarao <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  The Tat system functions independently of other types of protein secretory systems present in <em>E. coli.</em> However, Tat signal sequences exhibit overlap with Sec signal sequences and can direct proteins to both secretory systems (<span id='ref3862.17218314' class='cite'>Tullman-Ercek <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>; <span id='ref3861.17229735' class='cite'>Kreutzenbeck <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). One report suggests that Tat systems can transport small unstructured hydrophilic proteins although hydrophobic patches can abort transport at a late stage (<span id='ref4781.17848553' class='cite'>Richter <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Fan et al. (<span id='ref7510.20599707' class='cite'>2010</span>) have reputed that one signal is sufficient for the stepwise transport of two distinct passenger proteins across the thylakoid membrane.&nbsp; The 2012 view of how the Tat system works is provided in (<span id='ref8946.22683878' class='cite'>Palmer and Berks 2012</span>). The pore-forming part of TatA has been resolved by NMR(<span id='ref9040.20977272' class='cite'>Walther <em>et al.</em> 2010</span>).&nbsp; TatC helix 5 and the TatB transmembrane helix interact (<span id='ref8824.22742417' class='cite'>Kneuper <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>) and cooperate to produce recognition of Tat signal peptides in <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref9527.22761916' class='cite'>Lausberg <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; Transmembrane insertion of Tat peptides is driven by TatC and regulated by TatB (<span id='ref9683.23250441' class='cite'>Fr&ouml;bel <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; </span>In the TatBC receptor complex,
the transmembrane helix of each TatB is sandwiched between two TatCs, with one of
the inter-subunit interfaces incorporating a functionally important cluster of interacting polar
residues. TatA also associates with TatC at the polar cluster site (<span id='ref34546302.27914200' class='cite'>Alcock <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).&nbsp; TatC drives assemblyof the TatABC complex whereas TatA is essential for stability (<span id='ref34551052.15571732' class='cite'>Mangels <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>).&nbsp; &Delta;pH, in addition to &Delta;psi, contributes energetically to Tat transport in vivo in <em>E. coli </em>(<span id='ref34557520.37742925' class='cite'>Zhou <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>). Novel tail-anchored membrane proteins integrated by the bacterial twin-arginine translocase have been characterized (<span id='ref34557861.38363712' class='cite'>Gallego-Parrilla <em>et al.</em> 2024</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The TatA, TatB, TatC and TatE proteins in <em>E. coli </em>have 1, 1, 6 and 1 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs), respectively (sizes of 98, 171, 258 and 67 amino acyl residues, respectively) (<span id='ref4297.17545291' class='cite'>Punginelli <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).  TatA, TatB and TatE are homologous, and TatA and TatE, which are more similar to each other than they are to TatB, can partially substitute for each other and form heterooligomers (<span id='ref34544708.26483541' class='cite'>Eimer <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp; Both TatA and TatB have the N- out and C- in orientation (<span id='ref8522.22399293' class='cite'>Koch <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). They can be mutationally modified so only one of these proteins is required (<span id='ref4780.17881358' class='cite'>Barrett <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). The transmembrane and amphipathic helical regions of TatA, B and E are critical for function, but their C-terminal domains are not (<span id='ref870.12374820' class='cite'>Lee <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  Chan et al. (<span id='ref5522.17536842' class='cite'>2007</span>) have reported that the N-terminus of TatA is located in the cytoplasm rather than the periplasm. The C-terminus might have a dual topology; its orientation in the membrane could be dependent on the membrane potential. Thus, two architectures of TatA may exist in the membrane: one with a single transmembrane helix and the other with two transmembrane helices. The double transmembrane helix topology might be the building block for the translocation channel. However this suggestion was not supported by more recent work (<span id='ref8522.22399293' class='cite'>Koch <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; Multimeric TatA may form an expandable protein-conducting channel (<span id='ref6580.17669355' class='cite'>Lange <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span> ). The NMR solution structure of TatA has been published (<span id='ref7249.20726548' class='cite'>Hu <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>). Structural and biophysical studies of the amphipathic &alpha;-helical region of TatA from <em>E. coli</em>&nbsp;have been conducted (<span id='ref8536.21683683' class='cite'>Chan <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).&nbsp; TatB functions as an oligomeric binding site for folded Tat precursor proteins (<span id='ref7252.20926683' class='cite'>Maurer <em>et al.</em> 2010</span>). </span>The polar amino acid in TMS1 (possibly the only TMS in the protein) is not necessary for a functional TatA. Hydrophilicity and helix-stabilizing properties of this polar amino acid were found to be highly correlated with the Tat activity (<span id='ref34556038.36764519' class='cite'>Hao <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>For the Tat system of <em>E. coli</em>, like TatB, but different from TatA, TatE contacts a Tat signal peptide independently of the proton-motive force and restricts the premature processing of a Tat signal peptide. TatE embarks at the transmembrane helix five of TatC where it becomes so closely spaced to TatB that both proteins can be covalently linked by a zero-space cross-linker. This suggests that in addition to TatB and TatC, TatE is a functionally distinct component of the Tat substrate receptor complex (<span id='ref34549103.29358647' class='cite'>Eimer <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). The intrinsic ability of TatA in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> to flip out of the membrane core may play a role in its membrane-destabilizing effect during Tat-dependent translocation (<span id='ref34555788.36523158' class='cite'>Stockwald <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>TatC is required for interaction of TatA with TatB (<span id='ref1941.11279240' class='cite'>Bolhuis <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>) and has 6 established TMSs with both the N- and C-termini in the cytoplasm (<span id='ref2172.11867724' class='cite'>Drew <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>). Interaction and assembly of the substrate protein with the Tat complex appears to occur in several steps (<span id='ref2615.14580344' class='cite'>Alami <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  First, the twin arginine precursor associates with TatC.  Second, TatB associates with TatC.  Third, TatA association occurs only in the presence of a transmembrane pH gradient.   The TatA/B protein-translocating complex channel, of variable size, accommodates and transports the substrate protein complex (Dabney-Smith, 2006; <span id='ref3340.16407185' class='cite'>Gerard and Cline, 2006</span>; <span id='ref3341.16027357' class='cite'>Gohlke <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>; <span id='ref3342.15805540' class='cite'>Hicks <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>). TatB may mediate transfer of the folded substrate from TatC to the Tat pore (<span id='ref2615.14580344' class='cite'>Alami <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>). TatC may be peripheral to the TatA/B/C channel which accomodates proteins via the hydrophilic lining of amphipathic &alpha;-helices (<span id='ref4201.17565984' class='cite'>Greene <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). TatB and TatC both recognize the twin arginine signal sequence (<span id='ref4511.17936785' class='cite'>Strauch and Georgiou, 2007</span>), </span><span style='font-family: Times;'>and TatB thus forms an oligomeric binding 
site that transiently accomodates folded Tat precursors (<span id='ref7252.20926683' class='cite'>Maurer <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>). A TatC dimer is probably at the core of the Tat complex (<span id='ref7669.21709427' class='cite'>Maldonado <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).&nbsp; </span>Novel tail-anchored membrane proteins are integrated by the bacterial twin-arginine translocase (<span id='ref34557861.38363712' class='cite'>Gallego-Parrilla <em>et al.</em> 2024</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Gram-negative bacteria with fully sequenced genomes exhibit only a single TatC homologue, but many Gram-positive bacteria and archaea encode two (<span id='ref2139.12029389' class='cite'>Yen <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  Gram-positive bacteria such as <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> have two independently functioning systems (TatAyCy and TatAdCd in <em>B. subtilis</em>).  Expression of the bifunctional <em>B. subtilis</em> TatAd protein in <em>E. coli</em> revealed distinct TatA/B-family and TatB-specific domains (<span id='ref8627.21479530' class='cite'>Barnett <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). Plants have both mitochondrial and chloroplast homologues; for example, <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> has one chloroplast TatC homologue and two TatA homologues as well as two putative mitochondrial TatC homologues.  These proteins are not found in yeast and animals.  A few bacteria (i.e., <em>Rickettsia prowazekii</em>) have only one TatA homologue, but most have two, and several have three (&alpha;-proteobacteria and <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>). There are usually two sequence dissimilar paralogues (e.g., TatA and TatB in <em>E. coli</em>) and sometimes one sequence similar paralogue (e.g., TatE) (<span id='ref2139.12029389' class='cite'>Yen <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>). </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The energetics of the chloroplast Tat system (<span id='ref2396.12553911' class='cite'>Alder and Theg, 2003</span>; <span id='ref3338.15802249' class='cite'>Berks <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>; <span id='ref3343.15817413' class='cite'>Theg <em>et al</em>., 2005</span>) suggest a protein:H<sup>+</sup> antiport mechanism with about 100,000 H<sup>+</sup> released per transported protein (equivalent to about 10<sup>4</sup> ATP).  The Tat pathway may use about 3% of the total chloroplast energy yield.  In chloroplasts, cptatC and Hcf106 form a signal peptide precursor-bound receptor complex which assembles the oligomeric Tha4 translocation pore (<span id='ref3359.16407186' class='cite'>Dabney-Smith <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>; <span id='ref3340.16407185' class='cite'>G&eacute;rard and Cline, 2006</span>).  A 'trap door' mechanism was proposed in which oligomers of Tha4 amphipathic helices fold into the membrane to allow form-fitting passage of the substrate precursor protein (<span id='ref3359.16407186' class='cite'>Dabney-Smith <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  After translocation, the complex dissociates (<span id='ref3340.16407185' class='cite'>G&eacute;rard and Cline, 2006</span>). </span>Tha4 oligomers may dock with a precursor-receptor complex and undergo a
conformational switch that results in activation for protein transport.
This possibly involves accretion of additional Tha4 subunits into a larger
transport-active homo-oligomer (<span id='ref6405.19193764' class='cite'>Dabney-Smith and Cline, 2009</span>). Multiple precursor proteins bound to a single receptor complex can be 
transported together (<span id='ref6985.20339348' class='cite'>Ma and Cline, 2010</span>). Functional assembly of thylakoid Tat protein transport pathway components, Hcf106, cpTatC and Tha4, have been achieved in vitro (<span id='ref34555262.14653819' class='cite'>Fincher <em>et al.</em> 2003</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Three components are required for Tat transport, cpTatC, Hcf106, and Tha4, in thylakoids (the orthologous TatC, TatB, and TatA, respectively, in bacteria).  The thylakoid Tat system has been experimentally staged into several steps. (1) The precursor protein binds to a cpTatC-Hcf106 receptor complex, (2) a Tha4 oligomer assembles with the precursor-receptor complex to form the putative translocase, (3) and the precursor is transported into the lumen. After transport, Tha4 dissociates from the receptor complex, resetting the system for another round of transport (<span id='ref3706.17172598' class='cite'>G&eacute;rard and Cline, 2007</span>). Tha4 has been shown to undergo conformational changes that accompany protein transport (<span id='ref8938.22896708' class='cite'>Aldridge <em>et al.</em> 2012). </span></span>The optimally 15-residue length of the TatA and TatB TMSs can be seen as a compromise between the need for some hydrophobic mismatch to allow the membrane to reversibly reach the threshold thinness required for toroidal pore formation, and the permanently destabilizing effect of placing even shorter helices into these energy-transducing membranes (<span id='ref34554642.35490783' class='cite'>Hao <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> A genomic survey indicates that the TAT pathway is utilized to varying extents depending on the bacterium, from 0 to 20% of the total secreted proteins (<span id='ref2114.12562823' class='cite'>Dilks <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  While many prokaryotes use it primarily for the secretion of redox protein complexes, some Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria as well as archaea, use it to export non-redox proteins.  The composition of the TAT protein complex does not correlate with numbers of substrates but does with organismal phylogeny (<span id='ref2114.12562823' class='cite'>Dilks <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  One report suggests that the TAT pathway can export outer membrane proteins without a cleavable signal sequence (<span id='ref6346.18643934' class='cite'>Ferrandez and Condemine, 2008</span>).&nbsp;<em>Streptomyces</em> species have TatA, B and C (<span id='ref2139.12029389' class='cite'>Yen <em>et al.</em>, 2002</span>) and translocate large numbers of lipoproteins out via the Tat pathway. Lipoprotein biogenesis is essential in <em>S. coelicolor </em>(<span id='ref7814.20572939' class='cite'>Thompson <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>).&nbsp; </span>Tight accommodation of the folded mature region of a substrate protein by TatB contributes to the productive binding of Tat substrates to TatBC (<span id='ref34548058.29593092' class='cite'>Ulfig and Freudl 2018</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> TatC has been shown to serve as a specificity determinant for protein secretion via the Tat system (<span id='ref1275.11007775' class='cite'>Jongbloed <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>).  However, other proteins may play a role in recognition of the 'twin arginine' motif (<span id='ref1943.11309116' class='cite'>Oresnik <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  For example, DmsD is a Tat leader binding protein that interacts with TatB and TatC (<span id='ref2558.12813051' class='cite'>Papish <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  The energy-coupling mechanism for transport involves the pmf in both chloroplasts and <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref1271.10087917' class='cite'>Dalbey and Robinson, 1999</span>; <span id='ref1265.9367960' class='cite'>Settles <em>et al</em>., 1997</span>; 1998). The <em>E. coli</em> TatA may be capable of flipping orientation in the membrane so that its C-terminus is either in the cytoplasm or in the periplasm (<span id='ref2848.14701831' class='cite'>Gouffi <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>), but the physiological significance of this observation is not known. </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The heteromultimeric TatA<sub>d</sub></span>/TatC<sub>d</sub> of <em>B. subtilis</em> has been studied with respect to the functions of its two subunits (<span id='ref3427.16698798' class='cite'>Schreiber <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  TatAd localizes to the cytosol or membrane.  Soluble TatA can bind to the twin arg signal peptide of pre-PhoD prior to membrane integration.  It recruits the substrate protein to the membrane by interaction with TatC.  TatC (1) facilitates the membrane association of TatA and (2) stabilizes it (<span id='ref3427.16698798' class='cite'>Schreiber <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>). <em>B. subtilis </em>has two Tat complexes, each with two Tat subunits, TatA and TatC. TatAdCd and TatAyCy transport different substrate proteins (<span id='ref6552.19049517' class='cite'>Barnett <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). PhoD is secreted by the TatAdCd complex whereas YwbN is secreted by the TatAyCy complex (<span id='ref6644.19383693' class='cite'>Eijlander <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).</p>
<p>The Tat systems in most Gram-positive bacteria consists of TatA and TatC. TatA is a 
bifunctional subunit, which can form a protein-conducting channel by 
self-oligomerization and can also participate in substrate recognition. Hu et al. (<span id='ref7249.20726548' class='cite'>2010</span>) reported the solution structure of the TatA(d)
 protein from <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> by NMR spectroscopy, the first structure
 of the Tat system at atomic resolution. TatA(d) shows an L-shaped 
structure formed by a transmembrane helix and an amphipathic helix, 
while the C-terminal tail is largely unstructured. These results 
support the postulated topology of TatA(d) in which the transmembrane 
helix is inserted into the lipid bilayer while the amphipathic helix 
lies at the membrane-water interface. Moreover, the structure of TatA(d)
 revealed the structural importance of several conserved residues at the
 hinge region, thus shedding new light on further elucidation of the 
protein transport mechanism.&nbsp; The Tat system in <em>Streptomyces</em> species functions in the assembly of the cytochrome bc1 complex (<span id='ref10413.24142258' class='cite'>Hopkins <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).</p>
<p>In <em>E. coli</em>, substrate proteins initially bind to the integral membrane TatBC complex which then recruits the protein TatA to effect translocation.  Substrates bind on the periphery of the TatBC complex, causing a reduction in the diameter of TatBC. Although the TatBC complex contains multiple copies of the signal peptide-binding TatC protomer, purified TatBC-SufI complexes contain only 1 or 2 SufI molecules according to <span id='ref6734.19666509' class='cite'>Tarry <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>.&nbsp; The Tat system of <em>E. coli </em>has been reviewed (<span id='ref8946.22683878' class='cite'>Palmer and Berks 2012</span>; <span id='ref8521.22411976' class='cite'>Fr&ouml;bel <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; TatBC complexes, localized to the cell poles, function with the chaparone protein, DmsD (<span id='ref10626.20169075' class='cite'>Kostecki <em>et al.</em> 2010</span>).&nbsp; TatA assembly and
oligomerization occurs in response to substrate availability and can be reversed only by substrate transport. In contrast to TatA, the oligomeric
states of TatB and TatC are not affected by substrate or the PMF, although TatB
oligomerization requires TatC (<span id='ref10633.24003141' class='cite'>Alcock <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). TatA and TatB generate a hydrophobic mismatch important for the function and assembly of the Tat translocon in <em>E. coli</em>. Interacting short TMSs of larger TatA assemblies thin the membrane, and together with the laterally-aligned tilted amphipathic &alpha;-helicies, generated a deep V-shaped membrane groove, important for function (<span id='ref34554997.35809643' class='cite'>Mehner-Breitfeld <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p>A TatBC subcomplex and a homomeric TatA subcomplex comprise the TatABC complex (<span id='ref8521.22411976' class='cite'>Fr&ouml;bel <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>). TatB and TatC coordinately recognize twin-arginine signal peptides and accommodate them in membrane-embedded binding pockets. Binding of the signal sequence to the Tat translocase requires the proton-motive force (PMF). When targeted in this manner, folded twin-arginine precursors induce homo-oligomerization of TatB and TatA. Ultimately, this leads to the formation of a transmembrane protein conduit that possibly consists of a pore-like TatA structure. The translocation step again is dependent on the PMF. The <em>E. coli</em>&nbsp;TatA and TatB proteins have a stable N-out C-in topology in intact cells (<span id='ref8522.22399293' class='cite'>Koch <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>).&nbsp; It has been proposed that the TatA transmembrane pore could self-assemble via intra- and intermolecular salt bridges (<span id='ref9619.23332763' class='cite'>Walther <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).</p>
<p>Membrane protein assembly is a fundamental process in all cells. The membrane-bound Rieske iron-sulfur protein is an essential component of the cytochrome bc(1) and cytochrome b(6)f complexes, and it is exported across the energy-coupling membranes of bacteria and plants in a folded conformation in the (Tat) pathway. Although the Rieske protein in most organisms is a monotopic membrane protein, in actinobacteria, it is a polytopic protein with three TMSs. The Rieske protein of <em>Streptomyces coelicolor</em> requires both the Sec and the Tat pathways for assembly: the initial two TMSs integrated into the membrane in a Sec-dependent manner, whereas integration of the third TMS, and thus the correct orientation of the iron-sulfur domain, require the activity of the Tat translocase (<span id='ref9163.23045547' class='cite'>Keller <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Electron microscopy displayed TatA complexes in direct contact with the membrane-stabilizing 
PspA protein. PspB and PspC were important for the TatA-PspA contact, but the 
activator <span class='highlight'>protein</span> PspF was not involved in the PspA-TatA interaction, demonstrating that 
basal levels of PspA already interact with TatA (<span id='ref9195.22689583' class='cite'>Mehner <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). Elevated TatA levels 
caused <span class='highlight'>membrane</span> stress that induced a strictly PspBC- and PspF-dependent up-regulation 
of PspA. TatA complexes were found to destabilize membranes under these 
conditions. At native TatA levels, PspA deficiency clearly affected 
anaerobic TMAO respiratory growth, suggesting that energetic costs for <span class='highlight'>transport</span> of large Tat <span class='highlight'>substrates</span> such as TMAO reductase can become growth limiting in the absence of 
PspA (<span id='ref9195.22689583' class='cite'>Mehner <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). The physiological role of PspA recruitment to TatA may therefore 
be the control of <span class='highlight'>membrane</span> stress at active translocons.</p>
<p>Each subunit of TatA consists of a transmembrane segment, an amphiphilic helix (APH), and a C-terminal densely charged region (DCR). The sequence of charges in the DCR is complementary to the charge pattern on the APH, suggesting that the protein can be 'zipped up' by a ladder of seven salt bridges (<span id='ref9619.23332763' class='cite'>Walther <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). The length of the resulting hairpin matches the lipid bilayer thickness; hence a transmembrane pore could self-assemble via intra- and intermolecular salt bridges. The monomer-oligomer equilibrium of specific charge mutants was monitored (<span id='ref9619.23332763' class='cite'>Walther <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). Similar 'charge zippers' were proposed for other membrane-associated proteins, e.g., the biofilm-inducing peptide TisB, the human antimicrobial peptide dermcidin, and the pestiviral E(RNS) protein.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Tat-mediated protein translocation initiates by signal peptide recognition and
substrate binding to the TatBC complex. Upon formation of the TatBC-substrate protein complex,
TatA subunits are recruited and form the protein translocation pore. TatB forms a tight complex with TatC at 1:1 molar ratio with multiple
copies of both proteins. Cross-linking experiments demonstrate that TatB functions in tetrameric
units and interacts with both TatC and substrate proteins. The
solution structure of TatB in DPC micelles has been determined by Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR)
spectroscopy (<span id='ref10976.24699374' class='cite'>Zhang <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>). The structure shows an extended 'L-shape' conformation comprising four
helices: a transmembrane helix (TMH) alpha1, an amphipathic helix (APH) alpha2, and two solvent
exposed helices alpha3 and alpha4. The packing of TMH and APH is relatively rigid, whereas helices
alpha3 and alpha4 display notably higher mobility. The observed floppiness of helices alpha3 and
alpha4 allows TatB to sample a large conformational space, thus providing high structural plasticity
to interact with substrate proteins of different sizes and shapes (<span id='ref10976.24699374' class='cite'>Zhang <em>et al.</em> 2014</span>).</p>
<p>Redox Enzyme Maturation Proteins (REMPs) are system specific chaperones, which play roles
in the maturation of Tat-dependent respiratory enzymes. <span id='ref34545493.26826271' class='cite'>Kuzniatsova <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> applied the in vivo bacterial two-hybrid technique to investigate interaction of REMPs with the TatBC proteins, finding that all but
the formate dehydrogenase REMP dock to TatB or TatC. They focused on the NarJ subfamily; DmsD,
the REMP for dimethyl sulfoxide reductase in <em>E. coli,</em> had previously been shown to interact with TatB and
TatC. These REMPs interact with TatC cytoplasmic loops 1, 2 and 4 with the exception
of NarJ that only interacts with loops 1 and 4. An in vitro isothermal titration calorimetry study was
applied to confirm the evidence of interactions between TatC fragments and DmsD chaperone. Using a
peptide overlapping array, it was shown that the different NarJ subfamily REMPs interact with
different regions of the TatB cytoplasmic domains. Thus, REMP chaperones play a role
in targeting respiratory enzymes to the Tat system (<span id='ref34545493.26826271' class='cite'>Kuzniatsova <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>In <em>E. coli, </em>TatBC comprise the substrate receptor complex, and active Tat translocases are formed by the substrate-induced association of TatA oligomers with this receptor. Proteins are targeted to TatBC by their twin arginine signal peptides.&nbsp;&nbsp;<span id='ref34546489.28223511' class='cite'>Huang <em>et al.</em> 2017</span> isolated substitutions, locating to the transmembrane helix of TatB, that restored transport activity to Tat signal peptides with inactivating twin arginine substitutions. A subset of these variants also suppressed inactivating substitutions in the signal peptide binding site on TatC. The suppressors did not function by restoring detectable signal peptide binding to the TatBC complex. Instead, site-specific cross-linking experiments indicated that the suppressor substitutions induced conformational changes in the complex and movement of the TatB subunit. The TatB F13Y substitution was associated with the strongest suppressing activity, even allowing transport of a Tat substrate lacking a signal peptide. In vivo analysis using a TatA-YFP fusion showed that the TatB F13Y substitution resulted in signal peptide-independent assembly of the Tat translocase. <span id='ref34546489.28223511' class='cite'>Huang <em>et al.</em> 2017</span> concluded that Tat signal peptides play roles in substrate targeting and in triggering assembly of the active translocase. TatA organization in the inner membrane changes in response to changes in Tat A, B, and C subunit stoichiometries (<span id='ref34546567.28280110' class='cite'>Smith <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).</p>
<p>A subset of membrane proteins have globular, cofactor-containing extracytoplasmic domains requiring the dual action of the co-translational Sec and post-translational Tat pathways for integration. <span id='ref34547960.28513434' class='cite'>Tooke <em>et al.</em> 2017</span> identified new families of membrane proteins that are dual Sec-Tat-targeted. A predicted heme-molybdenum cofactor-containing protein, and a complex polyferredoxin protein each requires the concerted action of these two translocases for assembly. The mechanism of handover from he Sec to the Tat pathway requires relatively low hydrophobicity of the Tat-dependent transmembrane domain. This, coupled with the presence of C-terminal positive charges, results in abortive insertion of this transmembrane domain by the Sec pathway and its subsequent release at the cytoplasmic side of the membrane. These results point to a simple unifying mechanism governing the assembly of dual targeted membrane proteins (<span id='ref34547960.28513434' class='cite'>Tooke <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>The Tat pathway transports almost the entire secretome in haloarchaea. The TatC receptor component of this pathway shows greater variation in membrane topology in haloarchaea than in other organisms. The presence of a unique fourteen-transmembrane TatC homolog (TatCt) in haloarchaea indicates a strong correlation between the additional homologs and the large number of substrates transported via the haloarchaeal Tat pathway. Various combinations of TatC homologs with different topologies-TatCo, TatCt, TatCn, and TatCx have been observed in haloarchaea. In this report, on the basis of these combinations&nbsp;<span id='ref34549874.31193317' class='cite'>Ghosh <em>et al.</em> 2019</span> have segregated all haloarchaeal Tat substrates into two groups. The first group consists of substrates that are transported by TatCt alone, whereas the second group consists of substrates that are transported by the other TatC homologs (TatCo, TatCn, and TatCx). The various haloarchaea TatA components also shows the possible segregation towards the substrates.</p>
