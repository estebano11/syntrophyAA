<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.67 The Oligopeptide Transporter (OPT) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The OPT family consists of functionally well characterized oligopeptide (3-8 amino acid) transporters. One of the yeast homologues is the sexual differentiation process (ISP4) protein of <em>Schizosaccharomyces pombe</em>.&nbsp; <em>S. cerevisiae</em> and <em>S. pombe</em> each possess three paralogues of the OPT family. Two transporters from <em>S. cerevisiae</em>, one from <em>S. pombe</em>, and one from <em>Candida albicans</em> have been functionally characterized, and all are peptide uptake systems. Homologues are also found in plants, bacteria and archaea (<span id='ref7689.21347612' class='cite'>Gomolplitinant and Saier, 2011</span>).</span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>The prokaryotic homologues are very distant, being revealed only upon PSI-BLAST iterations, and they are also uncharacterized functionally.  Energy coupling probable involves H<sup>+</sup></span> symport. While the full-length yeast proteins are reported to be 700-900 residues long and exhibit 12 putative TMSs, bacterial homologue from <em>H. influenzae</em> is 633 amino acyl residues long and exhibits 15 putative TMSs (<span id='ref7689.21347612' class='cite'>Gomolplitinant and Saier, 2011</span>).</p>
<p>Eight<em> opt</em> genes encode putative oligopeptide transporters in <em>Candida albicans</em> (e.g. see OPT1, 2.A.67.1.1), almost all of which are represented by polymorphic alleles. <em>opt 1,2,3&Delta; </em>triple mutants have a severe growth defect, which was rescued by reintroduction of a single copy of OPT1, OPT2 or OPT3.The various oligopeptide transporters differ in their substrate preferences as shown by the ability of strains expressing specific <em>opt </em>genes to grow on peptides of defined length and sequence. These transporters can take up tri-octa peptides, each one with differing specificities (<span id='ref4156.16629678' class='cite'>Reuss and Morschhauser, 2006</span>).</p>
<p>An Fe<sup>3+</sup>-phytosiderophore uptake system of <em>Zea mays,</em> also known as 'yellow stripe1' (YS1) is encoded by the <em>ys1</em> gene, the expression of which is increased in both roots and shoots under iron deficient conditions (<span id='ref1286.11201743' class='cite'>Curie <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>). When expressed in a mutant yeast lacking its native iron uptake system, it corrects the defect specifically in Fe<sup>3+</sup>-phytosiderophore media.  YS1 has a glutamine-rich N-terminus that might function in Fe<sup>3+</sup> binding.  It has been shown to be a proton symporter that takes up phytosiderophore- and nicotianamine-chelated metals (<span id='ref2849.14699112' class='cite'>Schaaf <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>). Nicotianamine is a structural analogue of phytosiderophores and is also an intracellular metal chelator.</p>
<p>Nine genes (designated YS-like genes 1-9 or YS1-9) in <em>Arabidopsis thaliana</em> encode close homologues of YS1 (<span id='ref1286.11201743' class='cite'>Curie <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>). These proteins and nine other distant OPT family homologues are of about the same size (about 600 aas) and exhibit 12-13 putative TMSs. Some of these plant proteins may be more similar to the bacterial and archaeal homologues than to the yeast peptide porters. The plant homologues have been reviewed by <span id='ref6597.16382870' class='cite'>Lubkowitz (2006)</span>.<span id='ref6597.16382870' class='cite'><br /></span></p>
