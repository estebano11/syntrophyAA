<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong> 2.A.68  The p-Aminobenzoyl-glutamate Transporter (AbgT) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The AbgT family consists of the AbgT (YdaH) protein of <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref1933.9829935' class='cite'>Hussein <em>et al</em>., 1998</span>) and the MtrF drug exporter of <em>Neisseria gonorrhoeae</em> (<span id='ref3086.15901695' class='cite'>Folster and Shafer, 2005</span>).  The former protein is apparently cryptic in wild-type cells, but when expressed on a high copy number plasmid, or when expressed at higher levels due to mutation, it appeared to allow uptake (Km = 123 nM) and subsequent utilization of p-aminobenzoyl-glutamate as a source of p-aminobenzoate for p-aminobenzoate auxotrophs (<span id='ref3863.17307853' class='cite'>Carter <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).  p-Aminobenzoate is a constituent of and a precursor for the biosynthesis of folic acid.  MtrF was annotated as a putative drug efflux pump (<span id='ref3086.15901695' class='cite'>Folster and Shafer, 2005</span>). </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> AbgT is 510 amino acyl residues long and has 12-13 putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs). MtrF is 522 aas long and has 11 or 12 putative TMSs.  They are distant members of the Ion Transporter (IT) superfamily (<span id='ref2781.14643936' class='cite'>Prakash <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>; <span id='ref1934.10627041' class='cite'>Rabus <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).The 3-d structures of MtrF and a YdaH homologue have been solved, and functional studies show that it is a drug exporter.&nbsp; The 3-d structure shows that it has 9 TMSs with hairpin entry loops (<span id='ref34543959.25818299' class='cite'>Su <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp; </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The <em>abgT</em> gene is preceded by two genes, <em>abgA</em> and <em>abgB,</em> which code for homologous amino acyl amino hydrolases and hydrolyze p-aminobenzoyl glutamate to p-aminobenzoate and glutamate (Carter et al,. 2007). Because of the structural similarity of p-aminobenzoyl-glutatmate to peptides, and the enzymatic activities of the <em>abgA</em> and <em>abgB</em> gene products, it has been suggested that AbgT is also a peptide transporter (<span id='ref3863.17307853' class='cite'>Carter <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Demonstration of an energy requirement suggested an H<sup>+</sup></span>-dependent mechanism (<span id='ref3863.17307853' class='cite'>Carter <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).  Expression of these genes is regulated by AbgR and an unknown effector.</p>
<p>As noted above, the AbgT family of transporters has been thought to contribute to bacterial folate biosynthesis by
importing the catabolite p-aminobenzoyl-glutamate for producing folate.
Approximately 13,000 putative family members were identified in 2015 (<span id='ref34544692.26443496' class='cite'>Delmar and Yu 2015</span>). The X-ray structures of the full-length <em>Alcanivorax borkumensis</em> YdaH (AbgT) and
<em>Neisseria gonorrhoeae</em> MtrF proteins. The structures revealed that these two transporters
assemble as dimers with architectures distinct from all other families of transporters for which 3-d structures were available. Both YdaH
and MtrF are bowl-shaped dimers with a solvent-filled basin extending from the cytoplasm halfway
across the membrane bilayer. The protomers of YdaH and MtrF contain nine transmembrane helices and
two hairpins  (<span id='ref34544692.26443496' class='cite'>Delmar and Yu 2015</span>) which suggested a plausible pathway for substrate transport. A
combination of the crystal structure, genetic analyses and substrate accumulation assays indicated
that both YdaH and MtrF behave as exporters, capable of removing the folate metabolite
p-aminobenzoic acid from bacterial cells. In fact, it was shown that both YdaH and MtrF participate as antibiotic efflux pumps, mediating bacterial resistance to sulfonamide antimetabolite drugs. Possibly,
many AbgT-family transporters act as exporters, conferring resistance to
sulfonamides  (<span id='ref34544692.26443496' class='cite'>Delmar and Yu 2015</span>).</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction initially proposed for AbgT is:</p>
<p align='center'>p-aminobenzoyl-glutamate (out) + nH<sup>+</sup> (out) &rarr; p-aminobenzoyl-glutamate (in) + nH<sup>+</sup> (in)</p>
<p style='text-align: left;'>but the more recently proposed transport reaction is:</p>
<p align='center'>Sulfonamide drugs (in) + H<sup>+</sup> (out) &rarr; Sulfonamide drugs (out) + H<sup>+</sup> (in)</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><HR>This family belongs to the <A HREF="superfamily.php?id=1">IT Superfamily</A>.<BR><HR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Bolla, J.R., C.C.
