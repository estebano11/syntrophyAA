<p align='CENTER'><strong> 2.A.80  The Tripartite Tricarboxylate Transporter (TTT) Family </strong></p>
<p>A single member of the TTT family has been both functionally characterized and sequenced, the TctABC transporter of <em>Salmonella typhimurium.</em> This system includes three components: TctA, a large (504 aa) transmembrane protein with 12 predicted TMSs, TctB, a small (144 aa) transmembrane protein with 4 putative TMSs, and TctC, a periplasmic tricarboxylate-binding receptor (325 aas).  In these respects, TctABC resembles DctMQP of the TRAP-T family (TC #2.A.56).  However, there is little or no significant sequence similarity between these two systems or between members of the two different families.  TctC shows some sequence and motif similarities to the phosphonate (and other) binding receptors of bacteria (Tam and Saier, 1993).  The homologues of these three proteins are found in Gram-negative bacteria, Gram-positive bacteria, and archaea.  TctA homologues are the most conserved components of Tct family systems while TctB homologues are the least conserved.</p>
<p>All TctA homologues exhibit a well-conserved motif:</p>
<p align='center'>(Hy)<sub>6</sub> G (Hy)<sub>3</sub> G* (Hy)<sub>3</sub> G* (Hy)<sub>2</sub> P* G* (Hy) <br /> (Hy = a hydrophobic residue; * = a fully conserved residue.)</p>
<p>This motif comprises TMS 1, but it can also be found, less well conserved, in TMS 7.  This fact suggests that these proteins arose by an internal gene duplication event where a 6 TMS-encoding element duplicated to give a 12 TMS-encoding gene.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> TctC has been purified and studied (<span id='ref1859.383235' class='cite'>Sweet <em>et al</em>., 1979</span>, <span id='ref239.6141166' class='cite'>1984</span>).  It binds fluorocitrate, citrate, isocitrate and cis-aconitate with micromolar affinities and decreasing affinities in that order.  Binding of citrate to this dimeric protein proved to be Na<sup>+</sup>-dependent while divalent cations (Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup>) were inhibitory (<span id='ref1859.383235' class='cite'>Sweet <em>et al</em>., 1979</span>).  TctC homologues are found in large numbers of species of <em>Bordetella</em> and other &beta;-</span>proteobacteria (<span id='ref2333.12562821' class='cite'>Antoine <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>).  Transport Kms for fluorocitrate, citrate and isocitrate are about 3 &mu;M, 50 &mu;M and 170 &mu;M, respectively, and the Ka for Na<sup>+</sup> is 0.4 mM (<span id='ref801.7006757' class='cite'>Ashton <em>et al</em>., 1980)</span>.  Uptake is dependent on Na<sup>+</sup> and the membrane potential and is completely blocked by the addition of protonophores.  Mutants lacking the Tct system grow poorly or not at all on all three tricarboxylates and are resistant to fluorocitrates (<span id='ref389.6113534' class='cite'>Somers <em>et al</em>., 1981</span>).  The system is inducible when citrate or isocitrate, or to a lesser extent, cis-aconitate, is present in the growth medium (M.H. Saier, Jr., unpublished results).  Induction depends on the sensor kinase/response regulator pair, TctE/TctD, encoded by genes that map adjacent to the <em>tctABC</em>operon (<span id='ref1964.2838461' class='cite'>Widenhorn <em>et al</em>., 1988</span>).These systems have been reviewed (<span id='ref10892.14499931' class='cite'>Winnen <em>et al.</em> 2003</span>).</p>
<p>The generalized transport reaction probably catalyzed by TctABC is:</p>
<p align='CENTER'>Tricarboxylate (out) + nNa<sup>+</sup> (out)  &rarr;  tricarboxylate (in) + nNa<sup>+</sup> (in).</p>
