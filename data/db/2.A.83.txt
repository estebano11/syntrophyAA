<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.83  The Na<sup>+</sup>-dependent Bicarbonate Transporter (SBT) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Cyanobacteria possess a Na<sup>+</sup>-dependent bicarbonate (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup>) uptake system that is induced when the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration is low (<span id='ref2586.11904298' class='cite'>Shibata <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  The protein that catalyzes uptake of HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> is SbtA (slr1512) of Synechocystis PCC 6803.  It is 374 aas long and possesses 10 putative TMSs which probably arose by an intragenic duplication event where the primordial gene encoded a 5 TMS element.  Inactivation of a homologue of NtpJ of <em>Enterococcus hirae</em> (TC #2.A.38.4.1) (slr1509), which appears to function with SbtA, prevents growth of the bacteria in the presence of high Na<sup>+</sup> (&gt;100 mM).  This suggests that slr1509 is a Na<sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup> antiporter that in part generates the sodium motif force (smf) required for the uptake of HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> via the SbtA protein.&nbsp; </span><span id='ref34557754.38258089' class='cite'>Kim and Hong 2024</span> focused on recently identified modulatory signaling components that 
regulate the expression and activity of bicarbonate transporters.</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> There are two types of SbtA homologues in cyanobacteria, but all appear to function by a mechanism dependent on an NtpJ homologue (<span id='ref2586.11904298' class='cite'>Shibata <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>). </span>SbtA and its homologues display a common topology of ten transmembrane segments (TMSs). These proved to have arisen by an intragenic duplication event from an ancestral gene encoding a five TMS protein product (von <span id='ref4949.15263814' class='cite'>Rozycki <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>). A region of SbtA shows sufficient similarity to 10 TMS ABC-type integral membrane transport proteins to suggest a common origin. Phylogenetic analyses of the SbtA family revealed two clusters of cyanobacterial homologues with all non-cyanobacterial family members outside of these two clusters. The tree topology suggests that SbtA family members display multiple transport functions (von <span id='ref4949.15263814' class='cite'>Rozycki <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The transport reaction catalyzed by SbtA is:</span></p>
<p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'> HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> (out) + nNa<sup>+</sup> (out)  &rarr;  HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> (in) + nNa<sup>+</sup> (in) </span></p>
