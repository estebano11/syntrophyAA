<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><strong>2.A.87 The Prokaryotic Riboflavin Transporter (P-RFT) Family</strong></p>
<p>The RFT family consists of 5 TMS proteins of about 200 aas.  The proteins from <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> (YpaA; C. Vogel, unpublished results) and <em>Lactococcus lactis</em> (RibU; <span id='ref3444.16585736' class='cite'>Burgess <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>) have been characterized and shown to be riboflavin transporters.  Many results had suggested them to be riboflavin transporters before the recent definitive experiments were performed. The RFT family belongs to the BART superfamily (Mansour et al., 2007)</p>
<p>The YpaA protein of <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> [<span id='ref3440.6777606' class='cite'>Cecchini and Kearney, 1980</span>; <span id='ref3441.10529804' class='cite'>Gelfand <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>; <span id='ref3442.' class='cite'>Kreneva <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>; <span id='ref3443.12136096' class='cite'>Vitreschak <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>; C. Vogl et al., unpublished results) was first thought to be a riboflavin transporter because the regulatory region upstream of its structural gene included an RFN sequence which was known to be important for the regulation of riboflavin biosynthetic gene expression in response to cytoplasmic riboflavin (<span id='ref3441.10529804' class='cite'>Gelfand <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).  Many putative riboflavin transporter genes, in other low G+C Gram-positive organisms, showing homology to <em>ypaA</em>, also have an RFN element in their regulatory region, strengthening the argument that these proteins are riboflavin transporters (<span id='ref3441.10529804' class='cite'>Gelfand <em>et al</em>., 1999</span>).  Additionally, based on hydropathy plots (see Fig. 1A), YpaA, like RibU,  has 5 putative transmembrane segments (TMSs) consistent with a role in transmembrane transport.  Based on these observations, at least some RibU homologues appeared to be riboflavin transporters.</p>
<p>In 1976, <span id='ref3437.853026' class='cite'>Ledesma <em>et al.</em></span> demonstrated that several <em>Lactobacillus</em> species are dependent on external riboflavin for growth.  This discovery was confirmed and extended by M&oslash;retr&oslash; et al. (<span id='ref3438.' class='cite'>1998</span>).  These findings suggested that lactobacilli cannot synthesize but can transport riboflavin (<span id='ref3439.' class='cite'>Koser, 1968</span>).  Recently, functional data showed that RibU of <em>Lactococcus lactis</em> is a riboflavin transporter (<span id='ref3444.16585736' class='cite'>Burgess <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  The protein is a 5 TMS protein homologous to the YpaA protein of <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> (see below).  In contrast to wild-type <em>L. lactis</em>, a <em>ribU</em> mutant could not take up or use exogenous riboflavin.  The mutant also exhibited altered transcriptional control of the riboflavin biosynthetic operon, <em>ribGBAH</em> (<span id='ref3444.16585736' class='cite'>Burgess <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  Competitive inhibition suggested that flavin mononucleotide (FMN) and the toxic riboflavin analogue, roseoflavin, might also be substrates, but this was not demonstrated.  A uniport mechanism of action was suggested (<span id='ref3444.16585736' class='cite'>Burgess <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).</p>
<p>Almost all members of the RFT family derive from Firmicutes, but some are from Euryarchaeota and from actinobacteria, and one is from <em>Thermotoga maritima.</em> Surprisingly, no RFT family member is from a proteobacterium, and none of the Gram-negative bacterial subdivisions except Thermotogales is represented.</p>
<p>The majority of the organisms represented have a single RFT family member, but several have two, and one organism, <em>Clostridium acetobutylicum</em>, has three.  All of the bacteria with 2 or 3 paralogues are firmicutes: Clostridia, Lactobacilli, Lactococci, Streptococci, <em>Pediococcus pentosaceus</em> and <em>Thermoanaerobacter tengcongensis.</em></p>
