<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>2.A.89  The Vacuolar Iron Transporter (VIT) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Members of the VIT family (DUF125) have been characterized in yeast (Ccc1; TC #2.A.89.1.1) and plants (Vit1; TC #2.A.89.1.2) (<span id='ref3570.17082420' class='cite'>Kim <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  The former transports Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> into the vacuole where Fe<sup>2+</sup> is stored, while the latter has been shown to transport Fe<sup>2+</sup>.  Mutants accumulate excess Fe<sup>2+</sup> in the cytoplasm which can be toxic. Excess Fe<sup>2+</sup> is taken up into the vacuole.  Vacuolar iron storage is critical for seedling development, and so is Vit1.  Vit2 and Ccc1 have 5 putative TMSs in a 2 + 2 + 1 arrangement.  Homologues are found in eukaryotes, bacteria and archaea where they may have a 1 + 2 + 2 arrangement (e.g., TC #2.A.89.2.1 from <em>Pyrococcus abyssi</em>) or a 1 + 2 + 3 arrangement (e.g., TC #2.A.89.3.1 from <em>Burkholderia phytofirmans</em>).  Other bacterial and archaeal homologues are about 250 aas in length.  A 270 aa homologue EAN12646 from <em>Frankia</em> sp. has 5 TMSs in a 2 + 3 arrangement.  A 399 residue homologue from <em>Ustilago maydis</em> (EAK82927) has 5 putative TMSs in a 2 + 3 arrangement with two less hydrophobic peaks between the 2 and 3 strongly hydrophobic peaks.  Of the two pairs of 2 TMSs, the first peak is smaller than the second peak in both cases, suggesting that these proteins arose by an intragenic duplication followed by addition of other domains.  The separation between the 2 pairs is variable (&gt;100 aas to &lt;20 aas).  These proteins may have an extended hydrophilic N-terminus. Eukaryotic </span><span style='font-family: Times;'><span style='font-family: Times;'>VIT family proteins probably function by an H<sup>+</sup> antiport carrier-type mechanism accounting for vacuolar uptake. </span></span></p>
<p>Ccc1/VIT1 homologs are widely distributed among organisms with the exception of animals. The recent elucidation of the crystal structure of a Ccc1/VIT1 plant ortholog has enabled the identification of both conserved and species-specific motifs required for metal transport. In <em>Saccharomyces cerevisiae,</em> multiple transcription factors including Yap5 and Msn2/Msn4 contribute to the expression of CCC1 under high-iron conditions. <em>S. cerevisiae</em> strains express a partially functional Ccc1 protein that renders them sensitive to iron. Different regulatory mechanisms have been described for non-Saccharomycetaceae Ccc1 homologs (<span id='ref34552400.33304466' class='cite'>Sorribes-Dauden <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
