<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><strong>3.A.10 The H<sup>+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup>-</strong><strong>translocating Pyrophosphatase (M<sup>+</sup><strong>-PPase) Family</strong></strong></p>
<p><strong><strong> </strong> </strong></p>
<p>Proteins of the H<sup>+</sup>-PPase family are found in the vacuolar (tonoplast) membranes of higher plants, algae, and protozoa, and in both bacteria and archaea.  They are therefore ancient enzymes.  The plant enzymes probably pump one H<sup>+</sup> upon hydrolysis of pyrophosphate, thereby generating a proton motive force, positive and acidic in the tonoplast lumen.  They establish a pmf of similar magnitude to that generated by the H<sup>+</sup>-translocating ATPases in the same vacuolar membrane.  The bacterial and archaeal proteins may catalyze fully reversible reactions, thus being able to synthesize pyrophosphate when the pmf is sufficient.  The enzyme from <em>R. rubrum</em> contributes to the pmf when light intensity is insufficient to generate a pmf sufficient in magnitude to support rapid ATP synthesis. Both C-termini of the dimeric subunits of V-PPase are on the same side of the membrane, and they are close to each other (<span id='ref7142.19614743' class='cite'>Liu <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). Transmembrane domain 6 of vacuolar H<sup>+</sup>-pyrophosphatase appears to mediate both protein targeting and proton transport (<span id='ref7239.20937245' class='cite'>Pan <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>).</p>
<p>Eukaryotic members of the H<sup>+</sup>-PPase family are large proteins of about 770 amino acyl residues with fifteen or sixteen putative transmembrane &alpha;-helical spanners (TMSs).  The N-termini are predicted to be in the vacuolar lumen while the C-termini are thought to be in the cytoplasm.  These proteins exhibit a region that shows convincing sequence similarity to the regions surrounding the DCCD-sensitive glutamate in the C-terminal regions of the c-subunits of F-type ATPases (TC #3.A.2).  The H<sup>+</sup>-pyrophosphatase of <em>Streptomyces coelicolor</em> has been shown to have a 17 TMS topology with the substrate binding domain exposed to the cytoplasm.  The C-terminus is hydrophilic with a single C-terminal TMS.  The basic structure is believed to have 16 TMSs with several large cytoplasmic loops containing functional motifs (<span id='ref3033.15187077' class='cite'>Mimura <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).  Several acidic residues in the <em>Arabidopsis</em> H<sup>+</sup>-PPase have been shown to be important for function.  Some plants possess closely related H<sup>+</sup>-PPase isoforms.  These enzymes have the enzyme commission number EC 3.6.1.1. The cellular toolbox for the coordinated regulation of the internal pH involves 14-3-3 
proteins (TC# 8.A.98) phosphorylation events, ion concentrations, and 
redox-conditions (<span id='ref34555195.34177988' class='cite'>Cosse and Seidel 2021</span>).</p>
<p>Full-length members of the H<sup>+</sup>-PPase family have been sequenced from numerous bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes.  These H<sup>+</sup> pumping enzymes, which are probably homodimeric, have been reported to fall into two phylogenetic subfamilies (<span id='ref2097.11956221' class='cite'>Belogurov <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  One subfamily invariably contains a conserved cysteine (Cys<sup>222</sup>) and includes all known K<sup>+</sup>-independent H<sup>+</sup>-PPases while the other has another conserved cysteine (Cys<sup>573</sup>) but lacks Cys<sup>222</sup> and includes all known K<sup>+</sup>-dependent H<sup>+</sup>-PPases (Belogurov et al., 2000).  All H<sup>+</sup>-PPases require Mg<sup>2+</sup>, and those from plant vacuoles, acidocalcisomes of protozoa and fermentative bacteria require mM K<sup>+</sup>.  Those from respiratory and photosynthetic bacteria as well as archaea are less dependent on K<sup>+</sup>.  However, exceptions may exist (Belogurov et al., 2000).  It is not sure whether K<sup>+</sup> is transported.</p>
<p>The archaeon, <em>Methanosarcina mazei</em> G&ouml;1, encodes within its genome two H<sup>+</sup><span>-translocating pyrophosphatases, Mvp1 and Mvp2.  Mvp1 resembles bacterial PPases while Mvp2 resembles plant PPases (<span id='ref2074.15803653' class='cite'>B&auml;umer <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  Mvp2 was shown to translocate 1 H<sup>+</sup><span> per pyrophosphate hydrolyzed. </span></span>In the plant cell, the dominant proton pumps are the plasma membrane 
ATPase, the vacuolar pyrophosphatase (V-PPase), and the vacuolar-type 
ATPase (V-ATPase). All these pumps act on the cytosolic pH by pumping 
protons into the lumen of compartments or into the apoplast (<span id='ref34555195.34177988' class='cite'>Cosse and Seidel 2021</span>).</p>
<p>Some PPases from <em>Anaerostipes caccae</em>, <em>Chlorobium limicola</em>, <em>Clostridium tetani</em>, and <em>Desulfuromonas acetoxidans</em>&nbsp;have been identified as K<sup>+</sup>-dependent Na<sup>+&nbsp;</sup>transporters (<span id='ref7831.21527638' class='cite'>Luoto <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). Phylogenetic analysis led to the identification of a monophyletic clade comprising characterized and predicted Na<sup>+</sup>-transporting PPases (Na<sup>+</sup>-PPases) within the K<sup>+</sup>-dependent subfamily. H<sup>+</sup>-transporting PPases (H<sup>+</sup>-PPases) are more heterogeneous and form at least three independent clades in both subfamilies (<span id='ref7831.21527638' class='cite'>Luoto <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).</p>
<p>Lin et al. (<span id='ref8459.22456709' class='cite'>2012</span>) reported the crystal structure of a <em>Vigna radiata</em> H<sup>+</sup>-PPase (VrH<sup>+</sup>-PPase) in complex with a non-hydrolysable substrate analogue, imidodiphosphate (IDP), at 2.35&thinsp;&Aring; resolution. Each VrH<sup>+</sup>-PPase subunit consists of an integral membrane domain formed by 16 transmembrane helices. IDP is bound in the cytosolic region of each subunit and trapped by numerous charged residues and five Mg<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;ions. A previously undescribed proton translocation pathway is formed by six core transmembrane helices. Proton pumping can be initialized by PP(i) hydrolysis, and H<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;is then transported into the vacuolar lumen through a pathway consisting of Arg&thinsp;242, Asp&thinsp;294, Lys&thinsp;742 and Glu&thinsp;301. Lin et al. (<span id='ref8459.22456709' class='cite'>2012</span>) proposed a working model of the mechanism for the coupling between proton pumping and PP(i) hydrolysis by H<sup>+</sup>-PPases.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Membrane-integral pyrophosphatases (M-PPases) are crucial for the survival of plants, bacteria, and protozoan parasites. They couple pyrophosphate hydrolysis or synthesis to Na<sup>+</sup>&nbsp;or H<sup>+&nbsp;</sup>pumping. The 2.6&Aring;&nbsp;structure of <em>Thermotoga maritima</em> H<sup>+</sup>-PPase in the resting state revealed a previously unknown solution for ion pumping (<span id='ref9070.22837527' class='cite'>Kellosalo <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>). The hydrolytic center, 20 angstroms above the membrane, is coupled to the gate formed by the conserved Asp(243), Glu(246), and Lys(707) by an unusual 'coupling funnel' of six &alpha; helices. Helix 12 slides down upon substrate binding to open the gate by a simple binding-change mechanism. Below the gate, four helices form the exit channel. Superimposing helices 3 to 6, 9 to 12, and 13 to 16 suggests that M-PPases arose through gene triplication. By comparing the active sites, fluoride
inhibition data and the various models for ion transport, Kajander et al. (<span id='ref9992.23684653' class='cite'>Kajander <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>) concluded that membrane-integral PPases
probably use binding of pyrophosphate to drive pumping.&nbsp;</p>
<p>Membrane-bound pyrophosphatases (mPPases) are divided into K<sup>+</sup>,Na<sup>+</sup>-independent, Na<sup>+</sup>-regulated, and K<sup>+</sup>-dependent families. The first two families include H<sup>+</sup>-transporting mPPases (H<sup>+</sup>-PPases), whereas the last family comprises one Na<sup>+</sup>-transporting, two Na<sup>+</sup>-and H<sup>+</sup>-transporting subfamilies (Na<sup>+</sup>-PPases and Na<sup>+</sup>,H<sup>+</sup>-PPases, respectively), and three H<sup>+</sup>-transporting subfamilies (<span id='ref34547959.29519958' class='cite'>Artukka <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). Studies of the few available model mPPases suggested that K<sup>+</sup> binds to a site located adjacent to the pyrophosphate-binding site but is substituted by the epsilon-amino group of a lysine residue in the K<sup>+</sup>-independent mPPases. <span id='ref34547959.29519958' class='cite'>Artukka <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> performed a systematic analysis of the K<sup>+</sup>/Lys cationic center across all mPPase subfamilies. An Ala--&gt;Lys replacement in K<sup>+</sup>-dependent mPPases abolished the K<sup>+</sup> dependence of hydrolysis and transport activities and decreased these activities to the level (4-7%) observed for wild-type enzymes in the absence of monovalent cations. In contrast, a Lys--&gt;Ala replacement in K<sup>+</sup>,Na<sup>+</sup>-independent mPPases conferred partial K<sup>+</sup> dependence on the enzyme by unmasking an otherwise conserved K<sup>+</sup>-binding site. Na<sup>+</sup> could partially replace K<sup>+</sup> as activator of K<sup>+</sup>-dependent mPPases and the Lys--&gt;Ala variants of K<sup>+</sup>,Na<sup>+</sup>-independent mPPases. All mPPases were inhibited by excess substrate, suggesting strong negative cooperativity of active site functioning in these homodimeric enzymes. The K<sup>+</sup>/Lys center was identified as part of the mechanism underlying this effect. Possibly mPPase homodimers possess asymmetric active sites that may be an ancient prototype of the rotational binding-change mechanism of F-type ATPases (<span id='ref34547959.29519958' class='cite'>Artukka <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).</p>
<p>H<sup>+</sup>- and Na<sup>+</sup>-PPases are distributed in various organisms including 
plants, parasitic protozoa, archaea and bacteria, but are not 
present in animals or yeast (<span id='ref34549177.29534212' class='cite'>Segami <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).&nbsp; Acidification performed with the vacuolar-type 
H<sup>+</sup>-ATPase and H<sup>+</sup>-PPase is essential to maintain acidic conditions, which
 are necessary for vacuolar hydrolytic enzymes and for supplying energy 
to secondary active transporters. The physiological importance of the scavenging role of 
PPi has come to light. An overview of the main features of H<sup>+</sup>-PPases present in the 
vacuolar membrane is provided by&nbsp;<span id='ref34549177.29534212' class='cite'>Segami <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> in terms of tissue distribution in plants,
 intracellular localizations, structure-function relationships, 
biochemical potential as proton pumps and functional stability.</p>
