<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>3.A.12  The Septal DNA Translocator (S-DNA-T) Family</strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> During sporulation in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>, the SpoIIIE protein is believed to form a translocation pore at the leading edge of the nearly closed septum.  It then allows passage of the bacterial chromosome across the septum, functioning as a DNA exporter.  Thus, it exports DNA from the cell compartment in which it is expressed (<span id='ref2066.11778051' class='cite'>Sharp and Pogliano, 2002</span>).  When defective, due to mutations in the <em>spoIIIE</em> gene, only the leading portion of the chromosome (near OriC) is successfully translocated across the septum into the forespore compartment.  The <em>E. coli</em>FtsK protein is homologous to SpoIIIE, and it can free chromosomes trapped in vegetative septa.  It acts at the bacterial septum to couple chromosome segregation to cell division, pumping the DNA through the closing vegetative septum.  It has also been shown to function as a DNA motor protein that activates chromosome dimer resolution (<span id='ref2064.11832210' class='cite'>Aussel <em>et al</em>., 2002</span>).  Homologues of SpoIIIE are involved in cell to cell DNA transfer during plasmid conjugation.</span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> SpoIIIE is expressed constitutively in vegetative <em>B. subtilis </em>cells, and its homologues are found in a wide variety of bacteria, most of which do not sporulate.  These proteins may thus function generally as DNA translocation pores in vegetative prokaryotic septa, rather than just in sporulation septa.  SpoIIIE plays other roles in membrane fusion during engulfment of the forespore by the mother cell.  Marquis et al. (<span id='ref5506.18593879' class='cite'>2008</span>) have provided convincing evidence that one function of SpoIIIE is to strip off bound proteins such as RNA polymerase, transcription factors, remodeling proteins off the DNA so naked DNA enters the forespore.  They propose that the translocation-stripping activity of SpoIIIE plays a key role in reprogramming developmental gene expression in the forespore. </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> SpoIIIE and its homologues actively drive DNA transport using a mechanism dependent on ATP hydrolysis.  Thus, the carboxy terminal cytoplasmic part of SpoIIIE is a DNA-dependent ATPase.  It can track along DNA in the presence of ATP.  The amino terminal membrane-embedded part of SpoIIIE (residues 1-175 with four putative TMSs) localizes it to the division septum.  During sporulation, SpoIIIE appears to act as a DNA pump that actively moves one of the two replicated chromosomes into the prespore.  The presence of homologues in a broad range of bacteria suggests that this mechanism for active transport of DNA is widespread.&nbsp;</span>Burton et al. (<span id='ref4717.18160039' class='cite'>2007</span>) demonstrated that the two arms of the chromosome are simultaneously pumped into the forespore. Up to 70 molecules of SpoIIIE are recruited to the site of DNA translocation and assemble into complexes that could contain 12 subunits. Fusion of the septal membranes during cytokinesis precedes DNA translocation. A model for DNA transport in which the transmembrane segments of FtsK/SpoIIIE form linked DNA-conducting channels across the two lipid bilayers of the septum has been proposed (<span id='ref4717.18160039' class='cite'>Burton <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>).</p>
<p>SpoIIIE exports DNA with translocation polarity that is governed by the cell-specific regulation of its assembly, but FtsK is a reversible motor for which translocation polarity is governed by its DNA substrate. In differentiating <em>B. subtilis</em> cells, SpoIIIE assembles a complex only in the mother cell, from which DNA is exported. Becker and Pogliano (<span id='ref4916.18001347' class='cite'>2007</span>) have shown that altering chromosome architecture by <em>soj-spo0J</em> and <em>racA soj-spo0j</em> mutations allows wild-type SpoIIIE to assemble in the forespore and export the forespore chromosome. The chromosome is exported from the forespore when <em>oriC</em> fails to be trapped in the forespore. Thus, the position of <em>oriC</em> after septation determines which cell will receive the chromosome and which will assemble SpoIIIE.</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>A single plasmid-encoded protein, the septal DNA translocator TraB, is sufficient to promote conjugal plasmid transfer in mycelial streptomycetes. TraB proteins from plasmids pSG5 of <em>Streptomyces ghanaensis</em> and pSVH1 of <em>Streptomyces venezuelae</em> were characterized. TraB of pSG5 was expressed as a fusion protein with eGFP and found to be localized at the hyphal tips, which indicates that conjugation takes place at the tips of the mating mycelium. The TraB protein of pSVH1 was heterologously expressed with an N-terminal strep-tagII and purified as a soluble protein to near homogeneity. The purified protein was shown to hydrolyse ATP and to bind to a 50 bp non-coding pSVH1 sequence containing a 14 bp direct repeat. The protein-DNA complex was too large to enter an agarose gel, indicating that multimers of TraB were bound to the DNA. Denaturation of the protein-DNA complex released unprocessed plasmid DNA demonstrating that the TraB protein does not possess nicking activity. Thus, conjugal DNA transfer in streptomycetes is mediated by the septal DNA translocator TraB, a plasmid-encoded ATPase that interacts non-covalently with DNA and translocates an unprocessed double-stranded DNA molecule at the hyphal tip into the recipient.</span></p>
