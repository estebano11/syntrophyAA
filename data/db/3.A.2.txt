<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><strong>3.A.2  The H<sup>+</sup>- or Na<sup>+</sup>-translocating F-type, V-type and A-type ATPase (F-ATPase) Superfamily</strong></p>
<p>F-type ATPases are found in eukaryotic mitochondria and chloroplasts as well as in bacteria.  V-type ATPases are found in vacuoles of eukaryotes and in bacteria.  A-type ATPases are found in archaea.  All such systems are multisubunit complexes with at least 3 dissimilar subunits embedded as a complex in the membrane (F<sub>0</sub>, a:b:c = 1:2:~12) and (usually) at least 5 dissimilar subunits attached to F<sub>0</sub> (F<sub>1</sub>,  &alpha;:&beta;:&gamma;:&delta;:&epsilon; = 3:3:1:1:1 for F-type ATPases).  The eukaryotic proteins are more complicated than the bacterial enzyme complexes.  The a, b, &delta; and F<sub>1</sub> hexamer (&alpha;<sub>3</sub>&beta;<sub>3</sub>) comprise the stator.  The rotor (which consists of the c, &epsilon; and &gamma; subunits) is believed to rotate relative to the stator in response to either ATP hydrolysis by F<sub>1</sub> or proton transport through F<sub>0</sub>.  H<sup>+</sup> transport and ATP synthesis may therefore be coupled mechanically.  The F<sub>1</sub> portion of the bovine mitochondrial F-type ATPase has been solved to 2.8 &Aring; resolution.  Cross-linking studies suggest that the H<sup>+</sup> channel consists of a and c subunits where the external aqueous access channel is located to the inside of the a-subunit 4-helix bundle (<span id='ref3783.17035244' class='cite'>Schwem and Fillingame, 2006</span>).  Minimal requirements for proton translocation by the ATP synthase include a positive charge in subunit a and a weak interface between subunit a and oligomeric subunit c (<span id='ref4520.18068111' class='cite'>Ishmukhametov <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>).  These enzymes can pump H<sup>+</sup> or Na<sup>+</sup>.  A Na<sup>+ </sup>A-type ATPase from <em>Pyrococcus furiosus </em>has been described (<span id='ref5460.17614964' class='cite'>Pisa et al., 2007b</span>). ATP binding to the &epsilon;-subunit plays a regulatory role; ATP
binding may stabilize the ATPase-active form by fixing the
&epsilon;-subunit into the folded conformation (<span id='ref6793.17933866' class='cite'>Kato <em>et al.</em>, 2007</span>).&nbsp; Mechanisms of coupling H<sup>+</sup> movement to ATP synthesis, based on structural data for the a and c subunits, have been proposed (<span id='ref34543774.25707805' class='cite'>Allegretti <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp; The asymmetric features of the a subunit make it the master
regulator determining which of the two functions, ATP production or ATP dissipation,
will be performed (<span id='ref34544413.25497387' class='cite'>Nesci <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>). The structure of the a subunit (5ARA) reveals an unusual two transmembrane &alpha;-helices at a 70&deg; angle to the plane of the membrane (<span id='ref34547606.29100082' class='cite'>Tsirigos <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). The 3.6 &Aring; structure of the yeast F<sub>o </sub>domain has been solved by cryoEM, revealing how the protons travel through the complex, how the complex dimerizes, and how the dimers bend the membrane to produce cristae (<span id='ref34547613.29074581' class='cite'>Guo <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).&nbsp; The crystal structure of the <em>S. cerevisiae</em> c-subunit ring with bound oligomycin revealed the inhibitor docked on the outer face of the proton-binding sites, deep in the transmembrane region (<span id='ref34548062.29630891' class='cite'>Zhou and Faraldo-G&oacute;mez 2018</span>). A torque-coupled thermodynamic model of the F--ATPase has been proposed (<span id='ref34548941.28618520' class='cite'>Ai <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>).&nbsp; Electron cryoEM structures of mitochondrial, chloroplast, and bacterial ATP synthases have revealed the architecture of the FO region, helping to explain the mechanisms of proton translocation, dimerization of the enzyme in mitochondria, and cristae formation. These structures also show that ATP synthases exist in different conformational states, illustrating the flexibility and dynamics of these complexex (<span id='ref34549838.30240940' class='cite'>Guo and Rubinstein 2018</span>). <span id='ref34551732.32534049' class='cite'>Zancani <em>et al.</em> 2020</span> compared the structural and functional properties of F-ATP synthases in plant mitochondria with those of yeast and mammals.</p>
<p>All eukaryotic F-type ATPases pump 3-4 H<sup>+</sup> out of mitochondria, or into thylakoids of chloroplasts, per ATP hydrolyzed. ATP-driven stepwise rotation of FoF1-ATP synthase has been defined (<span id='ref34550947.15668386' class='cite'>Ueno <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>). Bacterial F-type ATPases pump 3-4 H<sup>+</sup> and/or Na<sup>+</sup> (depending on the system) out of the cell per ATP hydrolyzed.  These enzymes also operate in the opposite direction, synthesizing ATP when protons or Na<sup>+</sup> flow through the 'ATP synthase' down the proton electrochemical gradient (the 'proton motive force' or pmf) (<span id='ref4383.' class='cite'>Ferguson <em>et al</em>., 2006</span>).  V-type ATPases may pump 2-3 H<sup>+</sup> per ATP hydrolyzed, and these enzymes cannot catalyze pmf-driven ATP synthesis.  It has been proposed that this difference between F-type and V-type ATPases is due to a 'proton slip' that results from an altered structure in the membrane sector of V-type ATPases (<span id='ref1411.11532458' class='cite'>Perzov <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  This probably results from duplication (intragenic and/or intergenic) of the proteolipid (c) subunit.&nbsp; Several intact F-type ATPase complexes have been purified from different fungal species and analyzed for their properties and subunit comopositions after solubilization with various detergents (<span id='ref34544272.25759169' class='cite'>Liu <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>). Purified mitochondrial F-ATP synthase forms a Ca<sup>2+</sup>-dependent high-conductance channel matching the mitochondrial permeability transition pore (<span id='ref34550848.31554800' class='cite'>Urbani <em>et al.</em> 2019</span>). However, the ATP synthase may not be required for formation of the mitochondrial permeability transition pore (<span id='ref34552372.31992647' class='cite'>Walker <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). pH-dependent 11 degrees F1FO ATP synthase sub-steps reveal aspects of the FO torque generating mechanism (<span id='ref34554321.34970963' class='cite'>Yanagisawa and Frasch 2021</span>). ATP synthases found in the thylakoid membranes of photosynthetic organisms have some unique features not present in bacterial and mitochondrial systems. Among these is a larger-than-average transmembrane rotor ring and a redox-regulated switch capable of inhibiting ATP hydrolysis activity in the dark by uniquely adapted rotor subunit modifications.&nbsp;<span id='ref34554331.33890627' class='cite'>Cheuk and Meier 2021</span> and <span id='ref34553304.30901262' class='cite'>K&uuml;hlbrandt 2019</span> have reviewed the structures and mechanisms of ATP synthases involved in photosynthesis and ox phos, respectively. Additionally, <span id='ref34553493.33705299' class='cite'>Zubareva <em>et al.</em> 2020</span> have reviewed the diversities, similarities and differences of these enzymes.</p>
<p>The structure of the membrane integral rotor ring of the proton
translocating F(1)F(0)-ATPase synthase from spinach chloroplasts was
determined to 3.8 &Aring; resolution by X-ray crystallography (<span id='ref6475.19423706' class='cite'>Vollmar <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The rotor ring
consists of 14 identical protomers which are symmetrically arranged
around a central pore, but rings having any where from 8 protomers to 15 protomers have been reported (<span id='ref7379.20847295' class='cite'>Watt <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>). Comparisons to the c(11)-rotor ring of the
sodium translocating ATPase from <em>Ilyobacter tartaricus</em> showed that the
conserved carboxylates involved in proton or sodium transport,
respectively, are 10.6-10.8 &Aring; apart in both c-ring rotors. This finding
suggests that both ATPases have the same gear distance despite their
different stoichiometries. The putative proton binding site at the
conserved carboxylate E61 in the chloroplast ATP synthase differs from
the sodium binding site in <em>Ilyobacter</em>. Residues adjacent to the
conserved carboxylate show increased hydrophobicity and reduced
hydrogen bonding. The crystal structure reflects the protonated form of
the chloroplast c-ring rotor. <span id='ref6475.19423706' class='cite'>Vollmar <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span> proposed that upon deprotonation, the
conformation of E61 is changed to another rotamer and becomes fully
exposed to the periphery of the ring. Reprotonation of E61 by a
conserved arginine in the adjacent a subunit returns the carboxylate to
its initial conformation. The structure of the c-ring has been examined in different lipid bilayers (<span id='ref34549124.29197977' class='cite'>Kang <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>). F-type ATPases can be inhibited by ADP (<span id='ref34549254.30472953' class='cite'>Lapashina and Feniouk 2018</span>).&nbsp; Amino acids in the F<sub>O</sub> sector that influence H<sup>+</sup> translocation have been identified (<span id='ref34550046.30798467' class='cite'>Trombetti <em>et al.</em> 2019</span>). Substrate-induced tuning of the H<sup>+</sup>/ATP coupling ratio during catalysis might represent a key to the establishment of energy homeostasis (<span id='ref34555600.36275634' class='cite'>Turina 2022</span>). The use of OXPHOS-targeting drugs in oncology has been reviewed (<span id='ref34557255.37736880' class='cite'>Kalyanaraman <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>Phylogenetic clustering of the integral membrane constituents of F-type ATPases generally corresponds to the phylogenies of the organisms of origin, and consequently the systems in different organisms are probably orthologues.  The a subunit of F<sub>0</sub> (one copy per complex) spans the membrane five or six times.  The b subunits (2 copies per complex; heterodimeric in plant chloroplasts and blue green bacteria) span the membrane once; and the c subunits (called DCCD-binding lipoproteins; reportedly 10, 11, 12, or 14 copies per complex depending on the system) span the membrane two times.  Some F-type ATPases such as the Na<sup>+</sup>-translocating ATPase of <em>Acetobacterium woodii</em> probably contains 3 dissimilar but homologous c-subunit proteolipids of 8 and 18 kDa.  The V-type ATPase of <em>S. cerevisiae </em>also has 3 dissimilar c-subunits as mentioned in the next paragraph.  While c-subunits in the <em>E. coli</em> F-ATPase have 2 TMSs (one acive site asp per subunit and 12 copies per complex), V-type ATPases have 4 TMSs (one active site glu per subunit and 6 copies per complex), and an archaeal A-ATPase has 6 TMSs (2 active site Glu(s) per subunit and 4 subunits per complex).&nbsp; <span id='ref34555444.36171652' class='cite'>Marshansky 2022</span> reviewed the historical analysis of the discovery and study of transmembrane rotary ion-translocating ATPase nano-motors functioning in bacteria, eukaryotic cells and mitochondria of animals. The complex V ATPase consists of 16 subunits encoded by both mitochondrial DNA and 
nuclear DNA. On MT-ATP6, MT-ATP8, ATPAF2, TMEM70 and ATP5E gene of 
mitochondrial DNA, many mutations associated with Complex V deficiency 
have been identified.&nbsp;<span id='ref34557330.23866288' class='cite'>Li and Yang 2013</span> described the pathology, clinical features, diagnosis,
 treatment and molecular genetics of Complex V deficiency. Atp8 is present in the Mytilidae, and the mitochondrial genes may contribute to the adaptation of Mytilidae to different environments (<span id='ref34557331.36324074' class='cite'>Zhao <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
<p>The &alpha;, &beta; and c-subunits of F-type ATPases are homologues to the B, A and c- (or K-) subunits of V-type and A-type ATPases, respectively.  Other subunits in these protein complexes are probably homologous to each other, but this fact cannot always be demonstrated by statistical analyses of the sequences.  Thus, for the A-type ATPase of <em>Methanosarcina mazei,</em> the V-type ATPase of yeast, and the F-type ATPase of <em>E. coli</em>, respectively, the following subunit equivalences have been suggested: A = Vma1 (A) = &beta;; B = Vma2 (B) = &alpha;; C = Vma6 (d) = no <em>E. coli</em> F-type ATPase equivalent; Vma8 (D) = &gamma;; Vma4 (E) = &delta;; F = Vma7 (F) = &epsilon;; I = Vphl/stvl = a+b ?, and K = Vma3 (c) = c.  Additionally, the yeast V-type ATPase has 3 dissimilar c-subunits: Vma3(c), Vmal1(c') and Vma6(c''), and three subunits, Vma13(H), Vma5(c) and Vma10(G) which are not found in either the A- or F-type ATPases.  All of the yeast vacuolar ATPase subunits have an equivalent subunit in the V-type ATPases of clathrin-coated vesicles of higher eukaryotes.</p>
<p>Eukaryotic V-type ATPases acidify Golgi-derived vesicles, clathrin-coated vesicles, synaptic vesicles, liposomes, and plant vacuoles and function in protein trafficking, receptor-mediated endocytosis, neurotransmitter release, pH regulation, waste management, pro-hormone processing, protein degradation, etc (<span id='ref5461.' class='cite'>Kane, 2006</span>). They also function in the entry of various pathogenic agents, including 
many envelope viruses, like influenza virus, and toxins, like anthrax 
toxin. Plasma membrane V-ATPases function in renal pH homeostasis, bone 
resorption and sperm maturation, and various disease processes, 
including renal tubular acidosis, osteopetrosis, and tumor metastasis (<span id='ref7101.20450191' class='cite'>Toei <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>).   There are 13 subunits, 8 (A-H) in V<sub>1</sub> and 5 (a, c, c', c'', d and e).  The c-subunits are arranged in a ring with the a-subunit on the outside of the ring.  The proton channel may be at the a/c interface, and c rotates relative to a when ATP is hydrolyzed and H<sup>+</sup> is translocated.  Rotation of V-type ATPases has been demonstrated (<span id='ref2524.12598655' class='cite'>Imamura <em>et al</em>., 2003</span>). Two important mechanisms of regulating V-ATPase activity in vivo are reversible dissociation of the V<sub>1</sub> and V<sub>0</sub> domains and changes in coupling efficiency of proton transport and ATP hydrolysis (<span id='ref.18423392' class='cite'>Cipriano <em>et al.</em> 2008</span>). V-type ATPase proteolipids can form symmetrical 6-membered rings as is true for gap junction sheets from <em>Nephrops norvegicus</em> which are formed by a protein identical to the 4 TMS V-ATPase c-subunit.  A 14th subunit, Ac45, associated with V<sub>0</sub>, is found in some mammalian tissues. The crystal structure of the central axis DF complex of a prokaryotic V-ATPase has been solved (<span id='ref8247.22114184' class='cite'>Saijo <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). A&nbsp; 2.7-A cryo-EM structure of the yeast Vo proton channel revealed the location of ordered water molecules along the proton path, details of specific protein-lipid interactions, and the architecture of the membrane scaffold protein (<span id='ref34552250.33028525' class='cite'>Roh <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>) and a state of Vo shows the c-ring rotated by ~14 degrees . Two rotary states are in thermal equilibrium and depict how the protonation state of essential glutamic acid residues couples water-mediated proton transfer with c-ring rotation. Resuts rationalize a mechanism for inhibition of passive proton transport as observed for free Vo that is generated as a result of V-ATPase regulation by reversible disassembly in vivo (<span id='ref34552250.33028525' class='cite'>Roh <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). High glucose disrupts the autophagy lysosomal pathway in gingival epithelial cells via the ATP6V<sub>0</sub> C subunit (<span id='ref34552512.31471894' class='cite'>Huang <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p>By altering the pH of intracellular compartments, V-ATPases dictate enzyme activities,
govern the dissociation of ligands from receptors and promote the coupled transport of substrates
across membranes. In
tissues where V-ATPases are expressed at the plasma membrane, they can serve to acidify the
extracellular microenvironment. Additional roles that seem independent of H<sup>+</sup> translocation include fusogenicity, cytoskeletal tethering and metabolic
sensing (<span id='ref34544378.25453113' class='cite'>Maxson and Grinstein 2014</span>). CryoEM studies have revealed conformational heterogeneity and characteristic TMSs that are highly tilted with respect to the membrane (<span id='ref34546823.27532044' class='cite'>Mazhab-Jafari and Rubinstein 2016).</span> An oscillating electric (AC) field has been used to measure the biochemical activity of a rotary enzyme such as a vacuolar proton-ATPase (V-ATPase), specificallly to directly measure its mean rate of rotation in its native membrane environment (<span id='ref34549601.28345665' class='cite'>Ferencz <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). Cryo-EM structures of the intact V-ATPase from bovine brain have been solved with all the subunits including the subunit H, which is essential for ATPase activity. Two type-I transmembrane proteins, Ac45 and (pro)renin receptor, along with subunit c', constitute the core of the c-ring (<span id='ref34552121.32764564' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). Three different conformations of A/B heterodimers suggest a mechanism for ATP hydrolysis that triggers a rotation of subunits DF, inducing spinning of subunit d with respect to the entire c-ring. Many lipid molecules are in the Vo domain to mediate the interactions between subunits c, c', (pro)renin receptor, and Ac45 (<span id='ref34552121.32764564' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). The involvement of V-ATPases in cell physiology and disease has been reviewed (<span id='ref34552870.33326313' class='cite'>Eaton <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
<p>V-ATPase is a ~1 MDa membrane protein complex that functions in the acidification of endosomal/lysosomal compartments and hence in transport, recycling and degradative pathways. The complex, assembled from up to 30 individual polypeptides, operates as a molecular motor with rotary mechanics. Structural inferences about the eukaryotic V-ATPase and its subunits have been made by comparison to the structures of bacterial homologues. However, cryo-EM has revealed more about the catalytic mechanism of this proton pump and how its activity might be regulated in response to cellular signals (<span id='ref34548944.29464568' class='cite'>Harrison and Muench 2018</span>). V-ATPase inhibitors, concanamycin and indole pentadiene, insert into lipid membranes and can inhibit the enzyme from a membrane location (<span id='ref34551807.15157605' class='cite'>P&aacute;li <em>et al.</em> 2004</span>).</p>
<p>In mammalian cells, most of the V-ATPase subunits exist in multiple 
isoforms which are often expressed in a tissue specific manner. Isoforms
 of one of the V(0) subunits (subunit a) have been shown to possess 
information that targets the V-ATPase to distinct cellular destinations.
 Mutations in isoforms of subunit a lead to the human diseases 
osteopetrosis and renal tubular acidosis. The Vesicular proton pump (V-ATPase) in nerve terminals following exocytosis continues to pump, promoting neurotransmitter uptake via endocytosis (<span id='ref8090.21172605' class='cite'>Tabares and Betz, 2010</span>). A number of mechanisms are 
employed to regulate V-ATPase activity in vivo, including reversible 
dissociation of the V(1) and V(0) domains, control of the tightness of 
coupling of proton transport and ATP hydrolysis, and selective targeting
 of V-ATPases to distinct cellular membranes. Isoforms of subunit a are 
involved in regulation both via the control of coupling and via 
selective targeting (<span id='ref7101.20450191' class='cite'>Toei <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>).</p>
<p>The V-ATPase ring contains three different subunits, c, c' and c'' and is therefore probably asymmetric if two or all three are present.  In the yeast vacuolar ATPase, C (VMA3p) and C' (VMA11p) are 4 TMS proteins with N- and C-termini in the vacuolar lumen, but C'' (VMA16p) is a 5 TMS protein with N-terminus in the cytosol and the C-terminus in the lumen (<span id='ref3030.15252052' class='cite'>Flannery <em>et al</em>., 2004</span>).  Althought TMHMM predicts 7TMSs for the a subunit (P32563) of the yeast (<em>S. cerevisiae</em>)<em> </em>V-type ATPase, Wang et al (<span id='ref5268.18508769' class='cite'>2008</span>) provide evidence for an eight transmembrane helix model in which the C-terminus is located on the cytoplasmic side of the membrane. By angular reconstitution from electron microscopic images, a 21 &Aring; resolution structure shows an asymmetric protein ring with two small openings on the lumenal side and one large opening on the cytoplasmic side.  The central hole on the lumenal side is covered by a globular protein while the cytoplasmic opening is covered by two elongated proteins (<span id='ref2121.11533034' class='cite'>Wilkens and Forgac, 2001</span>). Evolution of the hexameric transmembrane ring of the eukaryotic V-ATPase proton pump has been investigated (<span id='ref8395.22230956' class='cite'>Finnigan <em>et al.</em>, 2012</span>).</p>
<p>Vacuolar H<sup>+</sup>-ATPases (V-ATPases) are large, multisubunit proton pumps that acidify the lumen of organelles in virtually every eukaryotic cell, and in specialized acid-secreting animal cells, the enzyme pumps protons into the extracellular space. In higher organisms, most of the subunits are expressed as multiple isoforms, with some enriched in specific compartments or tissues and others expressed ubiquitously. In mammals, subunit a is expressed as four isoforms (a1-4) that target the enzyme to distinct biological membranes. Mutations in a isoforms are known to give rise to tissue-specific diseases (<span id='ref34553542.34270960' class='cite'>Oot <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>). Exogenous expression of the a4 subunit in HEK293F cells permits assembly of a functional V-ATPase by incorporation of endogenous subunits. The ATPase activity of the preparation is &gt;95% sensitive to concanamycin A, indicating that the lipid nanodisc-reconstituted enzyme is functionally coupled.</p>
<p>In the reversible coupled rotatory mechanism for ATP-hydrolysis driven H<sup>+</sup> transport, the coupling scheme is now basically complete. During rotation of single molecules of F<sub>1</sub>, phosphate release drives the last 40&deg; of the 120&deg; step, and the 40&deg; rotation accompanies reduction of the affinity for phosphate. Moreover, the affinity for ADP decreases with rotation, and thus, ADP release contributes part of energy for rotation (<span id='ref4274.17662945' class='cite'>Adachi <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>; <span id='ref5659.17662937' class='cite'>Senior, 2007</span>). Surprisingly, Uchihashi et al. (<span id='ref7830.21817054' class='cite'>2011</span>), using high speed atomic force microscopy, have shown that the F<sub>1</sub> ATPase exhibits rotary catalysis in the absence of the rotor. Post-translational modifications modulate both the F0 and F1 proteins, affecting the ATPase activity in response to different stimuli (<span id='ref34547852.28782624' class='cite'>Nesci <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). The cryoEM structure of the ATP synthase from <em>Mycobacterium smegmatis</em> provides targets for treating tuberculosis (<span id='ref34554140.34782468' class='cite'>Montgomery <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>). Contemporary views on unidirectional catalysis mediated by F-type ATPases have been summarized (<span id='ref34556189.36982498' class='cite'>Zharova <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>Mycobacteria, such as <em>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</em>, depend on the activity of ATP synthase for growth. The diarylquinoline bedaquiline (BDQ), a mycobacterial ATP synthase inhibitor, is used for treatment of drug-resistant tuberculosis but suffers from off-target effects and is susceptible to resistance mutations. <span id='ref34556762.37377118' class='cite'>Courbon <em>et al.</em> 2023</span> used cryoEM and biochemical assays to study the interaction of <em>Mycobacterium smegmatis </em>ATP synthase with the second generation diarylquinoline TBAJ-876 and the squaramide inhibitor SQ31f. The aryl groups of TBAJ-876 improve binding compared with BDQ, while SQ31f, which blocks ATP synthesis ~10 times more potently than ATP hydrolysis, binds a previously unknown site in the enzyme's proton-conducting channel. BDQ, TBAJ-876, and SQ31f all induce similar conformational changes in ATP synthase, suggesting that the resulting conformation is particularly suited for drug binding, and high concentrations of the diarylquinolines uncouple the transmembrane proton motive force while for SQ31f they do not, which may explain why high concentrations of diarylquinolines, but not SQ31f, have been reported to kill mycobacteria (<span id='ref34556762.37377118' class='cite'>Courbon <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>The rotary proton- and sodium-translocating ATPases are reversible molecular machines present in all cellular life forms that couple ion movement across membranes with ATP hydrolysis or synthesis. Sequence and structural comparisons of F- and V-type ATPases have revealed homology between their catalytic and membrane subunits, but not between the subunits of the central stalk that connects the catalytic and membrane components. It has been proposed that these ATPases originated from membrane protein translocases which  evolved from RNA translocases (<span id='ref4531.17938630' class='cite'>Mulkidjanian <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). The Na<sup>+</sup>-pumping ATP synthase of <em>Acetobacterium woodii</em> has an unusual feature: its membrane-embedded rotor is a hybrid made of F and V-like subunits in a stoichiometry of 9:1, apparently not variable with the growth conditions (<span id='ref6410.19167341' class='cite'>Schmidt <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The diversity of rotary ion-translocating ATPases from different organisms (F- V- and A-types) has been reviewed (<span id='ref34553493.33705299' class='cite'>Zubareva <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p>Bacterial operons for F(1)F(0)-ATP synthase typically include an <em>uncI</em> gene encodes a small hydrophobic protein. When Suzuki et al. (<span id='ref4590.18083842' class='cite'>2007</span>) expressed a hybrid F(1)F(0) (F(1) from thermophilic <em>Bacillus</em> PS3 and Na<sup>+</sup>-translocating F(0) from <em>Propionigenium modestum</em>) in <em>E. coli</em> cells, they found that <em>uncI</em> derived from <em>P. modestum</em> was indispensable to produce active enzyme; without <em>uncI</em>, c-subunits in F(1)F(0) existed as monomers but not as a functional c(11)-ring. A plasmid containing only <em>uncI</em> and the c-subunit gene produced c(11)-ring, but a plasmid containing only the c-subunit gene did not. Direct interaction of the UncI protein with c-subunits was demonstrated. Na<sup>+</sup> induced dissociation of His-tagged UncI protein from the c(11)-ring but not from c-monomers. Thus, UncI is a chaperone-like protein that assists the c(11)-ring assembly from the c-monomers in the membrane (<span id='ref4590.18083842' class='cite'>Suzuki <em>et al</em>., 2007</span>). Ozaki et al., (2008) have confirmed these observations. The halotolerant cyanobacterium,<em> Aphanothece halophytica</em>, contains an Na<sup>+</sup>-dependent F1F0-ATP synthase with a potential role in salt-stress tolerance (<span id='ref7623.21262962' class='cite'>Soontharapirakkul <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).</p>
<p>The prokaryotic V-ATPase of <em>Enterococcus hirae, </em>closely related to the eukaryotic enzymes, provides a unique opportunity to study ion translocation by V-ATPases because it transports Na<sup>+</sup> ions, which are easier to detect by x-ray crystallography and radioisotope experiments (<span id='ref5411.18559856' class='cite'>Murata et al., 2008</span>). The purified rotor ring (K-ring) of the <em>E. hirae</em> V-ATPase binds one Na<sup>+ </sup>ion per K-monomer with high affinity, which is competitively inhibited by Li<sup>+</sup> or H<sup>+</sup>. The K-ring structure was determined at 2.8 A in the presence of Li<sup>+</sup>. Association and dissociation rates of the Na<sup>+</sup> to and from the purified K-ring were extremely slow compared with the Na<sup>+</sup> translocation rate estimated from the enzymatic activity, suggesting that interaction with the stator subunit (I-subunit) is essential for Na<sup>+</sup> binding to and release from the K-ring.</p>
<p>Subunit a of the vacuolar H<sup>+</sup>-ATPases is an integral membrane 100kDa subunit, thought to contribute to and leave buried carboxyl groups on the proteolipid subunits (c, c' and c'') during proton translocation. Subunit a contains a large N-terminal cytoplasmic domain followed by a C-terminal domain containing eight transmembrane (TM) helices. TM7 contains a buried arginine residue (Arg-735) that is essential for proton transport and is located on a helical face that interacts with the proteolipid ring (<span id='ref7963.21832060' class='cite'>Toei <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>). Residues important for proton transport are located on the cytoplasmic half of TM7 and TM8 and the luminal half of TM3, TM4 and TM7. The cytoplasmic hemi-channel is located at the interface of TM7 and TM8 of subunit a and the proteolipid ring, whereas the luminal hemi-channel is located within subunit a at the interface of TM3, TM4 and TM7.</p>
<p>Rotary ATPases/synthases from <em>Thermus thermophilus</em> and <em>Enterococcus hirae</em>&nbsp;are maintained intact with membrane and soluble subunit interactions preserved in vacuum. Mass spectra reveal subunit stoichiometries and the identity of tightly bound lipids within the membrane rotors. Moreover, subcomplexes formed in solution and gas phases reveal the regulatory effects of nucleotide binding on both ATP hydrolysis and proton translocation. Consequently, specific lipid and nucleotide binding&nbsp;can be linked&nbsp;with distinct regulatory roles (<span id='ref8058.22021858' class='cite'>Zhou <em>et al.</em>, 2011</span>).</p>
<p>Rotations of F<sub>1</sub>-ATPase occurs reversibly in discrete 120&deg; steps by precisely controlling both the external torque and the chemical potential of ATP hydrolysis. Toyabe et al. (<span id='ref8075.21997211' class='cite'>2011</span>) found that the maximal work performed by F<sub>1</sub>-ATPase per 120&deg; step is nearly equal to the thermodynamic maximal work that can be extracted from a single ATP hydrolysis event under a broad range of conditions. Their results support a nearly 100% free-energy transduction efficiency and a tight mechanochemical coupling.</p>
<p>The ability of c-rings to rotate within the F(o) complex derives from the interplay between the ion-binding sites and their nonhomogenous microenvironment (<span id='ref9290.20972431' class='cite'>Pogoryelov <em>et al.</em>, 2010</span>). Pogoryelov et al. (<span id='ref9290.20972431' class='cite'>2010</span>) demonstrated how the thermodynamic stability of the so-called proton-locked state is maximized by the lipid membrane. By contrast, a hydrophilic environment at the a-subunit-c-ring interface appears to unlock the binding-site conformation and promotes proton exchange with the surrounding solution. Rotation thus occurs as c-subunits stochastically alternate between these environments, directionally biased by the electrochemical transmembrane gradient. Intrinsic features of the annular arrangement of c subunits, the c-ring, provides the core of the membrane-embedded F<sub>O</sub> domain. The c-ring constitution is linked to the number of ions, H<sup>+</sup> or Na<sup>+</sup>, channeled across the membrane during the dissipation of the transmembrane electrochemical gradient, which in turn determines the species-specific bioenergetic cost of ATP, the 'molecular currency unit' of energy transfer (<span id='ref34547123.26621635' class='cite'>Nesci <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
<p><span id='ref34543774.25707805' class='cite'>Allegretti <em>et al.</em> 2015 r</span>eported the structure of a native, active mitochondrial ATP synthase dimer, determined by single-
particle electron cryomicroscopy at 6.2&nbsp;&Aring; resolution. The structure showed four long, horizontal
membrane-intrinsic &alpha;-helices in the a-subunit, arranged in two hairpins at an angle of
approximately 70 degrees relative to the c-ring helices. It had been proposed that a strictly
conserved membrane-embedded arginine in the a-subunit couples proton translocation to c-ring
rotation. A fit of the conserved carboxy-terminal a-subunit sequence places the conserved arginine
next to a proton-binding c-subunit glutamate. The map shows a slanting solvent-accessible channel
that extends from the mitochondrial matrix to the conserved arginine. Another hydrophilic cavity on
the lumenal membrane surface defines a direct route for the protons to an essential histidine-glutamate pair. The results explain how ATP production is coupled to proton translocation (<span id='ref34543774.25707805' class='cite'>Allegretti <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>The molecular structure of the transmembrane domain of ATP synthases is responsible for inner mitochondrial membrane bending. According to a hypothesized mechanism, ATP synthase dissociation from dimers to monomers, triggered by Ca<sup>2+</sup> binding to F1, allows mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) formation at the interface between the detached monomers (<span id='ref34547932.29555114' class='cite'>Nesci 2018</span>). However, although removal of either ATP synthase or ANT (adenine nucleotide translocase) eliminates the Ca<sup>2+</sup>-induced mPTP, elimination of the ATP synthase c subunit does not prevent the adenine nucleotide translocase (ANT)-dependent mitochondrial permeability transition (mPTP) (<span id='ref34557033.37566029' class='cite'>Neginskaya <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>). This suggests that mPTP formation relies on the interaction between constituents of the ATP synthase and ANT protein complexes. The cells in which these experiments were conducted had a vestigial ATP synthase complex with assembled F1 and peripheral stalk domains. These cells could still undergo mPTP activation, which could be blocked by the ANT inhibitor, bongkrekic acid. These results suggest that ANT can form the pore independently of the c-subunit but still requires the presence of other components of ATP synthase (<span id='ref34557033.37566029' class='cite'>Neginskaya <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Bedaquiline (BDQ, trade name Sirturo) for the treatment of multidrug-resistant tuberculosis disease, targets the membrane-bound F1Fo-ATP synthase and is a respiratory 'uncoupler', disrupting transmembrane electrochemical gradients, in addition to binding to enzyme targets.&nbsp;<span id='ref34549326.29499834' class='cite'>Hards and Cook 2018</span> summarized the role of bioenergetic systems in mycobacterial persistence and discuss the multi-targeting nature of uncouplers and the place these molecules may have in future drug development.</p>
<p>An arginine residue (Arg-735) in <span class='highlight'>transmembrane</span> helix 7 (TM7) of subunit a of the yeast ATPase is known to be essential
 for proton translocation. Arginine residues are usually assumed to 
'snorkel' toward the protein surface when exposed to a hydrophobic 
environment. However, <span id='ref34548788.30209131' class='cite'>Hohlweg <em>et al.</em> 2018</span> obtained evidence for
 the formation of a transient, membrane-embedded cation-&pi; interaction in
 TM7 between Arg-735 and two highly conserved nearby aromatic residues, 
Tyr-733 and Trp-737. They proposed a mechanism by which the transient, 
membrane-embedded cation-&pi; complex provides the necessary energy to keep
 the charged side chain of Arg-735 within the hydrophobic membrane. Such
 cation-&pi; interactions may define a general mechanism to retain charged 
amino acids in a hydrophobic membrane environment (<span id='ref34548788.30209131' class='cite'>Hohlweg <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>The strictly anaerobic rumen bacterium <em>Pseudobutyrivibrio ruminis</em> possesses 2 ATP synthases and 2 distinct respiratory enzymes, the ferredoxin oxidoreductase (Rnf complex, TC# 3.D.6) and the energy-converting hydrogenase (Ech complex, TC# 3.D.1.4.2) (<span id='ref34551322.31879356' class='cite'>Schoelmerich <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>). In silico analyses revealed that one ATP synthase is H<sup>+</sup>-dependent, and the other is Na<sup>+</sup>-dependent. Growth of <em>P. ruminis </em>was greatly stimulated by Na<sup>+</sup>, and a 2-component ion circuit for H<sup>+</sup> bioenergetics and a second 2-component ion circuit for Na<sup>+</sup> bioenergetics was demonstrated. These 2 circuits are prevalent in a number of other strictly anaerobic microorganisms (<span id='ref34551322.31879356' class='cite'>Schoelmerich <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p>The mitochondrial F1-F0-ATPase in the presence of Mg<sup>2+</sup> can both synthesize ATP and hydrolyze ATP to pump H<sup>+</sup>. Mg<sup>2+</sup> can be replaced by Ca<sup>2+</sup> , but only to sustain ATP hydrolysis, not ATP synthesis. When Ca<sup>2+</sup> inserts into F1, the torque generation built by the chemomechanical coupling between F1 and the rotating central stalk is unable to drive the transmembrane H<sup>+</sup> flux within F0. The structural F1-F0-ATPase distortion driven by ATP hydrolysis, sustained by Ca<sup>2+,</sup> is consistent with the permeability transition pore signal propagation pathway. Ca<sup>2+</sup>-activated F1-F0-ATPase, by forming the pore, may contribute to dissipation of the transmembrane H<sup>+</sup> gradient created by the same enzyme complex (<span id='ref34552444.33378096' class='cite'>Nesci and Pagliarani 2020</span>).</p>
<p>The V-type ATPase from citrus fruit has been solved in two rotational states (<span id='ref34555512.36041457' class='cite'>Tan <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). The structure defines the ATP:H<sup>+</sup> ratio of the enzyme, demonstrating that it can establish a maximum &Delta;pH of approximately 3, which is insufficient to maintain the low pH observed in the vacuoles of juice sac cells in lemons and other citrus fruit. Compared with yeast and mammalian enzymes, the membrane region of the plant V-ATPase lacks subunit f and possesses an unusual configuration of transmembrane alpha helices. Subunit H, which inhibits ATP hydrolysis in the isolated catalytic region of the V-ATPase, adopts two different conformations in the intact complex, hinting at a role in modulating activity in the intact enzyme (<span id='ref34555512.36041457' class='cite'>Tan <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>

The d-subunit couples ATP hydrolysis to H<sup>+ </sup> transport (<a>Owegi <em>et al</em>., 2006</a>).&nbsp; The a subunit (Vph1p) most likely has an 8 TMS topology (<a>Kartner <em>et al.</em> 2013; <span>Knight and Behm 2012)</span></a>.&nbsp; Structures for three

<p>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; An arginine residue (Arg-735) in <span class="highlight">transmembrane</span> helix 7 (TMS7) of subunit a of the yeast ATPase is known to be essential

'snorkel' toward the protein surface when exposed to a hydrophobic

membrane-embedded cation-&pi; complex provides the necessary energy to keep
 the charged side chain of Arg-735 within the hydrophobic membrane. Such
