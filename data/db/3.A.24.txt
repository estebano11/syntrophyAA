<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>3.A.24 The&nbsp;Type VII or ESX Protein Secretion System (T7SS) Family</strong></p>
<p>Mycobacteria possess several related protein secretion systems often referred to as Type VII Secretion Systems (T7SS) that may be distantly related to Type IV secretion systems (T4SS; TC# 3.A.7) (<span id='ref9401.19876390' class='cite'>Bitter <em>et al.</em> 2009a</span>;&nbsp;<span id='ref9402.19660950' class='cite'>Bitter <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>b). Virulent <em>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</em> (Mtb) possesses several regions of genetic virulence that are lacking in avirulent <em>M. bovis</em> BCG. One of these regions of difference, RD1, is present in all virulent strains and lacking in all avirulent strains tested. Deletion of this 9.5 kb region from Mtb yields attenuation of virulence, and this mutant can be complemented with the cloned region. This system is called the early secreted antigen 6 kilodaltons (ESAT-6) secretion system 1 (ESX1) or secretion in mycobacteria (Snm), and it delivers virulence factors into host macrophages during infection. Four proteins have been shown to be secreted via this system: ESAT-6, CFP-10, Rv3483c (MM1553 in <em>M. marinum</em>) and Rv3881c (Mb3881c in <em>M. marinum</em>) (<span id='ref9403.17908204' class='cite'>Xu <em>et al.</em> 2007</span>). Two characteristics unify all esx loci; first, the secretion of small (~100 aas) proteins with a conserved Trp-X-Gly (WXG) motif which contibutes to the formation of a helix-turn-hexix structure in the center of the protein, and second, the presence of a gene that encode a TM protein of the FtsK/SpoIIIE ATPase family (<span id='ref34548414.27665717' class='cite'>Gr&ouml;schel <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>). A phylogenetic tree of the various ESX components/systems has been published (<span id='ref34548414.27665717' class='cite'>Gr&ouml;schel <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
<p>There are ten ORFs in RD1:</p>
<p>(1) Rv3870: an ATP-dependent chaperone (747 aas; 3 putative TMSs in N-terminal region) (Includes a region (467-702 aas) homologous to SpoIIIE of <em>B. subtilis</em> in the 5-DNA-T family) (TC #3.A.12)</p>
<p>(2) Rv3871: an integral membrane protein (591 aas; 2 or 3 putative TMS in C-terminal region) may form a functional complex with Rv3870 (slight sequence similarity to a region of SpoIIIE)</p>
<p>(3) Rv3872: a PE repetitive protein (111 aas)</p>
<p>(4) Rv3873: a PPE repetitive protein (368 aas)</p>
<p>(5) Rv3874(cfp-10): a 10 kDa cultured protein (100 aas)</p>
<p>(6) Rv3875(esat-6): a 6 kDa cultured protein (100 aas) that forms a tight 1:1 complex with Rv3874, a potent T-cell antigen; Rv3874 and Rv3875 are homologous</p>
<p>(7) Rv3876: a protein with a proline- + alanine-rich N-terminus and a C-terminal region homologous to a P. aeruginosa flagellar biosynthesis protein with a Walker A motif (666 aas)</p>
<p>(8) Rv3877: an integral membrane protein (511 aas; 11 putative TMSs)</p>
<p>(9) Rv3878 and 3879: nonessential for virulence.</p>
<p>(10) Rv3860, an essential component of the ESX-1 secretion system (<span id='ref9404.18974091' class='cite'>Luthra <em>et al.</em> 2008</span>).</p>
<p>CFP-10 and ESAT-6 are secreted via the RD1 system and may be part of it. Because Rv3870 and 3876 have motifs suggesting they are ATPases, the system may be ATP-dependent, but this has not been demonstrated. Deletion of any of the following genes blocks virulence and secretion: Rv3870, 71, 74, 75 and 76. The (CFP-10)(ESAT-6) dimer is stable, but the exported monomers may not be.</p>
<p>The ESX-1 system (ESAT-6 system-1) is required for controlling host-cell response to infection (<span id='ref9405.14756778' class='cite'>Guinn <em>et al.</em> 2004</span>;&nbsp;<span id='ref9406.14557547' class='cite'>Hsu <em>et al.</em> 2003</span>;&nbsp;<span id='ref9407.14557536' class='cite'>Stanley <em>et al.</em> 2003</span>). ESX-1 is encoded by genes in the RD1 (region of difference 1) locus of the genome that is missing in the <em>M. bovis</em> Bacille Calmette-Gu&eacute;rin vaccine strain (<span id='ref9408.10348738' class='cite'>Behr <em>et al.</em> 1999</span>;&nbsp;<span id='ref9409.8631702' class='cite'>Mahairas <em>et al.</em> 1996</span>). This system includes a multitransmembrane protein, Rv3877 (Snm4), and two putative SpoIIIE/FtsK adenosine triphosphatase (ATPase) family members, Rv3870 (Snm 1) and Rv3871 (Snm2). These three proteins are required for secretion of two virulence factors, ESAT-6 and CFP-10 (<span id='ref9407.14557536' class='cite'>Stanley <em>et al.</em>, 2003</span>). ESAT-6 (product of the <em>esxA</em> gene) and CFP-10 (product of the <em>esxB</em> gene) interact to form a 1:1 dimer (<span id='ref9410.11940590' class='cite'>Renshaw <em>et al.</em> 2002</span>), and the stability of these proteins is interdependent<em> in vivo</em>. CFP-10, but not ESAT-6, interacts with the C-terminal domain of Rv3871, a cytosolic component of the ESX-1 system (<span id='ref3575.14557536' class='cite'>Stanley <em>et al.</em>, 2003</span>). Although the secretion of ESAT-6 and CFP-10 is critical for M. tuberculosis virulence, the molecular mechanisms of ESX-1 substrate selection and secretion are unclear. The targetting sequence in CFP-10 is at its C-terminus (<span id='ref9412.16973880' class='cite'>Champion <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>).</p>
<p>RD1 appears to play a role in a novel type of DNA transfer (conjugation) in <em>Mycobacterium smegmatis</em>. DNA transfer requires prolonged cell-cell contact and occurs only from donors to recipients. There are multiple transfer initiations from a donor chromosome and, as a result, the inheritance of any gene is location-independent (<span id='ref9413.16164565' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>). Transfer is not contiguous; instead, multiple non-linked segments of DNA can be inherited in a recipient. However, with appropriate selection, segments of DNA at least 266 kb in length can be transferred. In contrast to Hfr transfer, transconjugants can become donors, suggesting that the recipient chromosome contains multiple cis-acting sequences required for transfer but lacks the trans-acting transfer functions. The mechanistic details of how RD1 plays a role in conjugation is not known and could be indirect (<span id='ref9413.16164565' class='cite'>Wang <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>). However, gene transfer from <em>M. smegmatis</em> to mammalian cells has also been demonstrated, possible via the same 'conjugation' system (<span id='ref9414.17664267' class='cite'>Mo <em>et al.</em> 2007</span>).</p>
<p>Two characteristics unify all <em>esx</em> loci across the different phyla. First, the presence of genes that encode small secreted proteins of approximately 100 aas that have a conserved Trp-X-Gly (WXG) motif that contributes to the formation of helix&ndash;turn&ndash;helix structures in the centres of these polypeptides; and, second, the presence of genes that encode transmembrane proteins of the FtsK&ndash;SpoIIIE-like ATPase family. The best characterized proteins that contain the WXG motif are EsxA of <em>M. tuberculosis</em> and its adjacently encoded hetero-dimerization partner EsxB (also known as CFP10). Apart from these core characteristics, ESX systems are quite diverse (<span id='ref34548404.27665717' class='cite'>Gr&ouml;schel <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
<p>An ESX/Type VII secretion system in the plant pathogen, <em>Streptomyces scabies</em>, is required for normal sporulation development but not for virulence (<span id='ref9415.23009676' class='cite'>Fyans <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).  This system may export two sequence related proteins, EsxA and EsxB. ESX-1 is present in the avirulent species, <em>Mycobacterium smegmatis</em>, where it is involved in conjugation (<span id='ref9416.18554329' class='cite'>Coros <em>et al.</em> 2008</span>).  ESX-1 uses the ESX-1-specific chaparone protein, EspG to interact with the secreted PE/PPE complex, while a homologous EspG specific for ESX-5 functions with the PE/PPE complex secreted by ESX-5.  Thus, EspG proteins may be system-specific chaparones for T7SSs (<span id='ref9417.22843727' class='cite'>Daleke <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).</p>
<p>Rv3869, Rv3870 and Rv3877, exhibit 1, 3 and 11 predicted TMSs, respectively. Together with Rv3871, a cytosolic component of the ESX-1 system, these proteins form the ESX-1 secretory complex, with protein export driven by ATP hydrolysis. A C-terminal signal sequence allows the unstructured C-terminus of CFP-10 to be recognized by Rv3871 that itself interacts with the membrane protein Rv3870. Point mutation in this signal sequence abolish binding of CFP-10 to Rv3871 and prevented secretion of ESAT-6 and CFP-10. As Rv3870 and Rv3871 both show similarity to proteins of the FtsK-SpoIIIE ATPase family, these proteins might perform an essential part of the work necessary to secrete ESX-1 substrates. This mechanism resembles type IV secretion system in Gram-negative bacteria, where a membrane-bound SpoIIIE/FtsK-like ATPase recognizes an unstructured C-terminal sequence and directs the secreted substrate to the cytoplasmic membrane (<span id='ref9395.19155186' class='cite'>Simeone <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>).</p>
<p>The<em> M. tuberculosis</em> rv3616c-rv3614c gene cluster is involved in the regulation of ESAT-6 secretion. This cluster is regulated at the expression level byPhoR/P and EspR (<span id='ref9395.19155186' class='cite'>Simeone <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>).</p>
<p>The ESX-3 cluster is controlled by the iron-dependent regulator IdeR and the zinc uptake regulator Zur (previously named FurB). This suggests that ESX-3 might be involved in fundamental biological processes such as metal ion homeostasis, consistent with the essentiality of ESX-3 for <em>in vitro </em>growth of <em>M. tuberculosis </em>and the conservation of orthologous ESX-3 systems in a wide range of mycobacterial species (<span id='ref9399.19155186' class='cite'>Simeone <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>).</p>
<p>Mycobacterial genomes contain two unique gene families, the so-called PE and PPE gene families, which are highly expanded in the pathogenic members of this genus. One of the PPE proteins, PPE41, is secreted by pathogenic mycobacteria. As PPE41 lacks a signal sequence, a dedicated secretion system must be involved. A gene was identified in <em>Mycobacterium marinum </em>that showed strongly reduced PPE41 secretion (<span id='ref9400.17076665' class='cite'>Abdallah <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>). It was located in a gene cluster whose predicted proteins encode components of an ESAT-6-like secretion system. This cluster, designated ESX-5, is conserved in various pathogenic mycobacteria, but not in the saprophytic species <em>Mycobacterium smegmatis</em>. Different regions of this cluster were introduced in <em>M. smegmatis</em>, and only introduction of the complete ESX-5 locus resulted in efficient secretion of heterologously expressed PPE41. This PPE secretion system is also involved in the virulence of pathogenic mycobacteria, as the ESX-5 mutant of <em>M. marinum</em> was affected in spreading to uninfected macrophages (<span id='ref9400.17076665' class='cite'>Abdallah <em>et al.</em>, 2006</span>). Homologues are found in various Actinobacteria.</p>
<p><span id='ref34546166.27846571' class='cite'>Gray <em>et al.</em> 2016</span> showed that the ESX secretion-system family 
member ESX-4 is essential for conjugal recipient activity in 
<em>Mycobacterium smegmatis. </em>Transcription of <em>esx4</em> genes in the recipient 
requires coculture with a donor strain and a functional ESX-1 apparatus 
in the recipient. Conversely, mutation of the donor ESX-1 apparatus 
amplifies the <em>esx4</em> transcriptional response in the recipient. The effect
 of ESX-1 on esx4 transcription correlates with conjugal DNA transfer 
efficiencies. The data show that intercellular communication via ESX-1 
controls the expression of its evolutionary progenitor, ESX-4, to 
promote conjugation between mycobacteria (<span id='ref34546166.27846571' class='cite'>Gray <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).</p>
<p>Specialized protein transport systems, type VII secretion systems (T7SSs)-are central to the virulence of <em>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</em>, and they are also crucial for nutrient and metabolite transport across the mycobacterial cell envelope. <span id='ref34553272.33981042' class='cite'>Bunduc <em>et al.</em> 2021</span> presented the structure of an intact T7SS inner-membrane complex of <em>M. tuberculosis</em>. They showed how the 2.32-MDa ESX-5 assembly, which contains 165 TMSs, is restructured and stabilized as a trimer of dimers by the MycP5 protease. A trimer of MycP5 caps a central periplasmic dome-like chamber that is formed by three EccB5 dimers, with the proteolytic sites of MycP5 facing the cavity. This chamber may be part of a central secretion and processing conduit. Complexes without MycP5 show disruption of the EccB5 periplasmic assembly and increased flexibility, which highlights the importance of MycP5 for complex integrity. Beneath the EccB5-MycP5 chamber, dimers of the EccC5 ATPase assemble into three bundles of four transmembrane helices each, which together seal the potential central secretion channel. Individual cytoplasmic EccC5 domains adopt two distinctive conformations that probably reflect different secretion states. This suggests a previously undescribed mechanism of protein transport (<span id='ref34553272.33981042' class='cite'>Bunduc <em>et al.</em> 2021</span>).</p>
