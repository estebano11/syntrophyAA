<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='CENTER'><strong> 5.A.1 The Disulfide Bond Oxidoreductase D (DsbD) Family </strong></p>
<p>The best characterized member of the DsbD family is DsbD of <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref1684.11114333' class='cite'>Katzen and Beckwith, 2000</span>; <span id='ref1687.11085993' class='cite'>Krupp <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  The DsbD protein is membrane-embedded with a putative N-terminal TMS plus 8 additional TMSs.  The smallest homologues (190 aas with 6 putative TMSs) are found in archaea while the largest are found in both Gram-negative bacteria (758 aas with 9 putative TMSs) and Gram-positive bacteria (695 aas with 6 putative TMSs).  In the <em>E. coli</em> DsbD system, electrons are transferred from NADPH in the cytoplasm to periplasmic dithiol/disulfide-containing proteins via an electron transfer chain that sequentially involves NADPH, thioredoxin reductase (TrxB), present in the cytoplasm, thioredoxin (TrxA), also in the cytoplasm, DsbD, the integral membrane constituent of the system, and the periplasmic electron acceptors, DsbC, DsbE (CcmG) and DsbG.   All of these last three proteins can donate electrons to oxidized disulfide-containing proteins in the periplasm of a Gram-negative bacterium or presumably in the external milieu of a Gram-positive bacterium or an archaeon.  Thus, the pathway is:</p>
<p align='CENTER'>NADPH &rarr;&nbsp;TrxB &rarr;&nbsp;TrxA &rarr;&nbsp;DsbD &rarr;&nbsp;DsbC, DsbE, or DsbG &rarr;&nbsp;proteins.</p>
<p>Homologues include: (1) several thiol-disufide exchange proteins, (2) the cytochrome c-type biogenesis proteins, CcdA of <em>Paracoccus pantotrophus</em> and <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> (<span id='ref1686.11114924' class='cite'>Bardischewsky and Friedrich, 2001</span>; Le <span id='ref1685.10844653' class='cite'>Brun <em>et al</em>., 2000</span>), (3) the methylamine utilization proteins, MauF of <em>Paracoccus denitrificans</em> and <em>P. versutus</em> (<span id='ref1678.1590782' class='cite'>Chistoserdov <em>et al</em>., 1992</span>; Van <span id='ref1680.7957249' class='cite'>Spanning <em>et al</em>., 1994</span>), (4) the mercury resistance proteins (possibly Hg<sup>2+</sup> transporters) of <em>Mycobacterium tuberculosis</em> and <em>Streptomyces lividans</em> (<span id='ref1681.8676873' class='cite'>Brunker <em>et al</em>., 1996</span>; <span id='ref1679.1494353' class='cite'>Sedlmeier and Altenbuchner, 1992</span>), (5) suppressors of copper sensitivity (copper tolerance proteins) of <em>Salmonella typhimurium</em> and <em>Vibrio cholerae</em> (<span id='ref1682.8783519' class='cite'>Choudhury and Kumar, 1996</span>; <span id='ref1683.9260936' class='cite'>Gupta <em>et al</em>., 1997</span>), (6) coomponents of peroxide reduction pathways, and (7) components of sulfenic acid reductases. &nbsp;</p>
<p>DsbD contains three domains, each containing two reactive cysteines. One membrane-embedded domain, DsbD&gamma;, transfers electrons from thioredoxin to the carboxy-terminal thioredoxin-like periplasmic domain DsbD&gamma;.  Alanines were substituted for each of 19 conserved amino acid residues. 11 mutants caused defects in DsbC reduction. To analyze the redox state of each DsbD domain, a thrombin-cleavable DsbD (DsbD<sup>TH</sup>) was constructed from which all three domains as separate polypeptide chains were generated.  Mutants with strong defects included one mutant class that could not receive electrons from cytoplasmic thioredoxin, resulting in a DsbD that has all six of its cysteines disulfide bonded. One mutant class could not transfer electrons from DsbD&beta; to DsbD&gamma; (<span id='ref4266.16816179' class='cite'>Cho and Beckwith, 2006</span>).</p>
<p>DsbD contains three cysteine pairs that undergo reversible disulfide rearrangements (<span id='ref1687.11085993' class='cite'>Krupps <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>). TrxA donates electrons to the transmembrane cysteines C163 (C3) and C285 (C5) in putative TMSs 1 and 4 in the DsbD model proposed by Katzen and Beckwith (<span id='ref1684.11114333' class='cite'>2000</span>).  This dithiol then donates electrons to the periplasmic C-terminal thioredoxin motif (CXXC) of DsbD, thereby reducing C461 and C464 (C6 and C7, respectively).  This dithiol pair attacks the periplasmic N-terminal disulfide bridge at C103 and C109 (C1 and C2, respectively) which transfers electrons to DsbC and other protein electron acceptors as noted above. The substrates of DsbD include protein disulfide isomerases and a protein involved in cytochrome c assembly (<span id='ref34551184.15380548' class='cite'>Porat <em>et al.</em> 2004</span>).</p>
<p>DsbD catalyses an essentially irreversible reaction due to the fact that electrons flow down their electrochemical gradient from inside the cell (negative inside) to outside the cell (positive outside). In order to reverse the reaction, electrons are transferred from dithiol proteins in the periplasm to an electron acceptor in the cytoplasm as follows:</p>
<p align='CENTER'>reduced protein<sub>periplasm</sub>&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;DsbA<sub>periplasm</sub>&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;DsbB<sub>membrane</sub>&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;quinones<sub>membrane</sub>&nbsp;&rarr;&nbsp;reductase<sub>membrane</sub> <br />&rarr;&nbsp;terminal electron acceptor<sub>cytoplasm</sub> (e.g., O<sub>2</sub>, NO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup> or fumarate).</p>
<p>DsbB contains 4 essential cysteine residues, reversibly forming two disulfide bonds.  Although DsbA displays no proofreading activity for repair of wrongly paired disulfides, this activity is displayed by DsbC, DsbE and DsbG (<span id='ref1687.11085993' class='cite'>Krupp <em>et al</em>., 2001</span>).  Therefore, the two transmembrane pathways involving DsbD and DsbB together catalyze extracellular disulfide reduction (DsbD) and oxidation (DsbB) in a superficially reversible process that allows dithiol/disulfide exchange.</p>
<p>A class 
of DsbD <span class='highlight'>proteins</span>, named ScsB, are found in proteobacteria and <span class='highlight'>Chlamydia</span>.
 ScsB has a domain organization similar to that of DsbD, but its 
amino-terminal domain differs. In DsbD, this domain 
directly interacts with substrates to reduce them, which suggests that 
ScsB acts on a different array of substrates. Using <em>Caulobacter 
crescentus</em> as a model organism,&nbsp;<span id='ref9239.22493033' class='cite'>Cho <em>et al.</em> 2012</span> searched for the substrates of ScsB. ScsB provides electrons to a peroxide 
reduction pathway in the bacterial cell envelope. The 
reduction pathway comprises a thioredoxin-like protein, TlpA, and a 
peroxiredoxin, PprX. PprX is a thiol-dependent peroxidase 
that efficiently reduces both hydrogen peroxide and organic peroxides. Additional <span class='highlight'>proteins</span> that depend on ScsB for reduction include a peroxiredoxin-like protein, PrxL, 
and a novel protein disulfide isomerase, ScsC.&nbsp; Thus, the array of <span class='highlight'>proteins</span> involved in reductive pathways in the oxidative cell envelope is broad.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The best-known TM reductase is <em>E. coli</em> DsbD, which has a TM domain (DsbD&beta;) and two periplasmic domains (DsbD<span class='mb'>&alpha;</span> and DsbD<span class='mb'>&gamma;</span>), each containing a redox-active cysteine pair.<sup>&nbsp;</sup> Electrons are transferred from cytoplasmic thioredoxin A (TrxA) to 
DsbD&beta;, relayed across the membrane and through its periplasmic domains, 
and transferred to various periplasmic targets to maintain their 
reductive activity. Redox activities (and their associated proteins) include protein disulfide isomerization (DsbC), cytochrome <em>c</em> maturation (CcmG) and defense against oxidative stress (DsbG) (<span id='ref34544592.26389738' class='cite'>Williamson <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).</p>
<p>CcdA is a minimal homolog of DsbD&beta; that has six TMSs instead of eight as in DsbD&beta; and can also reduce CcmG. Another DsbD homolog, ScsB, found in <em>Salmonella typhimurium</em>,
 also lacks the last two TMSs but maintains similar periplasmic 
domains and functionality, thus suggesting that the last two TMSs 
are dispensable. Separate DsbD<span class='mb'>&alpha;</span> homologs exist in many bacteria with CcdA only. It has been proposed that CcdA has evolved into DsbD through
 fusion of the two periplasmic proteins to the TM domain, thus allowing 
greater electron-transfer efficiency (<span id='ref34544592.26389738' class='cite'>Williamson <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).</p>
<p>The
 transporter-like architecture of DsbD&beta; has been suggested from 
functional mutagenesis and biochemical studies probing solvent 
accessibility. The two functional cysteines of DsbD&beta; are on predicted TMSs 1 and 4. The sequence surrounding the cysteines is highly conserved, particularly the proline residues flanking each cysteine. Each proline in the PCX<sub>(2-3)</sub>P active sites is important for cysteine accessibility and redox activity. DsbD&beta; has an inverted pseudosymmetry, thus allowing access to the functional cysteines from both sides of the membrane,
 which allows the two cysteines to cycle through oxidized and 
reduced states and thus pass reducing power into the periplasm (<span id='ref34544592.26389738' class='cite'>Williamson <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).</p>
