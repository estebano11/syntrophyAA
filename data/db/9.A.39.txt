<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span><strong> 9.A.39&nbsp;  The Gram-positive Bacterial Hemoglobin Receptor (Isd) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span>The pathogenesis of human infections caused by the gram-positive microbe <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em> is reliant on the acquisition of iron from host hemoproteins. The iron-regulated surface determinant system (Isd) encodes a heme transport apparatus containing four cell wall-anchored proteins (IsdA, IsdB, IsdC and IsdH) that are exposed on the staphylococcal surface and hence have the potential to interact with human hemoproteins. Torres et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4211'>2006</a>) reported that <em>S. aureus</em> can utilize the host hemoproteins, hemoglobin and myoglobin, but not hemopexin, as iron sources for bacterial growth. They demonstrated that staphylococci capture hemoglobin on the bacterial surface via IsdB, and that inactivation of <em>isdB</em>, but not <em>isdA</em> or <em>isdH</em>, significantly decreases hemoglobin binding to the staphylococcal cell wall and impairs the ability of <em>S. aureus</em> to utilize hemoglobin as an iron source. IsdB removes heme iron from hemoglobin and transports it into the cell (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4211'>Torres <em>et al</em>., 2006</a>).  Liu et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref4894'>2008</a>) showed that hemin uptake involves transfer from IsdA to IsdC on the cell surface. </span></p>
<p><span>IsdB contains two N- and C-terminal peaks of striking hydrophobicity and two central Near Iron Transporter (</span>NEAT)&nbsp;domains (residues 150-260 and 370-450). This domain is involved in iron transport, sometimes complexed with a siderophore. It has an N-terminal YSIRK type signal peptide which contains the conserved motif: [YF]SIRK X<sub>3</sub> GX<sub>2</sub>S [VIA] and a C-terminal Gram-positive anchor domain with the LPXTG sortase processing site.<em> </em>IsdA,B,C,D and H all have an N-terminal peak of hydrophobicity and C-terminal peak of hydrophobicity, and they all may contain NEAT domains.&nbsp;</p>
<p>NEAT domains in IsdH/HarA extract heme from methemoglobin (<a class='reflink' href='#ref6641'>Pilpa <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>). IsdC is the central conduit through which heme is passed across the cell wall and binds this molecule using a Near Iron Transporter (NEAT) domain. NMR spectroscopy was used to determine the structure of IsdC in complex with a heme analog, zinc-substituted protoporphyrin IX (ZnPPIX) (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7394'>Villareal <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>). IsdC partially buries protoporphyrin within a large hydrophobic pocket that is located at the end of its &beta;-barrel structure. The central metal ion of the analog adopts a pentacoordinate geometry in which a highly conserved tyrosine residue serves as a proximal ligand. IsdC weakly binds heme (K(D) = 0.34 %u03BCM, and ZnPPIX rapidly dissociates from the protein. NMR studies of the apo-form of IsdC reveal that a 3(10) helix within the binding pocket undergoes a flexible to rigid transition as heme is captured. This structural plasticity may increase the efficiency of heme transfer across the cell wall by facilitating protein-protein interactions between apoIsdC and upstream hemoproteins (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7394'>Villareal <em>et al.</em>, 2008</a>).</p>
<p><span>All of the close homologues of IsdB occur in low G C Gram-positive bacteria. These include the haptoglobin-binding surface anchored proteins of S. aureus (BAF67896; 895 aas, 39% identity). However more distant homologues with overlapping domains are found in eukaryotes. Grigg et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7415'>2010</a>)&nbsp;have reviewed the Isd system of S. aureus. <br /></span></p>
<p><em>B. anthracis</em>, utilizes secreted 
hemophores to scavenge heme from host hemoglobin, thereby facilitating 
iron acquisition from extracellular heme pools and delivery to 
iron-regulated surface determinant (Isd) proteins covalently attached to
 the cell wall. However, several Gram-positive pathogens, including <em>B. 
anthracis</em>, contain genes that encode near iron transporter (NEAT) 
proteins that are genomically distant from the genetically linked Isd 
locus. NEAT domains are protein modules that partake in several 
functions related to heme transport, including binding heme and 
hemoglobin. This finding raises interesting questions concerning the 
relative role of these NEAT proteins, relative to hemophores and the Isd
 system, in iron uptake. Tarlovsky et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7414'>2010</a>) presented evidence that a <em>B. anthracis</em> S-layer homology (SLH) protein harboring a NEAT domain binds and 
directionally transfers heme to the Isd system via the cell wall protein
 IsdC. This finding suggests that the Isd system can receive heme from 
multiple inputs and may reflect an adaptation of <em>B. anthracis</em> to 
changing iron reservoirs during an infection.</p>
<p><em>Bacillus anthracis</em> secretes two NEAT (near iron transporter) proteins, IsdX1 and IsdX2, which scavenge heme from host hemoglobin and promote growth under low iron conditions. Fabian et al. (<span id='ref7618.19759022' class='cite'>2009</span>) presented evidence that the heme-bound form of IsdX1 rapidly and directionally transfers heme to IsdC, a NEAT protein covalently attached to the cell wall. The transfer of heme is mediated by a physical association between the donor and recipient. Unlike <em>Staphylococcus aureus</em>, whose NEAT proteins acquire heme from hemoglobin directly at the bacterial surface, <em>B. anthracis</em> secretes IsdX1 to capture heme in the extracellular milieu and relies on NEAT-NEAT interactions to deliver the bound heme to the envelope via IsdC.</p>
<p>The pathway for iron utilization in <em>S. aureus</em> appears to be:</p>
<p>substrate iron containing protein --&gt; Receptors (IsdH, A, B) --&gt; Cell wall conduit (IsdC) --&gt; Membrane ABC transporter (IsdDEF) --&gt; Cytoplasmic heme deregulation and iron release (IsdGI)</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Dryla A., B.
