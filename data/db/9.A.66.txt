<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.A.66 The Mistic (Mistic) Family&nbsp; </strong></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Mistic is an unusual 110 residue 
hydrophilic integral membrane protein that folds autonomously into the 
membrane, bypassing the cellular translocon machinery.  Mistic can be 
used for high-level production of other membrane proteins in their 
native conformations, including many eukaryotic proteins that have 
previously been intractable to bacterial expression.  The 3-D structure 
of Mistic has been solved revealing a 4-helix bundle with an unexpected 
polar lipid-facing surface (<span id='ref3103.15731457' class='cite'>Roosild <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>).
 The functional core of Mistic consists of 84 moderately conserved 
residues that are sufficient for membrane targeting and integration (<span id='ref4634.16704729' class='cite'>Roosild <em>et al.</em> 2006</span>). Cytoplasmic homologues that polymerize and form fibrils are present in <em>Bacillus leicheniformis </em>(<span id='ref6589.19475664' class='cite'>Dvir <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>). <br /></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> Isolated Mistic associate tightly with the bacterial membrane when expressed recombinantly in <em>E. coli</em>,
 and the monomeric detergent-solubilized protein binds tightly to 
micelles. Mistic targets proteins to the 
membrane, and when fused at its C-terminus to other proteins, 
it targets them to the membrane, facilitating
 insertion. The gene encoding it, <em>mstX</em>, 
overlaps by 4 nucleotides the <em>yugO</em> gene.&nbsp; </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'>Mistic may facilitate insertion 
of YugO (TC# 1.A.1.13.4). </span>MstX and <span class='highlight'>YugO</span> are regulators of biofilm formation in <em>B. subtilis </em>(<span id='ref34545853.23737939' class='cite'>Lundberg <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). Expression of <em>mstX</em> and the downstream putative K<sup>+</sup> efflux channel gene, <em><span class='highlight'>yugO</span></em>,
 is necessary for biofilm development in <em>B. subtilis</em>, and 
overexpression of <em>mstX</em> induces biofilm assembly. Transcription of the 
<em>mstX-<span class='highlight'>yugO</span></em> operon is under negative regulation by SinR, a transcription factor 
that governs the switch between planktonic and sessile states. 
Furthermore, MstX regulates the activity of Spo0A through a positive 
autoregulatory loop involving KinC, a histidine kinase that is activated
 by potassium leakage. The addition of potassium abrogated MstX-mediated
 biofilm formation (<span id='ref34545853.23737939' class='cite'>Lundberg <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>The interaction of Mistic with the bacterial membrane 
promotes the overexpression of other membrane proteins. The third 
helical fragment of Mistic interacts only with micelles but does 
not partition into lipid bilayers, but the other three 
helices interact with membranes in vivo and in vitro. Nevertheless, all 
of these short sequences can replace full-length Mistic as N-terminal 
fusions to achieve overexpression of a human G-protein-coupled receptor 
in <em>E. coli</em> (<span id='ref34545854.25297828' class='cite'>Marino <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>). A bioinformatic analysis of the Mistic family 
expanded the number of homologs from 4 to 20, including proteins outside
 the genus <em><span class='highlight'>Bacillus</span></em>. A highly conserved Shine-Dalgarno sequence in the operon mstX-<span class='highlight'>yugO</span> is important for downstream translation of the potassium ion channel <span class='highlight'>YugO</span> (<span id='ref34545854.25297828' class='cite'>Marino <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>). &nbsp;</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Dvir, H., M.E.
