<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p align='center'><span style='font-family: Times;'><strong>9.A.8  The Ferrous Iron Uptake (FeoB) Family </strong></span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> The FeoB protein of <em>E. coli</em> is an integral membrane protein of 773 amino acyl residues which is predicted to span the membrane 8-13 times as &alpha;-helices (<a class='reflink' href='#ref1810'>Kammler <em>et al</em>., 1993</a>).  Homologous proteins are encoded within the genomes of many bacteria and archaea.  The <em>E. coli</em> protein possesses an N-terminal 300 amino acyl residue hydrophilic domain that bears at its N-terminus a regulatory ATP/GTP binding motif as well as an S domain.  The N-terminal hydrophilic domain is homologous to prokaryotic and eukaryotic GTP binding proteins including the <em>E. coli</em> Era protein (P06616).&nbsp; GTP binding is required for efficient Fe<sup>2+ </sup> uptake, but GTP is hydrolyzed very slowly (<a class='reflink' href='#ref2404'>Marlovits <em>et al</em>., 2002</a>).&nbsp; The C-terminal transmembrane domain of FeoB catalyzes transport&nbsp; (<a class='reflink' href='#ref3092'>Hantke, 2003; </a><span id='ref9100.23024345' class='cite'>Hung <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>).&nbsp; Transport is probably regulated by or energized by the N-terminal intramolecular G-protein-like domain. Based on x-ray crystallographic data, the G-doman transmits information to the transmembrane domain in a fashion possibly similar to energy transfer in ABC transporters (<a class='reflink' href='#ref7207'>K&ouml;ster <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>).&nbsp;&nbsp; </span>Biochemical analyses demonstrated that the GTPase activity of <span class='highlight'>FeoB</span> is activated by K<sup>+</sup>, which leads to a 20-fold acceleration in its 
hydrolysis rate (<span id='ref10263.20220129' class='cite'>Ash <em>et al.</em> 2010</span>). Analysis of the structure identified a conserved 
asparagine residue likely to be involved in K<sup>+</sup> coordination, and 
mutation of this residue abolished K<sup>+</sup>-dependent activation.&nbsp; <span id='ref10263.20220129' class='cite'>Ash <em>et al.</em> (2010</span>) suggested
 that this, together with a second asparagine residue that is 
critical for the structure of the Switch I loop, allows K<sup>+</sup>-dependent activation in G proteins. The accelerated
 hydrolysis rate opens up the possibility that <span class='highlight'>FeoB</span> might indeed function as an active transporter. Iron uptake in eukaryotes has been reviewed (<span id='ref34549158.30510932' class='cite'>Sherman <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>) as has that in prokaryotes (<span id='ref34549174.29953152' class='cite'>Sestok <em>et al.</em> 2018</span>).&nbsp; Thus the mechanism of energy coupling to FeoB transport is an open question.</p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> </span></p>
<p><span style='font-family: Times;'> A FeoB homologue is present in <em>Helicobacter pylori.</em> This system takes up Fe<sup>2+ </sup> with high affinity (0.5 &mu;M) in a process that is inhibited by FCCP, DCCD and vanadate, indicating that uptake is energized by ATP or GTP hydrolysis (<a class='reflink' href='#ref1811'>Velayudhan <em>et al</em>., 2000</a>).  Fe<sup>3+ </sup> is first converted to Fe<sup>2+ </sup> by an extracytoplasmic Fe<sup>3+ </sup> reductase, and the resultant Fe<sup>2+ </sup> is taken up by FeoB.  FeoB appears to provide the major pathway for Fe<sup>2+ </sup> uptake in <em>H. pylori </em>and <em>C. perfringens,</em> and it is essential for colonization of the murine gastric mucosa in<em> H. pylori</em>. A similar FeoB homologue in the spirochete <em>Leptospira biflexa</em> has been implicated in Fe<sup>2+ </sup> uptake (<a class='reflink' href='#ref3094'>Louvel <em>et al</em>., 2005</a>). </span>A general protocol for the expression and purification of the intact transmembrane&nbsp; FeoBs from several bacteria has been described (<span id='ref34554804.35636558' class='cite'>Sestok <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>). Additionally, seasonal patterns in microbial carbon and iron transporter expression in the Southern Ocean has been examined (<span id='ref34557042.37596690' class='cite'>Debeljak <em>et al.</em> 2023</span>).</p>
<p>Prokaryotic FeoB proteins are
involved in G protein coupled Fe<sup>2+ </sup> transport. They are unique in that
the G protein is directly tethered to the membrane domain. <a class='reflink' href='#ref6745'>Guilfoyle <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a> reported the structure of the soluble domain of FeoB, including the G
protein domain, and its assembly into a trimer. Comparisons
between nucleotide free and liganded structures reveal the closed and
open state of a central cytoplasmic pore, respectively. In addition,
these data provide the first observation of a conformational switch in
the nucleotide-binding G5 motif, defining the structural basis for GDP
release. From these results, structural parallels are drawn to
eukaryotic G protein coupled membrane processes (<a class='reflink' href='#ref6745'>Guilfoyle <em>et al.</em>, 2009</a>).&nbsp; Diverse gut bacteria communities protect against harmful pathogens by nutrient blocking.</p>
<p>The Feo transport system consists of three proteins: FeoA, FeoB, and FeoC. The N-terminal domain (N-FeoB) has been shown to form a trimeric pore that may function as a Fe<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;gate. FeoC is a small winged-helix protein possessing four conserved cysteine residues with a consensus sequence that may provide binding sites for an [Fe-S]-cluster. Therefore, FeoC may be an [Fe-S]-cluster-dependent regulator that directly controls transcription of the <em>feo</em> operon. Hung et al. (<span id='ref9100.23024345' class='cite'>2012</span>) showed that <em>Klebsiella pneumoniae</em> FeoC (KpFeoC) forms a tight complex with the intracellular N-terminal domain of FeoB (KpNFeoB). The crystal structure of the complex revealed that KpFeoC binds to KpNFeoB between the switch II region of the G-protein domain and the effector S domain, and that the long KpFeoC W1 loop lies above the KpNFeoB nucleotide-binding site. These interactions suggest that KpFeoC modulates guanine nucleotide-mediated signal transduction. Binding of KpFeoC disrupts pore formation by interfering with KpNFeoB trimerization. Thus, KpFeoC may play a crucial role in regulating Fe<sup>2+</sup>&nbsp;transport as well as &nbsp;gene regulation. FeoA is a 75aa protein homologous to the N-terminus of FeoB2 of <em>Porphyromonas gingivalis</em>&nbsp;(TC#9.A.8.1.6) and some similarity &nbsp;to an internal hydrophilic segment of the RND heavy metal porter, CzcA of <em>Myxococcus xanthus</em>&nbsp;(TC#2.A.6.1.7).</p>
<p>In <em>Vibrio cholerae</em> the <em>feo</em> operon consists of three genes, <em>feoABC</em>. <em><span class='highlight'>feoB</span></em> encodes an 83 kDa protein with an amino terminal GTPase domain and a 
carboxy terminal domain predicted to be embedded in the inner membrane.&nbsp; In <em>V. cholerae</em>, FeoA and FeoC, as well as the more highly conserved <span class='highlight'>FeoB</span>, are all required for iron acquisition (<span id='ref10261.23955009' class='cite'>Weaver <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). FeoC interacts with the cytoplasmic domain of <span class='highlight'>FeoB</span>, and two 
conserved amino acids in FeoC were found to be necessary for the 
interaction with <span class='highlight'>FeoB</span>.&nbsp;</p>
<p>FeoB normally consists of a cytoplasmic soluble domain termed NFeoB and a C-terminal polytopic transmembrane domain.
NFeoB has two structural subdomains: a canonical GTPase domain and a
five-helix helical domain. The GTPase domain hydrolyses GTP to GDP through a well characterized
mechanism, a process which is required for Fe<sup>2+</sup> transport. The structure of the cytoplasmic domain of
FeoB from <em>Gallionella capsiferriformans</em> has been determined (<span id='ref10262.23545645' class='cite'>Deshpande <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). The <em>G.
capsiferriformans</em> NFeoB structure does not contain a helical domain, and the crystal structures of both the apo and GDP-bound protein reveals a domain-swapped dimer. I</p>
<p>Insertional inactivation of <em>feoB</em> in <em>C. perfringens </em>yielded altered growth properties and a
markedly reduced total iron and manganese content compared to the wild type. Thus, under
anaerobic conditions, FeoB is the major protein required for iron uptake into the cell and it may play an important role in the pathogenesis of <em>C. perfringens</em> infections (<span id='ref34546378.27178230' class='cite'>Awad <em>et al.</em> 2016</span>).&nbsp;</p>
<p>Bacterial FeoBs possess 
either GTPase or an NTPase with substrate promiscuity. This 
difference in nucleotide preference alters cellular requirements for 
monovalent and divalent cations. While the hydrolytic activity of the 
GTP-dependent FeoBs was stimulated by potassium, the action of the 
NTP-dependent FeoBs was not. Mutation of Asn11, having a role in 
potassium-dependent GTP hydrolysis, changed nucleotide specificity of 
the NTP-dependent FeoB, resulting in loss of ATPase activity (<span id='ref34551754.32220566' class='cite'>Shin <em>et al.</em> 2020</span>).</p>
<p>Feo transporters are the most widespread systems for ferrous iron uptake in bacteria and are critical for virulence in some species. The canonical architecture of Feo consists of a large transmembrane nucleoside triphosphatase (NTPase) protein, FeoB, and two small accessory cytoplasmic proteins, FeoA and FeoC.&nbsp;<span id='ref34554396.35012344' class='cite'>G&oacute;mez-Garz&oacute;n <em>et al.</em> 2022</span> conducted comparative analyses of Feo protein sequences. They found that FeoC, while absent in most lineages, is largely present in the &gamma;-proteobacteria although its sequence is poorly conserved. FeoC may couple FeoB NTPase activity with pore opening and was an ancestral element that has been dispensed with through mutations in FeoA and FeoB. FeoC-independent mutants of the <em>Vibrio cholerae</em> Feo system were isolated, and the <em>Shewanella oneidensis</em> FeoAB does not require FeoC; thus, the <em>Vibrio</em> FeoC sequences may resemble transitional forms on an evolutionary pathway toward FeoC-independent transporters. FeoC may have different functions in different species that retain this protein, and the loss of FeoC may be promoted by mutations in FeoA or by the fusion of FeoA and FeoB (<span id='ref34554396.35012344' class='cite'>G&oacute;mez-Garz&oacute;n <em>et al.</em> 2022</span>).</p>
