<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.B.10 The Putative Tripartite Zn<sup>2 </sup>Transporter (TZT) Family</strong></p>
<p><a class='reflink' href='#ref6362'>Gaballa <em>et al.</em>, 2002</a> have suggested that the <em>yciABC</em> genes, in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>, in a single operon, encode a zinc transporter. These genes are regulated by the zinc uptake repressor, Zur. Analysis of a yciA-lacZ fusion indicated that this operon is induced
under zinc starvation conditions and derepressed in the <em>zur</em> mutant.
Phenotypic analyses suggest that the YciA, YciB, and YciC proteins may
function as part of the same Zn(II) transport pathway. Mutation of <em>yciA</em> or <em>yciC</em>, singly or in combination, had little effect on growth of the
wild-type strain but significantly impaired the growth of the <em>ycdH</em> mutant under conditions of zinc limitation. Since the YciA, YciB, and
YciC proteins are not obviously related to any known transporter
family, they may define a new class of metal ion uptake systems. Mutant
strains lacking all three identified zinc uptake systems (YciABC, YcdHI-YceA, and ZosA) are dependent on micromolar levels of added zinc
for optimal growth.</p>
<p>The three proteins, YciA, B and C are annotaed as a GTP cyclohydrolase (FolE2), a transpeptidase (DUF5011), and a metal chaparone protein, respectively.&nbsp; It is quite likely that these proteins do not function in transport in spite of the suggestion of Gaballa et al.
