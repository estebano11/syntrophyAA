<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.B.105&nbsp;The Peptidase/Phosphatase/Lead Resistance Fusion Protein (PPLR) Family</strong></p>
<p>Proteome and transcriptome analysis, combined with mutagenesis, were used to better understand the response of <em>Ralstonia (Cupriavidus) metallidurans</em> CH34 to Pb<sup>2+ </sup>(<span id='ref7743.18953667' class='cite'>Taghavi <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>).&nbsp;Structural Pb<sup>2+</sup>-resistance genes of the pMOL30-encoded <em>pbrUTRABCD</em> operon form the major line of defense to Pb<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>(<span id='ref7743.18953667' class='cite'>Taghavi <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The expression of the <em>pbrR</em>(2) <em>cadA pbrC</em>(2) operon of the CMGI-1 region and the chromosomal <em>zntA gene</em> were clearly induced in the presence of Pb<sup>2+</sup>. After inactivation of the <em>pbrA</em>, <em>pbrB</em> or <em>pbrD</em> gene, expression of the <em>pbrR</em>(2) <em>cadA pbrC</em>(2) operon went up considerably. This points towards synergistic interactions between <em>pbrUTRABCD</em> and <em>pbrR</em>(2) <em>cadA pbrC</em>(2) to maintain a low intracellular Pb<sup>2+</sup>concentration, where <em>pbrR</em>(2) <em>cadA pbrC</em>(2) gene functions can complement and compensate for mutations in the <em>pbrA</em> and <em>pbrD</em> genes. This role of <em>zntA</em> and <em>cadA</em> to complement the loss of <em>pbrA</em> was further confirmed by mutation analysis (<span id='ref7743.18953667' class='cite'>Taghavi <em>et al.</em>, 2009</span>). The <em>pbrB</em>::Tn(Km2) mutation resulted in the most significant decrease of Pb<sup>2+</sup>resistance, indicating that Pb<sup>2+&nbsp;</sup>sequestration, avoiding re-entry of this toxic metal ion, forms a critical step in <em>pbr</em>-encoded Pb<sup>2+</sup>resistance.&nbsp;</p>
<p class='p1' style='text-align: left;'>Residues 1-190 ( TMSs 1-6) in PbrB/C code for a PAP2-type protein in a family of phosphatases and haloperoxidases. These homologues are found only in bacteria and may be integral membrane phospholipid phosphatases. The C-terminal residues (residues 200-340; TMSs 7-10) may code for a signal peptidase II (pfam01252). In fact,<em> </em>several member of this family are annotated as lipoprotein signal 
peptidases, suggesting that these integral membrane proteins may be 
proteases. Other members (see subfamilies 4 - 6; subfamilies 5 and 6 were formerly subfamilies 9.B.196.1 and 2, respectively) appear to belong to the Phosphodiester PA Phosphatase (PAP2) Superfamily (<span id='ref34550296.15801912' class='cite'>Sigal <em>et al.</em> 2005</span>).</p>
<p class='p1' style='text-align: left;'>The PAP2 superfamily consists of numerous integral membrane proteins, 
annotated in Pfam and NCBI as phosphodiesterases. 
Phosphatidylcholine (PC) hydrolysis generates two second 
messengers: phosphatidic acid (PA) and diacylglycerol (DAG). 
Phospholipase D (PLD) and phosphatidate phosphohydrolase (PAPase) are 
involved in their generation and therefore are key enzymes in signal 
transduction. Specific isoforms of these enzymes are activated by 
receptor occupancy in the brain. Phosphatidylinositol 
4,5-bisphosphate-dependent PLD (PIP2-PLD) and 
N-ethylmaleimide-insensitive PAPase (<span class='highlight'>PAP2</span>) have been suggested to act in series to generate the biologically active lipids PA and DAG (<span id='ref34544996.11830357' class='cite'>Salvador <em>et al.</em> 2002</span>).</p>
<p class='p1' style='text-align: left;'>PbrBC may be a phosphatase. While PbrA non-specifically exported Pb<sup>2+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup> and Cd<sup>2+</sup>, a 
specific increase in lead resistance is observed when PbrA and <span class='highlight'>PbrB</span> are coexpressed. Possibly Pb<sup>2+</sup> is exported from the cytoplasm by 
PbrA and then sequestered as a phosphate salt with the inorganic 
phosphate produced by <span class='highlight'>PbrB</span>.
 Similar operons containing genes for heavy metal translocating ATPases 
and phosphatases can be found in many different bacterial species, 
suggesting that lead detoxification through active efflux and 
sequestration is a common lead-resistance mechanism (<span id='ref34545593.19737357' class='cite'>Hynninen <em>et al.</em> 2009</span>).</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><HR>This family belongs to the <A HREF="superfamily.php?id=109">Phospholipid Transporter/Phosphatase (PL-TP) Superfamily</A>.<BR><HR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Ghachi, M.E., N.
