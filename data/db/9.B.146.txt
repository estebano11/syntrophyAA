<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.B.146 The Putative Undecaprenyl-phosphate N-Acetylglucosaminyl Transferase (MurG) Family</strong></p>
<p>The MurG family cosists of thousands of glycosyltransferases, many of which function in bacterial cell wall (peptidoglycan) synthesis.&nbsp; This family is also designated the Glycosyl Transferase (GT) 4, GT4, family, the WecA-like family and the Rfe family.&nbsp; Homologues are found in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes and vary in size from about 300 aas to over 500 aas.&nbsp; They usually exhibit 10 - 15 putative TMSs. The crystal structure of one member of this family, MraY of <em>Aquifex aeolicus</em>, the cell wall biosynthesis enzyme, phospho-MurNAc-pentapeptide translocase, has been solved at 3.3&Aring; resolution (<a class='reflink' href='#ref10303'>Chung <em>et al.</em> 2013</a>).&nbsp; The structure of this 10 TMS protein reveals the Mg<sup>2+</sup> binding site and the probable basis for catalysis.</p>
<p>Translocation of the peptidoglycan precursor Lipid II across the 
cytoplasmic membrane is a key step in bacterial cell wall synthesis. Using NBD-labelled Lipid II, van Dam et al. (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9697'>2007</a>) showed that Lipid II <span class='highlight'>transport</span> does not occur spontaneously and is not induced by the presence of 
single spanning helical transmembrane peptides that facilitate 
transbilayer movement of membrane phospholipids. <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> catalyses synthesis of Lipid II from Lipid I in lipid vesicles, but it did
 not result in membrane translocation of Lipid II. It was suggested that a specialized protein machinery is needed for 
transmembrane movement of Lipid II. It could be 
demonstrated that Lipid II translocation occurred in isolated <em>Escherichia coli</em> inner 
membrane vesicles and this <span class='highlight'>transport</span> could be uncoupled from the synthesis of Lipid II at low temperatures. The <span class='highlight'>transport</span> process appeared to be independent of an energy source (ATP or proton
 motive force).&nbsp;
 Lipid II transport may be coupled to transglycosylation activity on the periplasmic 
side of the inner membrane (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9697'>van Dam <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>).</p>
<p>In <em>Escherichia coli,</em> many enzymes including <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> are directly involved in the synthesis and assembly of peptidoglycan. <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> is an essential glycosyltransferase catalysing the last intracellular 
step of peptidoglycan synthesis. <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> exhibits a random distribution in the cell envelope with a relatively 
high intensity at the division site. This mid-cell localization is 
dependent on the presence of a mature divisome (<a class='reflink' href='#ref9698'>Mohammadi <em>et al.</em> 2007</a>). Its localization in the 
lateral cell wall appears to require the presence of MreCD. This could indicate a potential interaction between <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> and other proteins. <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> also associates with MreB and MraY in the same protein complex. The 
loss of rod shape of a <em>mreBCD</em> deletion strain could be ascribed to the loss of
 <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> membrane 
localization. Consequently, this could prevent the localized supply of 
the lipid II precursor to the peptidoglycan synthesizing machinery 
involved in cell elongation. Possibly the involvement of <span class='highlight'>MurG</span> in peptidoglycan synthesis concerns two complexes, one implicated in cell elongation and the other in division.</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Chung, B.C., J.
