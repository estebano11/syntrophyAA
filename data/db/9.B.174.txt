<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.B.174 The Two Tunnel Gated C-terminal Processing Protease (CTP) Family</strong></p>
<p>Spore formation in <em>Bacillus subtilis </em>relies on a regulated intramembrane proteolysis (RIP) pathway&nbsp;that synchronizes mother-cell and forespore development. This is the SpoIV transmembrane signaling pathway (<span id='ref10464.24318355' class='cite'>Ding <em>et al.</em>, 2013</span>).  Crystal structures of the activating protease, CtpB, reflecting distinct&nbsp;functional states, showed that CtpB constitutes a ring-like protein scaffold penetrated by two narrow tunnels. Access to the proteolytic sites sequestered within these tunnels is controlled by PDZ domains that rearrange upon substrate binding (<span id='ref34543623.24243021' class='cite'>Mastny <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>).</p>
<p>CtpB resembles a minimal version of a self-compartmentalizing protease regulated by a&nbsp;unique allosteric mechanism. Moreover, biochemical analysis of the PDZ-gated channel combined with&nbsp;sporulation assays reveal that activation of the SpoIV RIP pathway is induced by the concerted&nbsp;activity of CtpB and a second signaling protease, SpoIVB. This proteolytic mechanism is of broad&nbsp;relevance for cell-cell communication, illustrating how distinct signaling pathways can be&nbsp;integrated into a single RIP module (<span id='ref10464.24318355' class='cite'>Ding <em>et al.</em>, 2013</span>).</p>
