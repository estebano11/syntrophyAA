<TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="description"></a>&nbsp;<p><p style='text-align: center;'><strong>9.B.217.&nbsp; The Transmembrane PrsW Protease (PrsW) Family&nbsp;</strong>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The activation of the RNA polymerase sigma 
factor sigmaW in <em>Bacillus subtilis</em> is regulated by intramembrane 
proteolysis by a PrsW protease (<span id='ref34547090.16816000' class='cite'>Ellermeier and Losick 2006</span>). SigmaW is activated by proteolytic destruction of the 
membrane-bound anti-sigmaW factor RsiW in response to antimicrobial 
peptides and other agents that damage the cell envelope. RsiW is 
destroyed by successive proteolytic events known as Site-1 and Site-2 
cleavage. Site-2 cleavage is mediated by a member of the SpoIVFB-S2P 
family of intramembrane-acting metalloproteases, but the <span class='highlight'>protease</span> responsible for Site-1 cleavage is by <span class='highlight'>PrsW</span> (annotated YpdC) that is both necessary and sufficient for Site-1 
cleavage of RsiW.&nbsp;<span id='ref34547090.16816000' class='cite'>Ellermeier and Losick 2006</span> identified residues important for proteolysis and a cluster of acidic 
residues involved in sensing antimicrobial peptides and cell envelope 
stress. Another membrane protease, RasP, when defective, causes defects in competence development, protein secretion and membrane protein production (<span id='ref34547093.23155385' class='cite'>Zweers <em>et al.</em> 2012</span>). &sigma;<sup>V</sup> activation in <em>B. subtilis</em> is controlled by regulated intramembrane proteolysis and requires the site 2 <span class='highlight'>protease</span> RasP (<span id='ref34547094.23687273' class='cite'>Hastie <em>et al.</em> 2013</span>). RasP, an intramembrane metalloprotease of <em>Bacillus subtilis,</em> cleaves both the stress response anti-sigma factors
 RsiW and RsiV as well as the cell division protein FtsL, and remnant signal 
peptides, within their transmembrane segments (<span id='ref34547095.28674070' class='cite'>Parrell <em>et al.</em> 2017</span>). <span class='highlight'>PrsW</span> likely 
regulates the activation of the ECF &sigma; factor CsfT in <em>Clostridium difficile</em> and 
controls the resistance of <em>C. difficile</em> to antimicrobial peptides (<span id='ref34547092.21628514' class='cite'>Ho and Ellermeier 2011</span>). In Staphylococcus aureus, PrsS, a homologue of PrsW, and &sigma;<sup>S</sup> together mediate 
virulence and resistance to DNA damage and cell wall-targeting 
antibiotics via a common pathway (<span id='ref34547096.25741016' class='cite'>Krute <em>et al.</em> 2015</span>).</p>
<p><span class='highlight'>PrsW</span> proteases, the DUF2324 family and the
 &gamma;-secretase subunit APH-1 proteins share four predicted core 
transmembrane segments and possess similar yet distinct sets of sequence
 motifs (<span id='ref34547091.21570408' class='cite'>Pei <em>et al.</em> 2011</span>). Remote similarity between APH-1 and membrane proteases sheds 
light on APH-1's evolutionary origin and raises the possibility that 
APH-1 may possess proteolytic activity in the current or ancestral form 
of &gamma;-secretase.&nbsp;</p>
<p>The second sub-family in this family consists of DUF368 proteins and is only distantly related to the first sub-family. Precursors of peptidoglycan (PG) and other cell surface glycopolymers are synthesized in the cytoplasm and then delivered across the cell membrane bound to the recyclable lipid carrier undecaprenyl phosphate (C55-P). The transporter protein(s) that return C55-P to the cytoplasmic face of the cell membrane have been elusive.&nbsp;<span id='ref34555677.36450355' class='cite'>Sit <em>et al.</em> 2022</span> identified the DUF368-containing and DedA transmembrane protein families as candidate C55-P translocases. Gram-negative and -positive bacteria lacking their cognate DUF368-containing protein exhibited alkaline-dependent cell wall and viability defects, along with increased cell surface C55-P levels. pH-dependent synthetic genetic interactions between DUF368-containing proteins and DedA family members suggested that the putative C55-P transporter usage is dynamic and modulated by environmental inputs. C55-P transporter activity was required by the cholera pathogen for growth and cell shape maintenance in the intestine. <span id='ref34555677.36450355' class='cite'>Sit <em>et al.</em> 2022</span> proposed that conditional transporter reliance provides resilience in lipid carrier recycling, bolstering microbial fitness within and outside of the host.</p><BR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><HR>This family belongs to the <A HREF="superfamily.php?id=68">CAAX Superfamily</A>.<BR><HR></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5><a name="references"></a>&nbsp;<p><B>References:</B><P></TD></TR><TR><TD COLSPAN=5>Ellermeier, C.D. and R.
